## 1. What the new stories add up to

**US side:**  
The Pentagon has approved 5,000 Marines (31st MEU, USS Tripoli) and redirected special-operations aircraft (MC-130J from RAF Mildenhall) toward the Middle East. Rubio said nuclear material must be physically secured — “people are going to have to go and get it.” Ground invasion is not confirmed but is openly discussed; the White House has not ruled out a draft. AFR (Australia) called the Marine deployment “reeks of desperation” — two weeks into the war, with another two weeks before the MEU arrives, suggests the air campaign alone cannot deliver the stated goals.

**Allied pressure:**  
A YouGov poll (Bondi Partners) found 44% of Americans (and ~66% of Republicans) believe allies who publicly back the Iran operation should also send their own troops. Australia backed the war on Day 1 but has limited its contribution to a Wedgetail, missiles, and 85 personnel. That puts Canberra in a squeeze: domestically unpopular to send more, but under growing US expectation to contribute more if the war escalates (e.g. ground troops).

**Supply-chain widening:**  
China has halted refinery exports (no new fuel cargoes after 11 March), cutting a large share of Australia’s jet fuel supply and tightening Asian refined-product markets. This is the “second wave” of the oil shock: not only Hormuz disruption but Asian refining and export cuts. Chomsky’s oil → fertiliser → food chain is now joined by oil → refining → jet fuel (and other products) in non‑Gulf regions.

**Australia:**  
Legal experts (e.g. ANU’s Rothwell) say assisting a Gulf state’s self-defence makes Australia a party to an international armed conflict regardless of the “defensive only” label. Australian unions (ACTU and most affiliates) have not opposed the war; only MUA and AMWU have issued statements, and no industrial action has been called. At the same time, Australia is deepening non‑US regional architecture (Indonesia–Australia–Japan and Indonesia–Australia–PNG trilaterals, Morotai facility) — explicit hedging against US unreliability.

## 3. Likely path ahead (updated)

**Short term (next 1–2 weeks)**  
- **Ground troops:** The path to boots on the ground is open (MEU en route, special ops in theatre, Rubio’s “go and get it”). The main constraint is domestic (74% against ground troops; “most unpopular foreign war”). Signals to watch: 82nd Airborne orders, seizure of Kharg Island, any embed of US forces with Kurdish units inside Iran, or a spike in US casualties suggesting ground contact.  
- **Strait of Hormuz:** If it stays closed, fuel and food crises deepen; China/Asia refinery cuts already show the shock spreading. If it reopens, some pressure eases but structural damage (refinery runs, supply chains) will lag.  
- **Allies:** If Trump formally asks for allied troops, Australia (and others) face a binary choice: refuse and strain the alliance, or agree and inflame domestic opposition. The Bondi/YouGov poll shows that “verbal support only” is not what a large share of the US public expects.

**Medium term (rest of March – April)**  
- **Two branches:**  
  - **No ground invasion:** Face-saving de-escalation remains possible (messy ceasefire, “mission accomplished” narrative, Strait gradually reopened). Predictions that assume no ground war (e.g. “messy exit”) stay on the table.  
  - **Ground invasion:** If boots go in (e.g. limited objective like securing nuclear material or Kharg), Jiang’s “trapped troops” and “point of no return” logic becomes the central scenario: escalation, rescue missions, domestic political collapse (Vietnam analogy), and pressure on allies to contribute — exactly what the poll and Australian legal/union stories are already setting up.  
- **Economic/supply:** Regardless of ground troops, the oil–refining–fertiliser–food and oil–refining–jet-fuel chains will keep feeding inflation and supply stress (RBA rate expectations, Asian product cuts, Australian fuel/urea numbers already in the guide).

**What hasn’t changed**  
- The war was predicted; the pattern (playbook, allies dragged in, oil shock, no exit plan) holds.  
- The new stories don’t contradict the analysts; they fill in how ground troops are being prepared, how allies are being pressured, and how the supply shock is spreading.  
- The single biggest variable remains: do US (or allied) boots enter Iran? If yes, the “trapped troops / point of no return” and “allies expected to send troops” predictions become the dominant dynamics. If no, the path is still toward a messy, face-saving exit, with lasting economic and political damage.

## 4. Bottom line

The new stories **strengthen** the predictive framework rather than undermine it:  
- **Jiang:** Ground-troop path is real and official (MEU, special ops, Rubio); “no plan for the day after” and proxy limits are confirmed.  
- **Chomsky:** Allies are in (Australia legally and materially); oil shock is spreading (China jet fuel, Asian refining).  
- **Paul / Kovalik:** No contradictions; backfire and playbook continue to play out.  

The **likely path ahead** is now more clearly a fork:  
- **Path A:** No ground invasion → messy exit, lasting economic damage, alliance strain.  
- **Path B:** Ground invasion (even “limited”) → Jiang’s trap scenario and domestic blowback, with strong pressure on allies to send troops (as the poll and Australian position already anticipate).  

The new stories don’t change the predictions; they show we are closer to the fork and that the ground-troops branch is being actively prepared.
