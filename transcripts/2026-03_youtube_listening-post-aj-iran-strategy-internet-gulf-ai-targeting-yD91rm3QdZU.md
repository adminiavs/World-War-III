**CNN in Iran:** Al Jazeera has permanent presence in Iran; "this past week CNN became the first Western news outlet to get a team into the Islamic Republic since the war began." "That presence matters" — "some of the images CNN has been beaming out challenge the narrative coming out of Washington, Tel Aviv, and on occasion CNN's own studios in the US." But: "Media was very quick to ask why are we in this war, what is the justification" (e.g. "third party defense reasoning") "but not questioning enough how this war is being conducted … not asking show us what damage has been done to military bases … too much accepting what the Pentagon says at face value." **Iran internet blackout:** "While letting foreign journalists in, Iranian authorities are still keeping foreign news content, including Western propaganda, out. That is just part of the rationale for the continued clampdown." Restrictions "put in place during the anti-government protests across Iran in January." Iranian officials "able to get the word out, messaging Americans and Israelis that Iran and its counterattacks are just getting started." Citizens "can still communicate with each other online but only about 1% of normal internet traffic there is currently reaching the outside world." Expert: "Makes it much more difficult to communicate. Iran does have a national internet which people can use internally. It's the access to the outside they've cut off. Reasoning: control the way social media, propaganda, satellite television can influence Iranians' reaction to the war. In recent years social media and satellite television from outside have been quite influential. Also the internet can be used for information gathering in war by Israel and the United States — so they're trying to blind the US and Israel from being able to use Iranian internet to conduct the war." "United States and Israel have weaponized Iran's internet infrastructure against it — cyber attacks on banks, financial institutions, vital and critical infrastructure." "Sympathetic pundits … have access and come on Western channels." "Internet access becomes quite uneven … our picture is going to be stiltered and fragmentary."

Saudi Arabia and others quite conscious of the anger their own public still have against the US and Israel for the genocide in Gaza. The attack on Doha back in June, the so-called 12-day war, was a wake-up call. Even for countries that are not huge fans of Iran, they don't want to live in a region where Israel dominates and attacks at will." "Whether true or not, I don't think the leaks of various actions taken by Gulf countries in this war are intended to harm those countries. They're intended primarily for domestic Israeli consumption — to make Israelis feel better about the prospects of success. **AI and targeting (Matt Mahmoodi — Amnesty Tech, Cambridge):** "Since the first attacks on Iran, the White House and the Pentagon have struggled to contain their enthusiasm over their chance to test out the latest military hardware and software. US has spent years investing in AI-enabled targeting systems, autonomous weapons, long-range so-called precision missiles — identifying and striking targets easier, faster, more dangerous. As in Gaza, AI-powered systems appear to be playing a central role … guiding decisions that have life and death consequences."

- **New form of warfare:** "We're watching a new form of warfare emerge … going back to the start of the genocide in Gaza" — "target acquisition systems like Lavender and Gospel that rely on large tranches of information (social media data, government databases) to make determinations about whether someone is a combatant." "In the context of what we might be witnessing here in Iran, the Middle East — through the use of a large language model we're seeing a lot of those same technologies packaged together in a chat-prompt-based interface that allows the military to go in and simply put in a human-speak prompt that asks for particular targets to be generated with particular characteristics." "Zealousness, overzealousness with the idea that you can simply have a quick in-out strike through the use of artificial intelligence systems. Opportunistic moment the US government seems to have taken … relationships with tech companies who were all too happy, days before the war, to provide their technology no questions asked until the very last moment."

- **Accuracy / Manab school:** "How lethal, how accurate, how autonomous?" "Incredibly difficult to say how accurate … comes down to what your understanding of accuracy is." Lavender: "Claim that by using disparate sources of data you can generate a list of targets that can be hit with precision — but you end up with a list of 40,000 names. Who vetted these names? How do we end up with these names? Proximity to particular facilities? Posts on social media? These probabilistic systems work through a relational approach — having been connected on social media to a particular individual who may have said something or have a particular affiliation renders you a threat." "In Iran now — thousands of air strikes on what seems like indiscriminate grounds … to what extent is there any accuracy at all? If your aim is to decimate the entire country you could argue you acted with great accuracy in performing the devastation. But did you achieve the goal of eliminating the particular individuals you were interested in targeting? This is where target acquisition gets it wrong. It is an AI washing of the attempt to establish a legitimate target — simply saying because the system said these targets are correct that makes it a legitimate target, which is a violation of international law because individuals in the chain of command have not actively been involved in vetting and understanding who they're targeting and why." (Manab: "That girl school in Minab, bombing that killed at least 165 schoolgirls and staff.")

- **Human component / oversight:** "No matter how advanced these systems are, the failings come at the end where the human component still is." "Anthropic … insisting they have a restriction in the contract with the US government, the Department of War, that they are against the usage of autonomous weapon systems without oversight. However at this rate oversight can be defined in any number of ways. The US government can say somebody had a look at the list of targets for 15 seconds, 2 minutes, and they deemed that sufficient without ever having interrogated how the particular large language model arrived at the list in the first place. So the oversight itself is incredibly permissive."

- **Anthropic / Claude:** "Anthropic's beef with Trump and Trump's beef with Anthropic … are they the outlier? The only ones fighting back?" "Interesting framing — the fighting back component. The company was very willing to enter this contract to begin with with the US government, the Pentagon. We've seen a number of other AI companies over the last few years enter contracts with the Pentagon and Department of War, with US immigration and customs enforcement who has been actively involved in military tactics in the streets of Minneapolis. Involvement of AI companies in tactics of warfare is not new. The fact that Anthropic was able to demonstrate resistance merely days before the strikes took place, the preemptive air strikes on Iran, allowed them to position themselves at a moral high ground that lets them on the one hand sell their product for being incredibly potent in the context of warfare — as we see now couple of weeks on it has been revealed by reports that Claude may have been used in the context of the air strikes in Iran, which again gives them potency as a brand — and on the other hand posture that they resisted the government and you should be using their product at every turn because they are the good guys. That dichotomy — on the one hand having developed a product that can be used at scale for warfare and on the other being the morally superior AI company — allows them to bring in the profit, the kind of reach that AI companies can only dream of."

- **Accountability:** "Use of these systems can make it difficult to determine whether some attacks were deliberate, the result of intelligence failures, or errors in AI itself. Is that creating a way for militaries to evade accountability — just say the machine got it wrong?" "Under international law you have to have a legitimate target under international humanitarian law … and you have to have a chain of command in which there is accountability. There has to be a clear understanding of who is exercising the command and who is following the command. With the insertion of AI we have a complete muddying up of who made the decision to kill to begin with. Was it the system? The designer who designed the tool? The person who sat and operated the interface or took the indicator it was giving? These are fundamentally questions that mean these systems are more likely to automate killing at scale and create a dehumanization because it doesn't allow for individuals that operate them to really get into the weeds of interrogating systems — they simply follow. We have been calling constantly at Amnesty International, at the Stop Killer Robots Coalition, for an internationally binding instrument on autonomous weapon systems precisely because of this — there is currently no way of ensuring any oversight, no accountability, and companies are some of the parties that are able to simply skirt all of that whilst deriving most of the profit." "Fog of war, which was already thick, is being made thicker by AI." "Absolutely."

**Close:** "This past week a renowned Palestinian historian, Walid Khalidi, passed away at the age of 100. Born Jerusalem 1925, 23 years before the state of Israel. Khalidi will be remembered for pioneering work uncovering the facts of the Nakba, the catastrophe of 1948, including the Zionist master plan for the occupation and ethnic cleansing of Palestine. Skilled debunker of Israeli historical falsehoods; understood the international dynamics that sustained them. A speech Khalidi made at the UN in 2009 on the importance of US media and government support for Israel sounds like it could have been delivered yesterday: 'It is defended by US-supplied arms, sustained by the US mass media, shielded by US diplomacy, and often manned by armed US-born colonists. The US is a major part of the problem and the major actor in the solution.' Walid Khalidi embodied the kind of historical knowledge that Israel and its backers have tried so hard to suppress."
