# War, science, and escalation: fisticuffs to nukes to MAD (philosophical talk)

**Date:** March 2026 (undated; general reflection)
**Source:** YouTube (single speaker; scientist/educator; mentions Defense Innovation Board, Vietnam-era upbringing)
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XI9NG068TwI

**Context:** Reflective monologue on human conflict escalation (fisticuffs → bow → musket → automatic weapons → missile → bombs → nukes → hydrogen bombs → ICBMs), MAD and nuclear winter, scientists' complicity in war, belief systems vs evidence and rational disagreement, reasons for war (resources, religion), Vietnam-era formation of "war is bad," and closing with UFOs / Reagan's "common enemy from space" and hope for conversation and coexistence. No direct Iran-war day-specific claims.

---

## tl;dr

**Escalation:** Human conflict has escalated from one-on-one harm (fisticuffs) through bow (1 vs 10), musket (kill at 50 yards unseen), automatic weapons (1 vs 20+), missile (hundreds), bombs (thousands), fission bombs (Hiroshima/Nagasaki, ~20 kt, "first and only two times nuclear weapons were used in warfare"), hydrogen bombs (fusion, megatons), to ICBMs — one person can kill millions from a chair. "We are beholden to the sanity of anyone who has access to that button"; given neurodiversity, not everyone has rational capacity for decisions in the world's interest.

**MAD and nuclear winter:** MAD = first strike while weapons airborne gives time to launch; total exchange could ignite forests/vegetation → carbon soot → block sunlight. Nuclear winter (early 1980s) modeled global darkening → temperature drop → plants/animals die in sequence. Model assumed even distribution of strikes; in practice strikes concentrate on silos and cities (multiple nukes per city), so spread was overestimated. That work birthed long-term climate modeling and supported asteroid-extinction theory (Yucatán impact → 70% species extinct). Einstein: "I don't know how World War III will be fought, but I know World War IV will be fought with sticks and stones."

**Scientists and war:** Scientists historically complicit; governments reach for ingenuity of scientists and engineers (chemical → chemist, biowarfare → biologist, etc.). "What is a military battle? I have energy here, I want to put it there" — matter, motion, energy = physics. "There is no war that is won without the exploitation of science and technology at its center."

**Speaker's formation:** Grew up 60s/70s, Vietnam; "all I ever knew was war is bad." Statues to war heroes, Memorial Day/Veterans Day celebration vs Vietnam-era lack of same. Concluded: sometimes war necessary (e.g. Hitler, "fighting evil forces") but "who defines who's evil?" — "not the right is what matters, but victory. The strongest has the right." Scientists resolve disagreement with "we need more data"; politicians and others don't share that wiring. Served on Pentagon Defense Innovation Board: strength of nations going forward not just soldiers or nukes but "how clever you are with computing or calculations or strategies."

**Reasons for war:** Limited resources ("I want what you have"); religion/belief systems — belief doesn't have evidence science requires; "rational arguments tend to not work; you need methods of coercion or force, ultimately threat of violence." "The less tangible the thing you're fighting for, the more abstract, the less evidence, the more willing people are to die for it." War "glorious at the beginning," then body bags; civilian casualties from decisions by people in power who are "generally not themselves the same people who go do the fighting." League of Nations, UN, Geneva Conventions notwithstanding — "can we just all get along?"

**Close:** UFOs — part of military budget investigating; speaker has never seen unexplained; if threat from space, "maybe the greatest task would be to protect Earth from aliens." Reagan at UN (1980s): "Imagine how close we would be together if we had to fight a common enemy such as an invader from space." "Perhaps we need some outside universal threat to make us recognize this common bond." "If that's what it takes for us to get along, I guess so. But I still believe in the power of conversation and the power of coexistence ... we can all be different but yet all be together with common causes, common goals that are sensible, rational, sane."
