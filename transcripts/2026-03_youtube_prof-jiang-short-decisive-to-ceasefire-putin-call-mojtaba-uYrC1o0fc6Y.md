## tl;dr

**Opening contradiction:** "At the very start of this story lies the biggest contradiction of the current war. When the operation against Iran was launched, leaders in Washington and Tel Aviv believed the campaign would be **short and decisive**. Their military planners expected that a series of strong strikes would create a shock powerful enough to corner Iran quickly. The strategy was straightforward: destroy key military facilities, disable the air defense network, and force Tehran to accept tough conditions. Inside the White House, the belief was that this pressure would last only **a few days** before Iran either gave in or faced serious political instability at home. **Surprised.** But after nearly 2 weeks, the situation looks very different."

**Iran's response:** "Instead of retreating, Iran has responded forcefully and in ways that many Western analysts did not predict." Missile and drone strikes "spreading across the region, going far beyond the scale that was originally expected." "Sites connected to US military infrastructure are now being targeted" — reports of attempted attacks on American bases in Qatar; installations in Kuwait "facing growing threats." "Across the Persian Gulf, military infrastructure linked to the United States and its allies is under increasing pressure. What was supposed to remain a **limited military action is slowly turning into a broader regional confrontation.**" "Israeli cities are now living under the constant threat of missile attacks. Air raid warning systems are being triggered again and again."

**Israeli tone shift:** "An unexpected moment. A senior Israeli government official speaking to the **Washington Post** makes a statement that sounds almost like a step back from earlier goals. According to the official, **Israel is not seeking to escalate the conflict to the point of overthrowing Iran's government.** This message contrasts sharply with earlier statements from Israeli leaders who had spoken about completely dismantling Iran's military infrastructure and strategic capabilities. Now the tone is clearly changing. More voices are beginning to mention the possibility of a **ceasefire.** … If the military operation was moving exactly according to plan, why is there suddenly talk about stopping it so urgently?"

**Trump–Putin call:** "The next twist does not happen on the battlefield, but in international politics. During one of the most tense moments of the crisis, US President Donald Trump **unexpectedly holds a phone call with Russian President Vladimir Putin.** The fact that such a conversation takes place during an active military conflict immediately draws attention. The discussion lasts **nearly an hour.** Afterward, the Kremlin comments carefully — a Russian presidential adviser: the conversation was **frank and constructive.** Officially, the leaders discuss … the situation surrounding Iran, global energy markets, and possible diplomatic solutions. … Soon after the call, the atmosphere around the crisis begins to shift noticeably. **Signals coming from Washington suddenly seem mixed.** First, Donald Trump suggests that the United States might consider **partially easing sanctions on Russia's oil industry** in order to stabilize global energy prices. … Only hours later, the tone changes sharply. Trump releases a much tougher statement **warning Iran about fire and fury** if Tehran refuses to change its stance. … What exactly happened during that conversation with Moscow?"

**Russia–Iran theory:** "Within security circles, a new theory begins circulating. Some experts believe Washington may have tried to **pressure Russia into ending its unofficial support for Iran.** The discussion focuses on the possibility that **Russia could be sharing satellite reconnaissance data with Tehran.** Russia operates one of the largest space monitoring systems in the world, capable of tracking ships, aircraft, and military movements in real time. If Iran were receiving that kind of information, it could significantly improve the accuracy of its missile and drone attacks. Interestingly, several military analysts have already noticed a curious pattern. **In recent days, Iranian strikes appear to have become far more precise.**"

**Iran broadening strategy:** "During the first days of the conflict, Iran mainly responded with limited and carefully targeted strikes. Now, however, Tehran seems to be **expanding its strategy** into something much broader. The goal no longer appears to be simple retaliation, but a **wider effort to reshape the balance of power across the region.** According to regional sources, Iran has already launched **dozens of ballistic missiles along with hundreds of drones** — the conflict is entering a far more dangerous and unpredictable phase. … In a very short time, waves of ballistic missiles, attack drones, and even naval drones have been launched … Much of this pressure is directed at **American military infrastructure** across the Middle East. US bases in Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, and the UAE now face constant risk. … Even the most advanced defenses struggle when large numbers of threats arrive at the same time. That appears to be exactly the strategy Tehran is using. **Overwhelming radar and interception systems through sheer volume.**" Maritime pressure: "Reports emerged of a commercial vessel being attacked near the Saudi coastline, dangerously close to the **Strait of Hormuz.** Nearly 20% of the world's oil passes through this narrow passage. … Oil prices begin rising. … Iran seems prepared for a **prolonged confrontation.** This reality challenges the original expectation of a quick operation."

**Inside Iran — unity not chaos:** "Early in the campaign, US planners believed heavy strikes might trigger **internal instability**. The assumption was that pressure on key infrastructure would spark protests, political chaos, and eventually weaken the leadership. Iran had experienced protests in recent years. But the situation inside the country has evolved very differently. **Instead of division, a wave of national unity appears to be forming.** Faced with an external threat, many Iranians seem to be rallying around the state. Even groups that previously criticized the government are setting disagreements aside. **Protest activity has dropped sharply.** … Iran's **Assembly of Experts selects Mojtaba Khamenei**, the son of Ali Khamenei, as the country's **new supreme spiritual leader.** Large crowds gather in Tehran to show support, suggesting **the system is stabilizing rather than collapsing** — exactly the opposite of what many in Washington had expected. Authority is becoming more centralized; **the IRGC** is especially influential, 'functioning not only as a military force, but also as a powerful political actor.' The **Supreme National Security Council** is coordinating wartime strategy, effectively pushing the country toward a **wartime governing structure.** … The conflict could last much longer than anticipated for Israel."

**Israel adjusting / ceasefire hints:** "Israeli leaders have begun adjusting their tone, emphasizing that **removing Iran's government is not the primary goal.** Early discussions sometimes mentioned weakening Iran's leadership or regime change. Now the focus is on **limited objectives and avoiding a prolonged war.** The reason is simple. Iran continues to strike back. … Some [projectiles] are intercepted by Iron Dome … but even advanced systems have limits. If enough projectiles arrive at once, some inevitably get through. … Several rockets have reached Israeli territory. … **Subtle hints about a potential ceasefire** have started appearing in official statements."

**Washington two paths:** "The administration of Donald Trump appears to be approaching **two very different paths.** One option is the more moderate approach: declare that objectives have already been achieved, claim success, gradually step back, shift toward diplomacy. But such a move **risks being seen as a retreat** if Iran continues demonstrating its ability to strike US interests. The second option is far riskier: **expanding the war into a much larger military campaign that could even include a ground invasion.** The dangers of that path are enormous. Iran is one of the largest countries in the Middle East with more than **85 million people** and a vast territory far larger than Iraq. … Iran also has a strong missile arsenal, regional allies, and **mountainous terrain** that would complicate any invasion. **Many analysts warned that a full-scale war could resemble a conflict on the scale of the Vietnam War.** … The United States can no longer operate in the Middle East with the same freedom it once had. Any large military action now risks triggering a wider regional crisis. … Discussions about negotiations are becoming more common."

**Israel on the front line:** "For Israel … the situation is especially painful because it sits directly on the front line. Rockets are not landing far away. They are targeting Israeli cities. Sirens echo across Tel Aviv and Haifa. … The economy is beginning to feel the weight of prolonged mobilization. Tourism has nearly stopped. … Israeli leaders are speaking with growing urgency — stronger accusations, urgent appeals to allies, calls for decisive action. … **If the operation began as a display of strength meant to produce a quick victory, why does it now feel as though the loudest warnings coming from Tel Aviv are echoing just as loudly in Washington?**"
