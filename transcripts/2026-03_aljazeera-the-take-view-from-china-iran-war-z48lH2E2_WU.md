# Al Jazeera The Take: What does the US-Israeli war in Iran look like from China?

**Date:** March 2026 (during second week of war)
**Source:** Al Jazeera English — The Take (podcast / YouTube)
**Speaker:** Katrina Yu (Al Jazeera correspondent, Beijing); host Malika Bilal
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=z48lH2E2_WU

## tl;dr

Katrina Yu (Beijing) explains China’s view of the US-Israel war on Iran. China has been one of the loudest critics: condemned the assassination of Iran’s leaders, said the Middle East is being pushed to the “brink of a dangerous abyss,” called for immediate de-escalation and ceasefire, and wants a return to talks. About 40% of China’s imported crude comes through the Strait of Hormuz; China has ordered refiners to halt gasoline/diesel exports to hoard fuel, lifted petrol price caps to a four-year high (8% nationwide increase Tuesday), and is reportedly using its influence with Iran to keep Chinese ships transiting the Strait. Impact so far: higher pump prices; some petrochemical firms suspending operations over supply fears. China has ~100 days of crude stockpile and has diversified (Russia, Brazil, Kazakhstan); renewables now ~20% of energy, one in two new vehicles electric. Diplomatically: special envoy Zhai Jun in the region; Foreign Minister Wang Yi making calls; back-channel style. China’s narrative: US = instability, China = stability and reliable partner; the war “gives credence” to that. Gulf states still rely on the US for security; China can’t match that. If the US is tied down in the Middle East, it has fewer resources for the Asia-Pacific — an upside for Beijing. Red lines: if the conflict directly threatens China’s security or puts it in a “desperate” energy situation, China may be forced to act; military intervention still “pretty low” for now. Trump–Xi summit (expected soon): the war is another tension on a long list; complicates the meeting but also adds pressure on both sides to show stability. Chinese officials and public: official line is US hegemonic power imposing on a vulnerable country; some citizens support Iran (portrayed in China as a friendly partner); others are superficially pro-Trump. China has long seen Iran as a counterweight; if the regime falls, that may not be in China’s interest. China has spent decades diversifying energy and building renewables partly to prepare for scenarios like this — “national security energy policy.”
