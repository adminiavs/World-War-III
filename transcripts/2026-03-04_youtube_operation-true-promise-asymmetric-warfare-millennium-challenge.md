## tl;dr

April 1 strike on Iranian embassy in Damascus (seven dead, including two commanders): act of war, embassy as Iranian territory; follows 2020 assassination of Qasem Soleimani in Baghdad. Shows US/Israel military dominance: technology (precision strike, only Iranian embassy hit, Canadian embassy next door untouched) and intelligence (signals and human, e.g. spies in embassy). Iran had to respond: Operation True Promise, strike package of 300 drones and missiles. Israel claims 99% intercepted, no damage; Iran claims attack was intentionally designed to cause limited damage. Argument: Iran more credible because it must be strategic given US/Israel dominance; dominance does not guarantee victory. 2002 Millennium Challenge: US vs Iran invasion simulation; Iran won. Reason: asymmetrical warfare. Explanation: weaker side in familiar terrain (dark forest) can set traps and define terms of engagement; do not fight symmetrically (e.g. do not send navy against aircraft carrier); use drone swarms or suicide boats so that volume overwhelms defence; cost asymmetry (e.g. $20M in drones vs $1B carrier). True Promise cost Iran ~$10–30M, Israel defence ~$1B; in a full war Iran would use asymmetrical warfare and may prevail. Q: Why don’t Americans respond with more strategic thinking? In the second run of Millennium Challenge the US ruled asymmetrical warfare “cheating” and disallowed it; US then won. Empires are inflexible; hubris means they refuse to admit failings and to adapt; asymmetrical warfare works because it rewards flexibility and creativity, which the dominant power refuses.
