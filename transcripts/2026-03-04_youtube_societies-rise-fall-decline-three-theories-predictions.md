## tl;dr

The West has already entered the phase where systems decay faster than they can be repaired; what we see is the beginning of a structural transformation of global power. Recap: monotheism produced money, the individual, and the nation-state as dominant paradigms and thus modernity, with large costs. Question: why do societies rise and why do they fall? Signs of decline: conflict and war (Ukraine, Middle East, Southeast Asia, Trump on Mexico/Venezuela); environmental degradation; unemployment and disengagement (China "lying flat," US "quiet quitting"); lower birth rates (except Israel and Georgia); lower living standards; mental health crisis; debt (public and private); lower social cohesion and trust (e.g. not helping someone hurt on the street); disease and chronic illness; immigration as a sign of declining empires; unaffordable housing; coming pension crisis. Three theories: (1) Thomas Piketty, financialization: capitalism moves from consumer (real wealth) to financial (money, claims on wealth) to monopoly capitalism; real economy ~2% growth vs financial ~5%, so capital flows to finance not production; later-stage capitalism "eating itself." (2) Peter Turchin, elite overproduction: too many elite children competing for limited positions of power; rat-utopia experiments (Calhoun): abundance and no exit lead to status competition and collapse; zero-sum status; leads to war or revolution. (3) Oswald Spengler, civilizational life cycle: civilization as organism (village → town → city → mega city); at mega-city level, abstraction and money replace direct relations, individualism replaces collective mentality, people stop working hard (immigration to do labor), birth rates plummet; mega city = death of civilization; external threats in late stage do not unite but become another factor in internal conflict (alliance with enemy). Combined framework: at core, elite families (e.g. ~200 for Rome); three pillars of control (finance, intelligence, religion); managers (middle class, PMC) between owners and workers. Rise: consent, democracy, meritocracy, managers advocate for workers. Decline: elite overproduction, corporation in trouble, managers turn on workers (rent-seeking), bureaucracy grows, deception replaces consent. Collapse: elite factions, civil war/revolution, external forces take advantage; coercion. Priorities: unity → stability → survival. Collapse is fast (perfect storm, no early warning because critics silenced). Five predictions for the West (next 5–20 years): (1) decline of democracy and freedom, more authoritarianism (2) economic collapse (3) mass immigration / population replacement (4) civil conflict (5) foreign wars to channel domestic conflict outward (war or revolution from elite perspective). Predictions test the model. Aim is understanding how power works, not moral judgment first; "first we understand, then we judge, then we act." Next session: concrete historical examples. [Opening and closing frame the lecture as part of a "secret history" course.]
