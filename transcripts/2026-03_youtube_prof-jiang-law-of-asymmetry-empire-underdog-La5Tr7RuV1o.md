# Prof. Jiang: Law of asymmetry — why the US will lose the Iran war (class lecture)

**Date:** March 2026 (undated; "US Iran war that is happening right now")
**Source:** YouTube — class/lecture format ("last class we did an overview … today we're going to provide some theoretical framework"); same channel/style as other "prof jiang" videos per URL param
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=La5Tr7RuV1o

**Context:** Lecture providing a game-theory and historical framework for why the speaker predicts the United States will lose the war. **Law of asymmetry:** the US and Iran are not peers (US far stronger) but "usually the underdog has the advantage." Three imperial advantages — mass, organization, depth (ability to absorb losses) — become over time disadvantages (inequality/complacency, elite overproduction/factionalism, hubris). Empires fall when an enemy emerges with the opposite qualities: energy (motivation), openness (learning, meritocracy), cohesion (unity, common purpose). Application: the key question is whether Iran is becoming an energetic, open, and cohesive society; if yes, Iran wins and America loses. References Peter Turchin (elite overproduction).

## tl;dr

**Prediction and today's topic:** "I made the prediction that the **United States will lose this war.** In last class we did an overview of the military and strategic position and today we're going to provide some **theoretical framework** for how to understand how this war will progress and why ultimately I believe the United States will lose. Today I present to you a very important idea in **game theory.** … It's called the **law of asymmetry.**"

**Law of asymmetry:** "The United States and Iran are not peers. They're not equals. The United States is far stronger than Iran and we should expect the United States to overwhelm and destroy Iran really easily. **The law of asymmetry states that that's usually not the case. It's usually the underdog that has the advantage.** And we see this from history all the time." Examples: **Persians (c. 490 BC)** invaded mainland Greece (Athens and Sparta); Persia was "the first world empire … infinite resources, infinite manpower, infinite wealth" and "lost to the Greeks not once but twice." **Macedonians** under the Greeks were "a tribal army, not a very large army" and "were able to go into Persia … basically most of Eurasia and conquer it in about 10 years time." **Romans** "started off as a tribal people" and built the Mediterranean empire. **Vikings.** "A people in the borderlands, a tribe, a small ethnicity are able to conquer a great empire. And the law of asymmetry tells us why."

**Three advantages of an empire:** "(1) **Mass.** This just means that they have a lot of people. … The United States has a population of about 350 million people. But remember the United States can also draw on the populations of its allies which includes the Five Eyes, the angosphere. It also includes Europe. It also includes East Asia, South Korea, Japan. Basically wherever the Americans have a military base. So most of the world are either allies of United States or vassals of United States. So the Americans can basically draw on … billions and billions of people for its wars. (2) **Organization.** Hierarchy, bureaucracy, science and technology which leads to the most advanced weaponry. … Satellites for precision targeting. … B-2 bombers, F-15s … the most advanced weaponry ever in human history. (3) **Depth** [speaker says 'death' in the recording: the idea that] because your people are infinite, because your organization is so strong, because your resources are so vast, **you can afford to lose many, many wars.** … United States loses this war in Iran. If they want to go back, they can go back with even more soldiers, even more weaponry because their wealth is infinite." In theory, with these advantages, "an empire should be invincible and eternal. But we know for a fact that **all empires fall** and they fall very hard and … they don't last a very long time — maybe 20 years, maybe 300 years."

**Advantages become disadvantages:** "All these advantages are ultimately **disadvantages in the long term.** (1) **Mass** → when you have a lot of people you create **inequality** (competition for resources, too many people) → debt and slavery → your people become **complacent, lazy, indifferent.** They're competing against each other. They really don't like the government. They're not that motivated to work or to fight wars. So mass looks like an advantage in the beginning but over time becomes a disadvantage. (2) **Organization** means an **elite.** The problem is that over time the elite become **parasites.** Why? **Rent-seeking** — you have power and you seek to monetize your power by exploiting the mass. Landlords are a classic type. They don't really work but they make a lot of money because they own the land, control resources and capital, and that forces the mass into slavery and debt — 'what's happening in America today.' Everyone wants to be part of the elite and there are **too many elites** over time. Power is a zero-sum game, a hierarchy → **elite overproduction** (term coined by the historian **Peter Turchin**). His theory: you have turmoil, wars — e.g. the French Revolution, the Roman Republic — because there are too many elites and they start wars to gain power for themselves. So organization/elite leads to **factionalism or infighting.** 'That's what's happening in America today where the Democrats and the Republicans, they hate each other. They refuse to get along.' **Political polarization.** Another way of saying this: an empire over time becomes **insular.** They don't care about what happens outside the world. All they care about is Washington DC and as a result they make a lot of stupid mistakes. (3) **Depth** [death]: if there are no consequences to your actions you become **arrogant, lazy, incompetent.** You don't care. This leads to **hubris** — 'blindness to your own arrogance, a violent arrogance that makes you make silly mistakes, but you don't recognize your mistakes and you keep on repeating the same mistakes over and over again.' So: **mass → inequality; organization → elite overproduction → factionalism; depth → hubris.** That's why an empire must fall over time. But an empire cannot fall by itself. **It needs an enemy that forces it to collapse.** An enemy will emerge that will overwhelm the empire because it is able to take advantage of the weaknesses of the empire."

**Weaknesses of empire / advantages of the enemy:** "The first major weakness of the empire is that **the people aren't really motivated.** So if your people are really motivated, you have a huge advantage. We call this **energy.** … The empire becomes insular. You refuse to admit your mistakes. You refuse to learn from others. Well, if you are **willing to admit your mistakes, willing to learn, willing to promote the best and brightest, willing to be meritocratic**, you have a huge advantage. This idea of **openness.** … The empire is fractured. So if you're able to **maintain cohesion**, if people are able to remain united, if they're able to have a **common purpose**, they'll be strong. **Cohesion.** So if an enemy of the empire emerges with these three qualities — **energy, openness, and cohesion** — this enemy will defeat the empire."

**Application to the Iran war:** "What I want you to focus on as you follow the news in this war between United States and Iran is ask yourself one question. **Will Iran become an energetic, open, and cohesive society?** Because if it does, it will become invincible. The empire won't change because of hubris. It's too static. It's too stagnant. It's too bureaucratic. So the empire won't change. The empire will not admit its own mistakes. And quite honestly, Iran because it's a country of 92 million people still suffers from issues of mass, organization, and depth. And so **if it's able to transform itself into an energetic, open, cohesive society, then it will triumph over America.** That is the fundamental question in this war. Don't worry about America, its technology. Don't worry about the economy. Don't worry about these things. Just ask yourself one question. **Is Iran transforming as a society?** Are people becoming much more motivated? Are people in Iran learning from their mistakes and innovating and becoming resilient on the battlefield? Are the Iranians sticking together as a people? Because if the answer is yes to all three questions, then **Iran will win and America will lose.**"
