## 2. Credibility Assessment

### Scott Ritter, *Target Iran* (2006)

- **Qualifications:** Former UN weapons inspector in Iraq (1991–1998). First-hand experience with WMD claims and Iraq war build-up.
- **Strengths:** Book was treated as a serious critical source at publication (e.g. Democracy Now! with Seymour Hersh). Direct critique of Bush administration Iran policy and of exaggeration of Iran's nuclear program; aligns with later declassified and IAEA record. Insider perspective on how regime-change narratives are built.
- **Caveats:** Ritter later faced criminal convictions (2001 charge sealed after probation; 2009–2011 conviction and prison for conduct in an online sex sting). Post-release he has contributed to Russian state outlets (RT, Sputnik), which can affect how his current commentary is received. The 2006 book predates these issues and can be evaluated on its own for that period.
- **Verdict:** Credible as a **primary source for 2006 critical discourse** and for understanding contemporary claims about White House plans. Use the text for historical and argument structure; treat his later persona and affiliations separately when citing post-2006 work.

### Barsamian et al., *Targeting Iran* (2007)

- **Authors:** David Barsamian (journalist, Alternative Radio), Noam Chomsky (linguist, political analyst), Ervand Abrahamian (historian, Iran specialist), Nahid Mozaffari (scholar). Multiple expert perspectives.
- **Strengths:** Academic and activist publisher (City Lights). Covers 1953 coup, US–Iran collision course, internal Iranian dynamics, consequences of US policy. Chomsky and Abrahamian are widely cited in IR and history. Documentary adaptation (2013) indicates sustained interest.
- **Caveats:** Explicitly critical of US policy; not a neutral or pro-administration source. Still a credible **analytical and historical** source for the arguments it makes.
- **Verdict:** Credible for **historical context, policy critique, and predictions about consequences of US policy**. Fits with other project materials (Chomsky transcripts, Ron Paul 2006 speech).

## 3. Predictions Identified (from secondary sources and summaries)

## 4. Accuracy vs Outcomes

- **War:** 2006–2007 predictions said the trajectory would lead to war. No US–Iran war in the 2006–2007 or Bush-era timeframe; **large-scale US–Israel–Iran war appears in project news in March 2026**. So: **direction correct, timing wrong by roughly two decades**.
- **Regime change / plans:** Ritter and Ron Paul (and contemporary press, e.g. Hersh, *New Yorker*) correctly identified **active planning and rhetoric** for regime change and military options. That planning did not result in immediate war but aligns with a long-run pattern that later led to open conflict.
- **Oil, Hormuz, economic costs:** Ron Paul’s 2006 warnings (oil, Hormuz, inflation) are consistent with themes in 2026 project coverage (e.g. ING, oilprice, Binance, 7news food/fertiliser). **Partially testable and consistent** with later outcomes.
- **Blowback / intervention backfires:** Recurrent theme in both books and Ron Paul; 2026 news (regional escalation, Gulf targets, China/Russia angles) does not contradict that frame.

**Summary:** Predictions are **partially accurate**: correct about eventual military confrontation and about structural drivers (regime-change policy, oil, regional instability); **wrong or silent** on near-term timing (no 2006–2007 strike). Information is **accurate enough to be worth using** for context and comparison, with timing explicitly noted.

- **Credibility:** Both books are **credible for their purpose**: Ritter as 2006 critical insider perspective (with caveats on his later record), Barsamian/Chomsky et al. as scholarly and activist analysis with multiple experts. They align with other project sources (Ron Paul 2006, Chomsky transcripts).
