## Likely Scenario: Remainder of March 2026

Based on the trajectory in the archive, stated timelines (Hegseth: ~8 weeks; Trump: ~4 weeks), and structural factors:

### Week 2 (Mar 8-14): Escalation plateau

- **Air campaign intensifies** as announced ("surge dramatically"). B-2s and carrier-based strikes continue dismantling missile production, defence industries, and dual-use infrastructure. Israel continues parallel strikes.
- **Iran's retaliatory capacity diminishes** (already 90% reduction in ballistic, 83% drones by day 6) but does not stop. Expect continued drone/missile attacks on Gulf states and Israel at lower but persistent rates, with periodic larger salvos using stockpiled weapons.
- **Hormuz remains closed or severely restricted.** Insurance companies will not cover transit. Oil likely climbs toward $100-110+.
- **Humanitarian crisis deepens.** Internet stays near zero. Civilian casualties mount. Medical supply chains collapse. UN agencies escalate rhetoric.
- **Kurdish proxy front** in western Iran intensifies with CIA support. Turkey likely objects. This could create a secondary friction point with NATO.
- **Gulf states' anger grows.** Interceptor stocks deplete. Investment withdrawal threats become more concrete. Saudi, UAE, Qatar may begin formal diplomatic moves against the campaign or demand a timeline.
- **China sends special envoy** to the Middle East as announced. Xi-Trump summit preparations continue for late March.

### Week 3 (Mar 15-21): Attrition and pressure

- **Iran's air defences are largely destroyed** (80%+ already by day 7). US/Israel achieve operational air supremacy over most of Iran. But ground-based resistance, dispersed mobile launchers, and proxy operations continue.
- **Lebanon front:** Israel's "next phase" in Lebanon forces further displacement. Hundreds of thousands more refugees. International pressure mounts.
- **Economic damage widens globally.** Fertiliser crisis hits planting seasons (Southern Hemisphere winter crops, Northern Hemisphere spring crops). Food price inflation visible in consumer economies. Shipping costs remain elevated. LNG shortages affect Europe and Asia.
- **Iran's succession process:** Assembly of Experts works on selecting a new Supreme Leader. Hardliners consolidate. Mojtaba Khamenei or another IRGC-aligned figure likely emerges. This directly contradicts Trump's demand for a "say."
- **Diplomatic feelers:** Oman, Qatar, and possibly China attempt back-channel contact. Iran's stated position remains "no negotiation," but behind-the-scenes messaging likely begins through intermediaries. The pattern from the 12-day war (Israel eventually asked for ceasefire) may repeat, though the US position ("unconditional surrender") makes this harder.
- **Domestic US pressure builds.** Job losses, market declines, and gas prices create political friction. Anti-war protests intensify. But with Congress having voted, institutional constraint is absent.
- **Russia factor:** Intel sharing continues. Russia benefits from US distraction and high energy prices. Ukraine talks may stall further.

### Week 4 (Mar 22-31): Inflection point

- **Xi-Trump summit** (reported for late March). China's leverage is now significant: it holds US debt, buys Iranian oil, and controls critical supply chains. Taiwan, trade, and Iran are all on the table simultaneously. China's interest is in prolonging the conflict at manageable levels to drain US resources, not in resolving it quickly.
- **Regime change question becomes acute.** By week 4, US/Israel will have destroyed most of Iran's conventional military capacity (navy, air defence, missile production). But as Lammy, Pape (CBS), and Abrahamian all noted: air power alone has never toppled a regime. Iran's government continues to function. The question of ground troops or a political exit ramp becomes unavoidable.
- **Three possible trajectories for late March:**
  1. **Stalemate / "mowing the grass":** US declares objectives met (missile capacity degraded, navy destroyed, nuclear sites hit) and transitions to a sustained lower-intensity air campaign with no ceasefire. Iran continues retaliating at reduced levels. Hormuz partially reopens under naval escort. Oil settles at $95-110. No regime change. This is the most likely outcome by month's end.
  2. **Negotiated de-escalation:** Back-channel produces a framework (through Oman/China/Qatar). Iran accepts some form of nuclear constraints in exchange for end of bombing and partial sanctions relief. Trump reframes as victory. This is possible but unlikely by end of March given both sides' stated positions.
  3. **Ground escalation:** Kurdish offensive expands; US special forces or regular troops enter Iran (Khuzestan or Kurdish areas). This would be the scenario Abrahamian warned of in 2007 -- requiring 500k troops and draft -- and would transform the conflict. Trump has said "waste of time" but the structural logic of regime change pushes toward it. Unlikely in March but the pressure builds.

Turkey (Kurdish pushback), Indonesia (BoP), UK (no offensive use of bases), Spain (base refusal), Gulf (interceptors, $2T withdrawal talk), and **$1B/day + gas + 92k jobs** create a **political and fiscal ceiling**. ## Response to WSJ “missile cities blunder” (Iran’s underground bunkers)

**Source:** [WSJ](https://on.wsj.com/4lereBg). Iran built underground “missile cities” to protect its arsenal; less than a week in, US/Israeli aircraft strike launchers when they emerge and bombers have hit sites, “apparently entombing” weapons below. Analysts: much of the remaining stockpile is in bases whose locations are “mostly known” to US/Israel. Lair (James Martin Center): “What was once mobile and difficult to find is no longer mobile, and easier to hit.”

### What Chomsky would say

The frame is wrong. The article presents Iran’s “strategy” as a “blunder” and the “fundamental flaw” as Iran’s choice to go underground. But the flaw is not the missile-city concept; it’s the assumption that you can plan against a superpower that reserves the right to wage unlimited war. Iran had two bad options: stay above ground and be hit immediately, or go underground and be hit when you move or when entrances are struck. Calling that a blunder shifts responsibility from the attacker to the defender.

What goes unstated is the framework for thinkable thought: the US and Israel are entitled to circle the skies, “entomb” weapons, and destroy launchers “when they emerge to fire.” There is no question of whether they have the right to do this. The only question is how well Iran’s defenses held up. That’s the same framework that made the Iraq invasion a “mistake” (we got it wrong) rather than a supreme international crime. Here, Iran’s preparations “backfire”; our bombardment is just the given.

ElBaradei said a military attack would be “absolutely bonkers” and would drive the program underground. So Iran had already been pushed toward hardening and dispersal. Now the same policy that pushed them there is being used to say their response was a blunder. The “fundamental” the article misses: as long as the US and Israel insist that what we say goes, and that Iran may not have a deterrent, every Iranian strategy will be judged a failure in retrospect. The real blunder is the war.

### What a strategic analyst (Jiang / realist) would say

From a cold, strategic view: Iran’s investment in fixed underground sites assumed a limited strike scenario (one-off raids, Osirak-style) or a long standoff where survivability mattered more than rapid launch. It did not assume a full, sustained air campaign with persistent ISR and strike over “dozens” of bases. So in *this* war, the trade-off turned against them: mobility would have been more valuable than hardened hide sites once the adversary chose to maintain constant pressure and strike at emergence. Lair is right that “mobile and difficult to find” became “fixed and easier to hit.”

But “blunder” is a strong word. Iran did not choose this war. Given US/Israeli doctrine (decapitation, degradation of missiles, no tolerance for a serious deterrent), any posture would have been second-guessed after the fact. Above-ground and mobile would have been “exposed and wiped out in week one.” Underground and fixed: “entombed and easier to hit.” The lesson for other states is not that Iran was stupid; it is that against an opponent with full-spectrum dominance and no political constraint, passive defense is insufficient. You need either credible deterrence (which Iran was denied the right to have) or great-power cover. That’s why China and Russia matter in the long game: they change the calculus of what the US can do with impunity.

So in response to the WSJ: tactically, the piece is right that in this campaign the missile cities are being exploited. Strategically, the “blunder” is the war itself—and the side paying the lasting cost may still be the one that started it (oil, Hormuz, allies, stockpiles, “day after” deterrence). Iran’s bunkers did not cause the war; the decision to wage it did.
