# The Iran War and Canada

What the predictors said about Canada, what has happened, and what is likely to come.

Last updated: 9 March 2026 (Day 10 of the war)

---

## How Canada got involved

Canada was not consulted before Operation Epic Fury. It did not participate in the strikes. But within hours, Prime Minister Mark Carney and the Albanese government in Australia issued statements supporting the US action to prevent Iran from obtaining nuclear weapons.

Carney has been walking a tightrope ever since. He has said the strikes appeared "inconsistent with international law." He said his support was "not a blank cheque." He called the war "another example of the failure of the international order." But when asked directly, he could not rule out Canada's military participation.

Canada's position is defined by a single tension: it is too dependent on the United States to openly oppose a US war, and too democratic to support a war its population did not vote for and that may be illegal.

## What the predictors said about Canada

### Professor Jiang (~March 2025, one year before the war)

Jiang specifically listed Canada among the countries that would be drawn into the coalition. His predicted lineup was: UK, Israel, Saudi Arabia, Australia, UAE, Poland. Canada was not on that list, but the logic he described applies directly.

Jiang's broader argument about middle powers is central to understanding Canada's position. Middle powers like Canada and Australia face the same structural problem:

1. **They depend on the US for security and trade.** Canada shares the world's longest undefended border with the United States. Its economy is deeply integrated with America's. Trump has openly threatened tariffs, annexation rhetoric, and pressure on Canada -- making the cost of defying the US very high.

2. **They have no independent capacity to shape the war.** Canada does not have the military power to intervene in the Middle East in a way that would change the outcome. Its options are limited to diplomacy, humanitarian assistance, and managing the domestic fallout.

3. **They are structurally pulled toward compliance.** NATO, Five Eyes, and bilateral defence agreements mean that even when Canada says "we are not participating," its intelligence apparatus, its military partnerships, and its political relationships make true neutrality very difficult.

Jiang's game theory applies here: Canada is a player with very few moves, caught between a powerful ally it cannot control and a war it cannot stop. The rational move is to say as little as possible while preparing for the economic consequences.

Jiang also made a broader prediction about what the war means for the world order. He argued that if the US loses or becomes bogged down in Iran, the world becomes multipolar -- and middle powers like Canada will need to find new security arrangements. His March 2025 lecture explicitly described a post-American world in which countries like Canada would need to align with regional blocs rather than rely on a single superpower. Carney's Davos speech -- "Nostalgia is not a strategy; stop invoking a rules-based international order as though it still functions as advertised" -- reads like Jiang's argument translated into the language of a G7 leader.

### Noam Chomsky (2006-2007)

Chomsky had specific things to say about Canada in *Targeting Iran* (2007) and *What We Say Goes* (2007):

1. **The US has alienated Canada.** Chomsky noted that the US under Bush had managed to alienate even Canada -- "they just told them to get lost" -- over NAFTA disputes and sovereignty issues. The pattern he described (the US treating allies as subordinates and expecting compliance) is visible again under Trump, who has threatened tariffs on Canada, floated annexation, and made Carney's diplomatic life very difficult.

2. **Canada as an energy provider.** Chomsky noted that Canada is "one of the major energy providers to the United States." This gives Canada a different economic position from most US allies in the Iran war. While countries like Australia and Japan are hurt by high oil prices (they import oil), Canada's oil sands and energy sector actually benefit from elevated prices. This creates a split interest: Canada's government opposes the war diplomatically, but parts of its economy profit from the oil spike the war has caused.

3. **Allies drawn in "defensively."** Chomsky predicted that allied countries would be drawn into the war under the label of "defensive" operations, even if they disagreed with the offensive. Canada has so far resisted this pull more successfully than Australia or the UK, but Defence Minister McGuinty's statements -- "Canada was not involved, not consulted, not providing support" -- may not hold if the war escalates and Trump applies pressure.

### Ron Paul (2006)

Paul's predictions about oil, Hormuz, and economic blowback apply to Canada differently from most countries. Canada is a major oil producer (the world's fourth-largest). An oil price spike that devastates importing countries like Australia, Japan, and most of Europe can actually boost Canadian government revenues and energy sector profits in the short term.

But Paul also warned about inflation, hidden costs, and the broader economic damage from a regional war. Canada's economy is tightly linked to the US economy. If the war causes a US recession, inflation shock, or financial market disruption, Canada will be hit regardless of its oil revenues.

Paul's warning about Congress abdicating war powers has a parallel in Canada: Carney has not sought parliamentary approval for any military involvement, and his refusal to rule out participation means the decision could be made without democratic debate.

## What has actually happened to Canada (Days 1-10)

### Diplomatic position

- **Day 1 (28 Feb):** Carney backed the strikes alongside Albanese, supporting US action to prevent Iran from acquiring nuclear weapons.

- **Day 4 (4 Mar):** Carney conceded the strikes were "prima facie inconsistent with international law" and said his support was "not a blank cheque." He described the war as "another example of the failure of the international order."

- **Day 5 (5 Mar):** Speaking in Canberra alongside Albanese, Carney told reporters he wanted de-escalation but could not rule out Canada's military participation in the war (Al Jazeera). Analysts warned Trump could pressure Canada for military support if the conflict escalated. Carney had not been consulted before the operation.

- **Day 5 (5 Mar):** At the Lowy Institute in Sydney, Carney spoke frankly about Trump: "It is not easy." His approach: respect but not obsequiousness.

- **Day 7 (7 Mar):** Defence Minister McGuinty urged both sides to seek a ceasefire. He stated that Canada was "not involved, not consulted, not providing support" and that the American-Israeli incursion was "theirs." He said middle powers like Australia and Canada should stick together, look after their citizens in the region, and monitor the situation.

### The Carney doctrine: "Nostalgia is not a strategy"

The most significant Canadian contribution to the political response has been Carney's framing of the crisis. At Davos earlier in 2026, he declared: "Nostalgia is not a strategy. Stop invoking a rules-based international order as though it still functions as advertised."

Guardian columnist Zoe Daniel called the war "a vindication" of Carney's Davos speech. The argument: the US-Israeli attack on Iran, conducted without UN Security Council authorisation and likely illegal under international law, proves that the old rules-based order is dead. Middle powers cannot rely on it. They need to "close ranks" and build something new.

Carney has positioned Canada and Australia as "strategic cousins" -- middle powers that share the same dilemma and should cooperate to navigate it. This language is new and significant. It represents a shift from the traditional "alliance with America" framing toward a "protect ourselves from America's decisions" framing.

### Domestic impact

- **Anti-war protests in Canada.** IRIB (Iranian state TV) listed Canada among countries with anti-war demonstrations (alongside London, Washington, Berlin, San Francisco, and others).

- **IRGC terrorism on Canadian soil.** Bolton noted that both Canada and Australia had experienced IRGC-linked terrorism domestically, which made their support for anti-Iran action politically easier.

- **Rare earth diversification.** Reuters reported (6 Mar) that Japan, France and Canada are working on alternatives to a US-led trade bloc for rare earth supplies. This is consistent with the broader theme of allies hedging against US unpredictability -- not just in the Iran war but in trade, technology, and strategic resources.

- **Oil and energy.** Unlike Australia, Canada is a net oil exporter. The oil price spike (Brent above $108 on 9 Mar) benefits Alberta's oil sands in the short term. But the broader economic disruption, inflation, and trade uncertainty hurt the Canadian economy overall, especially sectors tied to US demand.

## What the predictions say will happen next for Canada

### Short term (Week 2, March 10-14)

- **Pressure from Trump.** If the war escalates and casualties mount, Trump will pressure allies to contribute more. Canada is a NATO member and a Five Eyes partner. Trump has already publicly criticised the UK for insufficient support and threatened Spain with trade sanctions. Canada -- which Trump has previously threatened with annexation rhetoric, tariffs, and economic pressure -- is an obvious target for similar treatment.

- **The "can't rule it out" problem.** Carney's refusal to rule out military participation leaves the door open. If Gulf states escalate their requests for help, or if a NATO article is invoked in connection with the conflict, Canada could be pulled in further. Each step is small; the cumulative direction is toward involvement.

- **Energy windfall vs inflation.** The oil spike helps Canadian government revenues but hurts Canadian consumers through higher gas prices and inflation. This creates domestic political tension: the government benefits fiscally from a war it says it opposes.

### Medium term (March 15-31)

- **The middle-power bloc.** Carney's "strategic cousins" pitch to Australia could expand. If the war continues, countries like Canada, Australia, Germany, France, Japan and South Korea -- all US allies, all uneasy about the war, all unable to stop it -- may begin coordinating more actively on de-escalation, humanitarian response, and economic hedging. This is the world Jiang described: the post-American order forming not after the war ends, but during it.

- **The Xi-Trump summit.** A late-March summit is expected. Canada has been visiting Beijing -- Carney is among the leaders who have met Xi recently to diversify markets away from the US (CBC). If China brokers or facilitates a de-escalation, Canada's early diplomatic positioning (supporting strikes but insisting on legality and de-escalation) will look prescient. If the war escalates, Canada will face harder choices.

- **Rare earth and supply chain diversification.** The Japan-France-Canada rare earth initiative is a concrete step toward reducing dependence on US-led economic structures. This is consistent with Chomsky's long-term prediction that US aggression would push allies toward alternative arrangements, and with Jiang's prediction that the war would accelerate the shift to a multipolar world.

### Longer term

- **If troops go in.** Jiang's ground invasion scenario would create pressure on NATO allies to contribute. Canada sent troops to Afghanistan for over a decade. The political and institutional channels for Canadian military participation already exist. The question is whether Carney's "not a blank cheque" holds under real pressure, or whether it follows the same path Chomsky described: allies protest privately, comply publicly, and participate "defensively" until the distinction between defence and offence disappears.

- **The petrodollar question.** Jiang argued that the war could collapse the petrodollar system. For Canada -- a major oil exporter that trades in US dollars -- this would have profound consequences. If Gulf states switch to other currencies or if the dollar's dominance weakens, Canada's energy revenues and US trade relationship would both be affected. Canada's interest in diversifying trade (the Carney-Xi meetings, the rare earth bloc, the "strategic cousins" pitch to Australia) suggests Ottawa is already hedging against this scenario.

- **Canada's identity crisis.** Chomsky's observation from 2007 -- that the US had "alienated Canada" by treating it as a subordinate -- has deepened under Trump. Canada is being forced to answer a fundamental question: is it an American ally that follows where the US leads, or is it an independent middle power that makes its own decisions? The Iran war is accelerating that question. Carney's "nostalgia is not a strategy" is an answer, but it is an answer that has not yet been tested by real pressure.

## The difference between Australia and Canada

Australia and Canada are often discussed together as "middle powers" facing the same dilemma. But there are important differences:

| | Australia | Canada |
|---|---|---|
| **Oil** | Net importer; fuel crisis within days | Net exporter; short-term energy windfall |
| **Military involvement** | Three crew on US sub that sank Iranian ship; Pine Gap providing targeting data; Gulf states asking for military help | Not involved, not consulted, not providing support (so far) |
| **Fuel vulnerability** | 26 days diesel; farmers cut off; panic buying; Tasmania ran dry pre-war | Energy-rich; not facing fuel crisis |
| **Stock market** | ASX lost $111bn in one day (3.4%) | Less directly exposed (energy stocks up) |
| **Political framing** | "This is not Iraq and we are not the Howard government" (Wong) | "Not a blank cheque"; "inconsistent with international law" (Carney) |
| **Structural bind** | AUKUS ($368bn submarines), Pine Gap, Five Eyes | NATO, Five Eyes, US border/trade dependence |
| **Biggest risk** | Fuel crisis, food crisis, mission creep | Trump pressure, involuntary military participation, NATO invocation |

The core dilemma is the same: both countries are too dependent on the US to say no, too democratic to say yes without consequences, and too small to change the outcome. The difference is in where the pain lands first. For Australia, it is fuel and food. For Canada, it is political and diplomatic.
