# The Iran War for Beginners

A plain-language guide to the US-Israel war on Iran, the people who predicted it, and what is likely to happen next.

Last updated: 9 March 2026 (Day 10 of the war)

---

## Part 1: What is happening?

On 28 February 2026, the United States and Israel launched a massive military attack on Iran. They called it Operation Epic Fury. In the opening hours, they killed Iran's supreme leader, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, in an airstrike on Tehran.

The stated reason: Iran was close to building a nuclear bomb and posed an immediate threat to the United States. No evidence for an imminent threat was provided.

Ten days later, the war has spread far beyond Iran. At least a dozen countries have been affected. Missiles, drones and bombs have hit Iran, Lebanon, Israel, Bahrain, Qatar, the UAE (including Dubai), Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Jordan, Iraq and Cyprus. Hundreds of civilians are dead. Oil prices are spiking. Flights across the Middle East have been cancelled. The Gulf cities that millions of people called home are being evacuated.

This guide explains three things:

1. Who saw this coming (some people warned about it 20 years ago)
2. What they said would happen (and how much of it has come true)
3. What is likely to happen next (based on their track record so far)

## Part 2: The people who predicted this war

Three voices stand out because they made specific, testable predictions -- and got most of them right.

### Ron Paul (2006)

**Who is he?** A Republican congressman from Texas. He gave a speech to Congress on 6 April 2006 titled "Iran: The Next Neocon Target."

**What was he saying?** That the same people who pushed the US into the Iraq war were already planning to do the same thing to Iran, using the same arguments (weapons of mass destruction, regime change, pre-emptive war). He warned that an Iran war would be much worse than Iraq.

**His key predictions in plain English:**

- America will eventually attack Iran using the same playbook it used for Iraq
- Oil prices will spike dramatically and ordinary Americans will pay for it at the pump
- The Strait of Hormuz (a narrow waterway where a huge share of the world's oil passes through) will be disrupted
- The war will be paid for by printing money, which means hidden inflation that hits the poor and middle class hardest
- Congress will not stop it, even though the Constitution says only Congress can declare war
- The war will backfire in ways nobody expects, worse than Iraq

### Noam Chomsky (2006-2007)

**Who is he?** A professor at MIT, one of the most cited scholars in history. He wrote two books about a potential Iran war: *What We Say Goes* (2007) and *Targeting Iran* (2007, with Ervand Abrahamian and others).

**What was he teaching?** Chomsky's argument is that the US does not attack countries because of genuine threats. It attacks countries that refuse to follow American orders. Iran has been independent of American control since its revolution in 1979, and that independence -- not nuclear weapons -- is the real reason the US wants to destroy it. He argued that the US follows a pattern: it offers a country impossible terms, the country refuses, and then the US uses the refusal as proof of hostility to justify war.

**His key predictions in plain English:**

- The US and Iran were on a "collision course" and the US was following the exact same path it took with Iraq
- Attacking Iran would not weaken the hardliners in charge -- it would make them stronger. When you bomb a country, the people rally around whoever is in charge, even if they did not like them before
- Iran would be pushed closer to Russia and China, creating exactly the kind of powerful anti-American alliance that US policy is supposedly trying to prevent
- European countries would get dragged in, even if they disagreed with the war, because they would feel obligated to help "defend" their allies
- The war would cause an oil shock that would raise food prices worldwide, because fertiliser (which farmers need to grow food) is made from oil and gas, and a lot of it passes through the Strait of Hormuz
- Propaganda and fear would override common sense, just like they did with the Iraq WMD lie. Most Americans would be skeptical, but the war would happen anyway

**The core of Chomsky's teaching:** The world is not divided into good guys and bad guys the way the news presents it. It is divided into countries that follow American orders and countries that do not. Countries that follow orders get called "allies" and "democracies" (even if they are dictatorships, like Saudi Arabia). Countries that refuse get called "threats" and "rogue states." The rules of international law are applied to enemies and ignored when they get in the way of what powerful countries want to do. This is not a conspiracy theory -- it is a pattern you can track by reading the public record. Chomsky's method is simple: compare what governments say with what they actually do, and read the documents they publish.

### Professor Jiang (2024-2026)

**Who is he?** A high school teacher who taught a class on game theory and geopolitics. His lectures were recorded and uploaded to YouTube on a channel called "Predictive History." He started teaching about a potential Iran war roughly two years before it happened, and his predictions have been remarkably accurate.

**What was he teaching?** Jiang uses a tool called game theory to explain the war. Game theory is a way of thinking about situations where the outcome depends on what multiple players do. Instead of asking "who is right?", game theory asks: "what does each player want, and what will they do to get it?" Jiang's core argument is that all the major players -- the US, Iran, Israel and Saudi Arabia -- are all acting logically according to their own interests, but those interests lead to a catastrophe that none of them can individually prevent.

**His key predictions in plain English:**

**Two years before the war (~April-July 2024):**

- If Trump wins the 2024 election, he will start a war with Iran. (He said this was almost certain.)
- Iran fights differently from how the US fights. Iran uses cheap, small weapons (drones that cost $50,000) against America's expensive weapons (missiles that cost $1-13 million each). This means Iran can afford to keep fighting much longer than the US can afford to keep defending. He called this "asymmetric warfare."
- The Gulf countries (Dubai, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia) look rich and powerful but are actually extremely fragile. They have almost no fresh water (they get it from desalination plants that turn seawater into drinking water). They import most of their food. Their populations are mostly foreign workers who will leave if things get bad. Iran can destroy these countries simply by hitting their water plants and oil facilities with cheap drones.
- Three forces are pushing America toward war: (1) the Israel lobby and evangelical Christians who believe war in the Middle East fulfils biblical prophecy, (2) the need to protect the dollar's dominance (the "petrodollar" -- Gulf countries sell oil in US dollars, which keeps the dollar valuable), and (3) Saudi Arabia, which sees Iran as a mortal threat but learned from its failed war in Yemen that it cannot fight Iran alone
- When Khamenei dies or is killed, his son Mojtaba will be chosen as the next supreme leader, because the military (the IRGC) prefers him -- he is unpopular with ordinary Iranians and would depend on the military to stay in power, which gives the military more control

**One year before the war (~March 2025):**

- War will happen within two to four years. (It happened 11 months later.)
- The war will be given a patriotic name (he guessed "Operation Iranian Freedom") and justified with five arguments: (1) democracy and freedom for Iran, (2) Iran is one month from a nuclear bomb, (3) protect shipping lanes, (4) protect allies like Israel and Saudi Arabia, (5) terrorism. He pointed out that America had been saying "Iran is one month from a bomb" for the past 10 years and it was never true.
- The US-led coalition would include the UK, Israel, Saudi Arabia, Australia and the UAE. (All confirmed.)
- The US will use "shock and awe" -- massive bombing, high-tech weapons, special forces -- because that is the only kind of war the US military knows how to fight since it abandoned traditional ground warfare after 2003. But shock and awe does not work against Iran, because Iran has spent decades preparing for it. Iran has spread its military command across 31 separate centres so that destroying the capital does not stop the war.
- If the US sends ground troops into Iran, those troops will be trapped. Iran is a mountainous country of 90 million people. You cannot supply an army through mountains when the enemy has drones and rockets. The troops will not be soldiers -- they will be hostages. And once they are trapped, the US will be forced to send more troops to rescue them (this is called the "sunk cost fallacy" -- throwing good money after bad because you cannot admit the first investment was a mistake).
- The Iranian people will NOT welcome American soldiers. They remember that the US and UK overthrew their democratic government in 1953 and installed a dictator (the Shah). They watched what happened to Iraq next door. They have a strong sense of national identity going back thousands of years. And their religion teaches them that resisting American power is a sacred duty.
- Trump is like Zelensky (the president of Ukraine) -- a TV personality who cares more about how things look on camera than about whether they work strategically

**The core of Jiang's teaching:** Every country involved in this war is acting rationally according to its own goals. The problem is that all their rational decisions add up to a disaster. The US wants regime change (which requires ground troops). Iran wants the US to invade (because it knows a ground war in Iran would be a catastrophe for America). Israel and Saudi Arabia both want the US and Iran to destroy each other, so that Israel is left as the dominant power in the Middle East. The result is that everyone is moving toward a ground invasion that will be terrible for everyone, but especially for ordinary soldiers and civilians.

## Part 4: What is likely to happen next

Based on the track record of these predictions so far, here is what the evidence suggests.

### The next few days (Week 2: March 10-14)

**The bombing will continue but will not end the war.** The US and Israel will keep hitting Iranian oil facilities, military sites and infrastructure. Iran's air defences will get weaker. But Iran's ability to fire drones and missiles will not stop, because those weapons are small, cheap and hidden across a huge mountainous country.

**The new supreme leader will be announced.** Mojtaba Khamenei will likely be confirmed. Israel has already said it will try to kill him too. This creates a cycle: kill the leader, a new one appears, threaten to kill the new one. Chomsky predicted this exact pattern -- attacking a country makes the hardliners stronger, not weaker.

**Oil prices will keep climbing.** The Strait of Hormuz is closed or nearly closed. This affects about 20% of the world's oil supply. Gas prices were already up 11% in one week. Analysts are warning of $100+ oil soon, and Qatar has warned $150 within three weeks. This hits ordinary people at the pump and at the grocery store.

**The ground troops question will get louder.** Trump has floated 5,000 troops. Axios reported discussions about special forces raids to secure uranium. The Pentagon has reportedly planned for a 100-day war (through the autumn). The pressure to send soldiers in will grow because the bombing alone is not achieving regime change. This is exactly what Jiang warned about: the logic of the war pushes toward a ground invasion, even though a ground invasion in Iran would be far worse than Iraq or Afghanistan.

**The water crisis in the Gulf will deepen.** Both sides are now hitting desalination plants (the facilities that produce drinking water from seawater). Iran hit one in Bahrain. The US hit one on Qeshm Island in Iran. The Gulf countries get 42-90% of their drinking water from these plants. If more are targeted, there will be a genuine humanitarian crisis in some of the world's richest countries.

### The next few weeks (March 15-31)

**Food prices will start to rise worldwide.** A third of global fertiliser trade passes through the Strait of Hormuz. If the strait stays closed through March, the damage to the 2026 growing season will be locked in regardless of when the war ends. Chomsky flagged this exact chain: oil shock leads to fertiliser shortage leads to food inflation leads to political instability in countries far from the battlefield.

**The real casualty numbers will start to come out.** Iran claims 220 US soldiers were killed in a single day. The US says the number is 8-10. Seven US deaths have been officially confirmed over 10 days. The gap between the two numbers is unsustainable. Soldiers talk. Medics talk. Satellite images show destroyed bases. At some point, the truth surfaces. This is what happened in Vietnam: the government's numbers and reality diverged until the gap could no longer be maintained.

**China will use its leverage.** A summit between Xi Jinping and Trump is expected in late March. China is Iran's biggest oil customer. China holds enormous amounts of US debt. China has already publicly warned that the situation is heading toward nuclear escalation and must stop. At the summit, China will have significant bargaining power.

**The question will become: how does this end?** There are three possible paths:

1. **Stalemate** (most likely in the short term): The US declares it has "degraded" Iran's military, scales back bombing, Hormuz partially reopens, oil settles at $95-110. No regime change. The war becomes a low-intensity conflict that grinds on. Nobody wins.

2. **Negotiations** (possible): Back-channel talks through Oman, Qatar or China produce some kind of deal. Iran agrees to nuclear constraints in exchange for the bombing stopping. Trump declares victory. Iran declares victory. The reality is that nothing fundamental has changed, but everyone has a story to tell.

3. **Ground escalation** (unlikely but no longer impossible): Special forces go in for uranium. Something goes wrong. Soldiers are captured or killed. The "sunk cost" logic kicks in: we have to send more troops to rescue the ones already there. This is the scenario Jiang has been warning about for two years. It is also the scenario Iran has been preparing for and, according to Jiang, actually wants.

### The bigger picture

The most important thing to understand is that this war is not a surprise to anyone who was paying attention. Three separate groups of people -- a Republican congressman in 2006, a world-renowned MIT professor in 2007, and a high school teacher in 2024 -- all independently reached the same conclusion: the United States was going to attack Iran, it was going to use the same arguments it used for Iraq, and it was going to go badly.

They were right about the hardliners getting stronger. They were right about the Gulf countries being vulnerable. They were right about the coalition. The predictions that have not yet come true -- ground troops becoming hostages, nuclear threats, the dollar being damaged, the war being worse than Iraq -- are all trending in the direction they described.

None of these people had secret information. Ron Paul read the public statements of the people advocating for war and took them at their word. The lesson is not that these three people are unusually brilliant (though they may be). The lesson is that the information was available to anyone. The pattern was visible. The warnings were public. The war happened anyway.

## Part 5: Key concepts explained simply

### Asymmetric warfare

When a weaker country fights a stronger country, it does not try to match the stronger country weapon for weapon. Instead, it finds cheap ways to make the war expensive for the other side. Iran's approach: use drones that cost $50,000 to force America to fire missiles that cost $1-13 million. Even if the US shoots down every drone, it goes broke first. The Pentagon fired a quarter of its entire supply of one type of missile (THAAD interceptors) in a single engagement in June 2025, and it only produced 11 replacements that entire year.

### The petrodollar

The US dollar is the world's main currency because oil-producing Gulf countries (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Qatar) sell their oil in dollars. When other countries want to buy oil, they need dollars, which keeps demand for the dollar high. In return, the Gulf countries invest their dollars back into American banks and stock markets. If the Gulf countries collapse or switch to another currency, demand for the dollar drops and the American financial system is in serious trouble. This is why the US has military bases all over the Gulf -- not primarily to defend those countries, but to protect the system that keeps the dollar dominant.

### Shock and awe vs decentralized warfare

"Shock and awe" is the American military strategy of hitting the enemy's capital and leadership with overwhelming force so that the rest of the country surrenders. It worked in Iraq in 2003 -- Baghdad fell quickly. But Iran has learned from Iraq. Instead of putting all its leadership in one place, Iran spread its military command across 31 separate centres, each with its own plans and authority to act independently. Killing the supreme leader or destroying Tehran does not stop the war, because every other region keeps fighting on its own. This is like the difference between a company with one CEO (cut off the head, the body falls) and a network with no single leader (there is no head to cut off).

### The sunk cost fallacy

This is a thinking error where you keep investing in something that is failing because you have already spent so much on it. If you buy a movie ticket and the movie is terrible, the sunk cost fallacy is what keeps you in your seat thinking "I paid $15, I might as well stay." In war, the sunk cost fallacy is what keeps countries fighting wars they are losing. Once American soldiers are in Iran and some of them die, it becomes politically impossible to say "we made a mistake, let's leave" because then the deaths were "for nothing." So you send more soldiers, and more of them die, and now it is even harder to leave. This is exactly what happened in Vietnam. Jiang argues it is exactly what will happen in Iran if ground troops go in.

### Game theory

Game theory is a way of analysing situations where the outcome depends on what multiple players do. It does not ask "who is right?" -- it asks "what does each player want, and what will they logically do?" Jiang's key insight is that every major player in this war is acting rationally from their own perspective, but their individual rational choices add up to a collective disaster:

- **The US** wants regime change in Iran (which requires ground troops to actually achieve)
- **Iran** wants to absorb the bombing and lure the US into a ground invasion (because it knows a ground war in Iran would be catastrophic for America)
- **Israel** benefits most if both the US and Iran exhaust each other, leaving Israel as the dominant power in the region
- **Saudi Arabia** wants the same thing as Israel, but Saudi Arabia is much easier to destroy than Israel (flat desert, exposed oil and water infrastructure, mostly foreign workers who will leave)

The result: everyone is moving toward a ground invasion that will be terrible for everyone, especially for ordinary soldiers and civilians, but each player has their own reason to keep pushing in that direction.

### Desalination

Gulf countries have almost no natural fresh water. They get their drinking water from desalination plants -- large industrial facilities on the coast that remove salt from seawater. These plants are expensive, fragile, and easy to hit with a cheap drone. Kuwait gets 90% of its drinking water from desalination. The UAE gets 42%. Saudi Arabia gets 70%. If these plants are destroyed, millions of people have no water. Both sides in this war have now hit desalination plants (Iran hit one in Bahrain; the US hit one on Qeshm Island in Iran). This is why Jiang says the Gulf countries are fundamentally indefensible: the thing their populations need most to survive is sitting on the coast, unprotected, within easy range of Iranian drones.

## A note on sources and skepticism

This guide presents the predictions of three people who got a lot of things right. That does not mean everything they say is correct. Ron Paul has political views that many people disagree with. Chomsky's framework is controversial in mainstream political science. Jiang is a high school teacher whose lectures include speculation and theories alongside well-sourced analysis.

The reason their predictions matter is not because they are always right. It is because the people who were supposed to get it right -- intelligence agencies, Pentagon planners, mainstream media analysts -- got it wrong. The CIA predicted Iranians would welcome American intervention. They did not. The Pentagon predicted a quick, decisive campaign. It was not. The administration predicted that killing the supreme leader would break the regime. It did not.

When the official predictions fail this badly, it is worth listening to the unofficial ones that did not.

Read everything critically. Check the sources. Ask who benefits. That is what Chomsky, Paul and Jiang all teach, each in their own way.
