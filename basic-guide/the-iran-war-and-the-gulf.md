# The Iran War and the Gulf

What is happening to Dubai, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia and Oman -- and why a high school teacher said six years ago that these countries were fundamentally indefensible.

Last updated: 13 March 2026 (Day 14 of the war)

---

## What happened

On 28 February 2026, the United States and Israel launched Operation Epic Fury against Iran. Iran responded by firing drones and missiles at Middle Eastern countries that host US military bases. Within two weeks, every Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) state -- Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait and Oman -- has been struck.

These countries did not choose this war. Most of them tried to stay out of it. But they host US military bases, and Iran views those bases as part of the attack on its territory. Mojtaba Khamenei, Iran's new supreme leader, stated: "We have warm relations with 15 neighbours. The enemy established bases in some of them to secure dominance. We attacked only those bases."

The Gulf countries are now trapped between their dependence on the United States for security and Iran's declaration that any country hosting US operations is a legitimate target.

## Why the Gulf is so vulnerable

Professor Jiang taught this in his lectures starting in 2024, roughly two years before the war. His argument was not that the Gulf countries were weak militarily. It was that they were structurally fragile -- designed for peacetime prosperity but fundamentally indefensible in a war.

### 1. Water

The Gulf countries have almost no natural fresh water. They get their drinking water from desalination plants -- large industrial facilities on the coast that remove salt from seawater. These plants are expensive, fragile, and easy to hit with a cheap drone.

| Country | Drinking water from desalination |
|---------|--------------------------------|
| Kuwait | ~90% |
| Saudi Arabia | ~70% |
| UAE | ~42% |
| Qatar | ~99% |
| Bahrain | ~60% |

Both sides have now hit desalination infrastructure. Iran struck a plant in Bahrain. The US struck one on Qeshm Island in Iran. If more are targeted, millions of people face a genuine water crisis in some of the world's richest countries.

Jiang's point: you can build the tallest skyscraper in the world, but if one drone hits the plant that makes your drinking water, the skyscraper is uninhabitable.

### 2. Food

The Gulf countries import most of their food. Saudi Arabia imports about 80% of its food supply. The UAE imports roughly 90%. These imports arrive by sea -- through shipping lanes that are now under threat -- and by air, through airports that are now being struck by drones.

The Strait of Hormuz handles not just oil but roughly a third of the world's fertiliser trade. The Gulf countries themselves are major fertiliser producers (Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Oman). When their facilities are hit and the Strait is closed, the food system is damaged at both ends: the countries cannot import food easily, and the world cannot get the fertiliser it needs to grow food.

### 3. People

A large proportion of the population in Gulf countries are foreign workers -- people from South Asia, Southeast Asia, Africa and elsewhere who came for jobs. When a war starts:

- Foreign workers want to leave
- Their embassies want to evacuate them
- The labour force that runs the economy -- construction, hospitality, healthcare, transport -- starts to disappear
- The ultra-wealthy leave first (reports of private jets costing $250,000 for a one-way ticket out of Dubai)
- The people who stay are the ones who cannot afford to leave

Jiang described this as the fundamental vulnerability of the Gulf model: these countries look like superpowers on Instagram but function like labour camps in a crisis. When the workers leave, the economy stops.

### 4. Oil infrastructure

The Gulf countries' wealth comes from oil and gas facilities that are spread across flat desert terrain -- easy to see from the air and easy to hit with cheap drones. Unlike Iran (which is mountainous and has spent decades hiding military assets in tunnels and caves), the Gulf is flat and exposed.

Iran can hit every oil facility, desalination plant, airport and military base in the Gulf with drones that cost $50,000 each. The interceptor missiles used to shoot those drones down cost $1-13 million each. Even if every drone is intercepted, the Gulf goes broke on interceptors before Iran runs out of drones.

This is Jiang's asymmetric warfare argument applied to the Gulf: the maths are against the defenders.

## The financial exodus

The war is not just a military crisis for the Gulf. It is an economic one.

- **Citi and HSBC** are suspending branch operations in the region after Iran threatened financial partners of the US
- **Standard Chartered** is evacuating Dubai
- The Dubai government has had to reassure the world that air defences intercepted drones near the airport -- but "near the airport" is not a reassuring phrase for investors or tourists
- **Data centres at risk:** Gulf countries host 70+ data centres (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud, Oracle, Alibaba), with roughly $30 billion in projects under construction. Iran has explicitly warned that infrastructure linked to Google, Microsoft, Palantir, IBM, Nvidia, and Oracle could become targets
- **Aluminium prices** hit a 4-year high because major smelters are located in Qatar and Bahrain
- Gulf countries collectively hold trillions of dollars in sovereign wealth funds. There has been talk of $2 trillion in potential withdrawals from Western markets. Even the rumour of such withdrawals moves markets.

Jiang predicted that the Gulf states would be "permanently damaged as destinations for Western investment and expatriates." Whether that proves true depends on how long the war lasts, but the exodus of banks, the suspension of operations, and the targeting of airports suggest the damage is already significant.

## The political crisis

### Bahrain

Bahrain has a Shia-majority population ruled by a Sunni monarchy. The ruling Al Khalifa family has long relied on Saudi and Western support to maintain power. During the 2011 Arab Spring, Saudi troops entered Bahrain to suppress pro-democracy protests.

Now, the war has reignited that tension. Mass protests have erupted against the ruling class's role as a launchpad for the US war against Iran. Bahrain has imported anti-riot troops from Jordan -- the first time since 2011 it has called in foreign forces to suppress domestic unrest. Authorities are firing tear gas into crowds.

A US official told Drop Site News that the demonstrations show how "deeply unpopular" the US-backed regional order is in Bahrain and "the broader Muslim world."

### Qatar

Qatar has tried harder than any other Gulf state to maintain relationships with both the US and Iran. Qatar hosts the largest US military base in the Middle East (Al Udeid) but also maintains diplomatic ties with Iran and has historically served as a mediator.

The war has made that balancing act impossible. Qatar PM Sheikh Mohammed bin Abdulrahman condemned the Iranian strikes as "unacceptable under any pretext" while simultaneously calling on both sides to return to negotiations: "Iran's strikes on its neighbours serve benefit for no one."

Qatar's FM had previously described the situation as a "betrayal" -- Qatar had tried to mediate between Iran and the international community, and was attacked anyway. The UNSC voted for a resolution denouncing Iranian attacks on GCC states, but resolutions do not stop drones.

### Saudi Arabia

Saudi Arabia sees Iran as its primary regional rival. The kingdom has been ramping up oil flow through its east-to-west pipeline to bypass the Strait of Hormuz. But this pipeline is itself vulnerable to attack, and if Yemen's Houthis resume attacks on vessels in the Red Sea, even the Red Sea port alternative is at risk.

Saudi Arabia's crown prince warned in 2018 that if the Iran nuclear deal was scrapped, Saudi Arabia would develop its own nuclear weapons. The deal was scrapped. The war has been launched. The nuclear proliferation risk that Kovalik warned about is now live.

### The UAE

The UAE has tried to diversify its economy away from oil toward tourism, finance, and technology. Dubai in particular has positioned itself as a global hub -- a place where the world does business. Iranian drones striking residential buildings and airports in Dubai directly attacks that brand.

The UAE called the Iranian strikes "brutal and unjustified Iranian aggression" -- but the phrase captures their frustration at both sides. The UAE did not ask for this war but is paying the price for hosting US military infrastructure.

## What the predictors said

### Jiang (2024)

Jiang was the most specific about the Gulf. His key arguments:

- The Gulf countries "look rich and powerful but are actually extremely fragile"
- Iran can destroy these countries "simply by hitting their water plants and oil facilities with cheap drones"
- The foreign workers who run the economy "will leave if things get bad"
- The Gulf countries' wealth depends on being perceived as safe and stable; once that perception is broken, the money leaves
- The GCC states would eventually "break with the US" -- privately at first, then publicly

On Day 14, the private breaking is visible (Qatar PM condemning strikes, Bahrain importing riot police, Citi/HSBC leaving) but no Gulf state has yet publicly asked US forces to leave.

### Chomsky (2006-2007)

Chomsky predicted that the oil shock would create a "fertiliser shortage, which would cause a food crisis." The Gulf countries are both victims and vectors of this chain -- they are major fertiliser producers whose output is now disrupted, and they are food importers whose supply lines are under threat.

He also predicted that allies would be drawn into the war "defensively" and that "European countries would get dragged in." The Gulf countries are the most extreme example: they did not choose to participate in the attack on Iran, but because they host US bases, they are being struck as if they did.

### Kovalik (2018)

Kovalik described the ultimate goal as "creative destruction" -- the destruction of the Middle East's oil capacity so the world would depend on American fossil fuels. Whether or not this is the conscious intent, the effect is consistent: Gulf oil infrastructure is being targeted, 20 million barrels per day are off the market, and the US (the world's largest oil producer) is positioned as the alternative supplier.

## What happens next for the Gulf

### If the war ends within weeks

The physical damage is repaired. Airports reopen. Banks return. But the perception of safety is permanently dented. Insurance premiums for Gulf operations rise. Some businesses and expatriates do not come back. The Gulf's growth trajectory is slower than it would have been.

### If the war continues through March and beyond

- **Water becomes critical** if more desalination plants are hit. Millions of people depend on these facilities. There is no alternative water source in the desert.
- **The financial exodus accelerates.** If Citi and HSBC are the first to leave, others follow. The Gulf's role as a global financial hub weakens.
- **The expatriate workforce thins.** The people who build, clean, cook, drive, nurse and teach in Gulf cities start to leave. The cities cannot function without them.
- **Oil revenue drops.** If oil cannot be exported through the Strait, revenue drops even as global prices rise -- the Gulf has oil but cannot sell it.
- **Political pressure mounts.** Bahrain is already importing riot police. If other Gulf populations begin protesting their governments' role in a war they did not choose, the political stability that underpins Gulf prosperity is threatened.

### The question nobody is asking

The Gulf countries built their entire model on three assumptions: that the US would protect them, that Iran would not attack them directly, and that the world would keep sending money, workers and tourists. All three assumptions have been broken in two weeks.

Jiang's deepest argument is that the Gulf's vulnerability is not a temporary wartime problem. It is a structural reality that the war has revealed. The desalination plants, the oil facilities, the airports, the imported food, the foreign workers, the exposed coastlines -- none of these things become less vulnerable when the war ends. The knowledge that a $50,000 drone can shut down a country that spent billions on skyscrapers is now public. That knowledge does not go away.

Whether the Gulf can rebuild its model after the war, or whether the war marks the beginning of a permanent decline, depends on questions that nobody in the region -- or in Washington -- appears to be asking yet.
