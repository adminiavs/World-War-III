# The Road to 2026

A timeline of the key events that led to the US-Israel war on Iran. Most of this history is absent from current news coverage. Understanding it changes everything about how the war looks.

Last updated: 13 March 2026 (Day 14 of the war)

---

## 1953: Operation Ajax -- the coup that started everything

**What happened:** Iran had a democratically elected prime minister named Mohammad Mossadegh. In 1951, Mossadegh nationalised Iran's oil industry -- meaning he took control of Iran's own oil away from the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (now known as BP). Under the previous arrangement, Iran received only 16% of the profits from its own oil. Mossadegh proposed a 50/50 split.

The British refused. Instead of negotiating, they went to the Americans. In 1953, the CIA (under the codename Operation Ajax) and British intelligence overthrew Mossadegh's government and installed Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi -- a monarch who would rule as a dictator for the next 26 years.

**Why it matters now:** The Shah created a police state enforced by SAVAK, one of the most brutal secret police forces in the world. Iranians remember this. When Americans talk about "bringing democracy to Iran," Iranians remember that the last time the US intervened in their country, it destroyed their democracy and installed a tyrant.

Chomsky: "The people who hold the clubs typically like to forget history. The people who are hit by the clubs tend to remember."

Kovalik traced the entire "plot to attack Iran" back to this moment: "The United States has, through decades of meddling, helped make Iran what it is today."

---

## 1979: The Islamic Revolution

**What happened:** After 26 years of the Shah's dictatorship -- supported by the US, armed by the US, kept in power by the US -- the Iranian people overthrew him. The revolution of 1979 replaced the Shah with an Islamic republic led by Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini.

Shortly after, Iranian students stormed the US embassy in Tehran and held 52 Americans hostage for 444 days. This event -- the hostage crisis -- became the defining image of Iran in the American mind. It created a well of anger that US politicians have drawn on ever since.

**What is usually left out:** The hostage crisis did not happen in a vacuum. The students who stormed the embassy were motivated partly by the fear that the US was planning another coup -- the same way it had overthrown Mossadegh in 1953 from the same embassy. The US had just admitted the deposed Shah into America for medical treatment, and many Iranians believed this was a prelude to reinstalling him.

Abrahamian (in *Targeting Iran*): "Politicians who have given in to ultimatums are usually seen as traitors. The ones who have stood up against ultimatums are seen as national heroes." This is the lens through which Iranians view their own revolution.

**Why it matters now:** 1979 is the moment Iran stopped following American orders. In Chomsky's framework, this -- not nuclear weapons, not terrorism, not human rights -- is the real reason the US has been trying to destroy Iran ever since. "Iran disobeyed orders."

## 1980-1988: The Iran-Iraq War

**What happened:** Saddam Hussein's Iraq invaded Iran in 1980, beginning an eight-year war that killed an estimated 500,000 to 1,000,000 people. Saddam used chemical weapons -- supplied with Western knowledge and in some cases Western materials. The US at various points supported both sides, but predominantly backed Iraq.

**Key facts usually omitted:**

- The US removed Iraq from its terrorism list in 1982 specifically to provide military aid against Iran
- Donald Rumsfeld visited Baghdad as Reagan's envoy in 1983, shaking hands with Saddam
- When Saddam gassed Kurdish civilians at Halabja in 1988, the US initially blamed Iran
- The US Navy shot down Iran Air Flight 655 in 1988, killing all 290 civilians on board. President George H.W. Bush said: "I will never apologise for the United States of America. Ever. I don't care what the facts are."

**Why it matters now:** Half a million Iranians died in trenches while the world either looked away or actively helped the enemy. The UN did nothing about Saddam's chemical weapons. This experience is burned into the Iranian national memory. It is why Iran built its own military capabilities, why it does not trust international guarantees, and why -- as Abrahamian explained -- many Iranians see nuclear deterrence as a matter of survival, not aggression.

---

## 2003: The Iraq War -- the invasion that strengthened Iran

**What happened:** The US invaded Iraq in March 2003, claiming Saddam Hussein had weapons of mass destruction. He did not. The WMD claims were fabricated or exaggerated. Saddam was overthrown, tried, and executed.

**The irony:** Saddam's Iraq was Iran's most powerful regional enemy. By destroying him, the US eliminated Iran's main adversary and created a power vacuum that Iran filled. Iraq's new government was dominated by Shia politicians with close ties to Tehran. As Chomsky noted: "Rumsfeld, Cheney and Wolfowitz created Shiite-dominated Iraq, close to Iran."

**The lesson Iran learned:** The US destroyed Iraq, which had given up its WMD programs. It left North Korea alone, which had nuclear weapons. The lesson, as Israeli military historian Martin van Creveld wrote: "The invasion of Iraq was basically a plea to Iran to develop nuclear weapons." If you want to avoid being attacked, get a deterrent. If you give up your deterrent, you get attacked.

**The lesson the US did not learn:** Ron Paul gave his "Iran: The Next Neocon Target" speech to Congress in April 2006 -- three years after the Iraq invasion, warning that the same people, using the same arguments, were already planning the same thing for Iran. Ritter published *Target Iran* the same year with the same warning. Neither was heeded.

## 2003: The grand bargain Iran offered -- and the US rejected

**What happened:** In May 2003, Iran sent a comprehensive proposal to the US through the Swiss ambassador. Iran offered:

- Full transparency on its nuclear program
- Cooperation against al-Qaeda
- Acceptance of a two-state solution for Israel and Palestine
- An end to support for Hamas and Hezbollah's military activities
- A normalisation of relations

The Bush administration did not reply. The Swiss ambassador who transmitted the offer was reprimanded.

Chomsky: "We'll negotiate if they first concede in advance. And also with a gun pointed at their heads."

**Why it matters now:** When the administration says "Iran refused to negotiate," the 2003 offer is never mentioned. Iran offered everything the US said it wanted -- nuclear transparency, regional cooperation, recognition of Israel. The US refused to even respond.

---

## 2015: The JCPOA -- the nuclear deal

**What happened:** After years of negotiations, Iran signed the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) with the US, UK, France, Germany, Russia and China. Under the deal, Iran agreed to strict limits on its nuclear program -- reducing its uranium enrichment, dismantling centrifuges, and submitting to the most rigorous inspections regime in the world. In exchange, international sanctions on Iran were lifted.

The IAEA confirmed repeatedly that Iran was complying with the deal.

**The two views:**

- **The mainstream view:** The JCPOA was a diplomatic achievement that prevented Iran from getting a nuclear weapon through negotiation rather than war.
- **Kovalik's view:** The JCPOA was a "Trojan Horse" -- "always intended" to strip Iran of its nuclear deterrent, leaving it defenceless. "The United States was motivated to end Iran's nuclear ambitions for the precise purpose of leaving Iran vulnerable to attack."

---

## 2018: Trump withdraws from the JCPOA

**What happened:** In May 2018, President Trump unilaterally withdrew the US from the nuclear deal, despite Iran's verified compliance. He reimposed sanctions -- the harshest in history -- on Iran and any country that did business with it.

Iran had complied with every provision of the deal. The IAEA confirmed it. European allies opposed the withdrawal. It happened anyway.

**Why it matters now:** Kovalik's "Trojan Horse" thesis rests on this sequence: convince Iran to give up its nuclear capabilities under a deal, then break the deal and attack the now-defenceless country. Whether or not this was the conscious intent, the sequence is exactly what happened -- JCPOA signed (2015), Iran complies, US withdraws (2018), sanctions reimposed, war launched (2026).

Khamenei had privately opposed the deal, believing the US was not a reliable partner. Kovalik: "In his substantial wisdom, he was correct in his misgivings."

## 2020: The assassination of Qasem Soleimani

**What happened:** On 3 January 2020, the US assassinated Major General Qasem Soleimani -- the commander of the IRGC's Quds Force and one of the most powerful figures in Iran -- in a drone strike at Baghdad airport. He was in Iraq on a diplomatic visit.

Soleimani was not a minor figure. He was the architect of Iran's regional strategy, the man who built the "axis of resistance" (Hezbollah, Iraqi Shia militias, the Houthis), and arguably the second most powerful person in Iran after Khamenei. His assassination was the most provocative US act against Iran since 1953.

**Iran's response:** Iran launched ballistic missiles at US bases in Iraq (Ain al-Asad). The US initially claimed no casualties. It later emerged that over 100 soldiers suffered traumatic brain injuries. Trump dismissed the injuries as "headaches."

**Why it matters now:** The Soleimani assassination showed Iran that the US was willing to kill its top leaders without warning, in a third country, during what Iran considered a diplomatic mission. This is the context for Iran's military preparations in the years that followed -- the same preparations that Iran drew on when Operation Epic Fury began.

Jiang taught the Soleimani assassination as a turning point: it was the moment Iran concluded that war was not a question of "if" but "when."

## 2024: Operation True Promise -- the dress rehearsal

**What happened:** In April 2024, Israel struck the Iranian embassy in Damascus, Syria, killing seven people including two senior IRGC commanders. An embassy is considered sovereign territory under international law. The strike was unprecedented.

Iran responded with Operation True Promise: approximately 300 drones and missiles fired at Israel. Israel claimed 99% were intercepted and no significant damage was done. Iran claimed the attack was intentionally designed to be symbolic -- a demonstration of capability, not an attempt to cause mass casualties.

**Jiang's analysis:** This is one of the most important events in Jiang's teaching. He argued that True Promise accomplished all four of Iran's strategic goals simultaneously:

1. **United the population:** Showed Iranians their military could strike Israel directly
2. **Built alliances:** Demonstrated to Russia and China that Iran was willing to fight
3. **Won global opinion:** By designing the strike to cause minimal casualties, Iran maintained the moral high ground
4. **Weakened the enemy:** Israel wanted to respond disproportionately, but the US restrained it -- creating tension between Israel and America

Jiang: "From a military dominance perspective, Operation True Promise accomplished nothing. From an asymmetric warfare perspective, it accomplished all four major goals."

**The cost asymmetry:** True Promise cost Iran an estimated $10-30 million. Israel spent at least $1 billion defending against it. This is the core of asymmetric warfare -- the weaker side makes the war expensive for the stronger side.

**Why it matters now:** True Promise was the dress rehearsal for what happened in March 2026. Iran tested its drone and missile capability, learned from the results, and refined its approach. When Operation Epic Fury began, Iran's retaliatory strikes were more effective -- drones were "finding their mark" as interceptors flagged, the THAAD radar was destroyed, and the Strait of Hormuz was closed. The $50,000 drone vs. $1-13 million interceptor equation that Jiang described in April 2024 is now playing out at scale.

## 2024-2025: The path to war

**What happened in the background:**

- **November 2024:** Trump won the presidential election. Jiang had predicted: "If Trump wins, he will start a war with Iran." He gave this roughly a two-to-four year timeline.
- **2025:** The US withdrew from the JCPOA's remaining provisions, reimposed maximum-pressure sanctions, and began a massive military buildup in the Middle East. Israel continued striking Iranian targets in Syria. Hezbollah was degraded through years of conflict. Syria was weakened by civil war. The "axis of resistance" was being dismantled -- exactly the sequence Kovalik described in 2018.
- **June 2025:** A brief Israel-Iran exchange saw the Pentagon fire over 100 THAAD interceptors -- a quarter of its entire supply -- while producing only 11-12 replacements per year. The ammunition crisis that Jiang predicted was already visible.
- **Late 2025:** The Bondi Hanukkah attack in Australia (December, 15 killed) and other incidents hardened political support for action against Iran in allied countries.

## 28 February 2026: Operation Epic Fury

**What happened:** The US and Israel launched a massive coordinated attack on Iran, killing Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in the opening strikes. The operation was given a patriotic name (Jiang had predicted "Operation Iranian Freedom"; the actual name was "Operation Epic Fury"). It was justified with five arguments -- all of which Jiang had listed a year earlier: nuclear threat, protecting allies, protecting shipping lanes, fighting terrorism, and bringing democracy.

No evidence of an imminent Iranian attack was provided. The Pentagon later told Congress there was no intelligence suggesting Iran was planning to strike first. A peace deal through Oman was "within reach" before the strikes began.

Fourteen days later, the war has spread to over a dozen countries, killed more than 1,350 Iranian civilians and 630 Lebanese, cost the US over $11 billion in the first week alone, and closed the Strait of Hormuz -- the artery through which 20% of the world's oil flows.

Every person who warned this would happen -- Paul in 2006, Chomsky in 2007, Kovalik in 2018, Jiang in 2024 -- was ignored. The war happened anyway.

## The pattern

Read the timeline again. There is a pattern:

| Year | What the US did | What it said | What actually happened |
|------|----------------|--------------|----------------------|
| 1953 | Overthrew Iran's democracy | "Protecting freedom" | Installed a dictator for 26 years |
| 1980s | Armed Saddam against Iran | "Containing aggression" | 500,000+ Iranians died; Saddam used chemical weapons |
| 2003 | Rejected Iran's grand bargain | (Did not respond) | Lost the best chance for peace |
| 2003 | Invaded Iraq | "WMD" | No WMD; strengthened Iran; created chaos |
| 2015 | Signed nuclear deal | "Preventing a bomb" | Iran complied; US withdrew anyway |
| 2018 | Withdrew from deal | "Iran can't be trusted" | Iran had been complying |
| 2020 | Killed Soleimani | "Imminent threat" | No evidence of imminent attack |
| 2026 | Launched war | "Nuclear threat" | No bomb; IAEA and CIA confirmed; peace deal was on the table |

Chomsky's method: compare what governments say with what they actually do.
