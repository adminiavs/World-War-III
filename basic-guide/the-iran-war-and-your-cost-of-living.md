# The Iran War and Your Cost of Living

How a war 10,000 miles away is making your groceries, petrol, and everything else more expensive -- and why it is going to get worse before it gets better.

Last updated: 13 March 2026 (Day 14 of the war)

---

## The short version

The US and Israel attacked Iran on 28 February 2026. Iran responded by closing the Strait of Hormuz -- a narrow waterway through which about 20% of the world's oil supply passes. Within two weeks:

- Oil went from under $70 a barrel to over $120 (now ~$90 after emergency reserve releases)
- US gas prices went from $2.98 to $3.58 a gallon ($5+ in California)
- Australian diesel went from A$1.70 to A$2.20 per litre
- New Zealand petrol is heading toward $3 per litre
- The Dow Jones crashed 750 points in a single day
- Fertiliser prices surged, threatening the 2026 growing season worldwide
- The IEA called it "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market"

This guide explains the chain of cause and effect: how a war in the Middle East reaches your wallet, why it is going to keep getting worse, and who predicted all of this twenty years ago.

---

## Step 1: The Strait of Hormuz closes

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow channel between Iran and Oman. It is only 33 miles wide at its narrowest point. About 20% of the world's oil -- roughly 20 million barrels per day -- passes through it.

Before the war, about 138 tankers transited the Strait every day. By Day 14, that number had collapsed to approximately one. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) declared: "Not a liter of oil through the Strait of Hormuz." An IRGC spokesman warned: "Expect oil at $200 per barrel."

Iran has laid mines in the Strait -- estimates range from 2,000 to 6,000. The US military has destroyed 16 Iranian mine-laying vessels, but the mines already in the water remain. At least 10 vessels have been attacked since the war began. Five major shipping lines have suspended Gulf operations. International tankers are largely avoiding the Strait entirely.

Saudi Arabia has a pipeline from its eastern coast to the Red Sea, but oil experts say this will not replace what the Strait carried. If Yemen's Houthis resume attacks on vessels in the Red Sea, even that alternative is at risk.

---

## Step 2: Oil prices spike

When 20% of the world's oil supply is taken off the market, the price of the remaining oil goes up. This is basic supply and demand.

| Date | Brent crude (per barrel) | US gas (per gallon) |
|------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| 27 February (day before war) | ~$68 | $2.98 |
| Day 6 (peak) | ~$120 | Rising |
| Day 14 | ~$90 (after emergency reserves) | $3.58 ($5+ in California) |

The IEA (International Energy Agency) has released 400 million barrels of oil from strategic reserves -- more than double the amount released after Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine. This brought prices down from their peak, but it is a stopgap. Strategic reserves are finite. You cannot keep draining them indefinitely.

Trump released 172 million barrels from the US Strategic Petroleum Reserve, which is now only 59% full. The same day, he claimed rising oil prices were "good" because "the United States is the largest oil producer in the world... when oil prices go up, we make a lot of money." Oil company profits do not reach consumers paying $5 a gallon.

Deutsche Bank analyst Jim Reid: "With each passing day it gets harder to argue that the disruption will only prove temporary."

Oxford Economics modelling: if oil hits $140 per barrel for eight weeks, it would trigger recessions in the Eurozone, the UK, and Japan. The US would face a "temporary standstill."

---

## Step 3: Everything that moves gets more expensive

Oil is not just fuel for your car. It is the energy that moves everything in the global economy. When oil gets more expensive, the cost increase cascades through every supply chain:

**Transport:** Every product you buy was delivered by a truck, a ship, or a plane that runs on diesel or jet fuel. When diesel costs more, delivery costs more, and the price of the product goes up.

**Manufacturing:** Factories use energy. When energy costs more, manufacturing costs more.

**Heating and electricity:** In many countries, electricity is generated partly from oil and gas. Higher energy prices mean higher utility bills.

**Air travel:** Airlines are directly exposed. Air New Zealand shares dropped 8%. Route viability is at risk.

The result: even if you do not drive a car, the war is making your life more expensive.

## Step 4: The fertiliser crisis -- this is the one that will hurt most

This is the step that most people do not see coming. Noam Chomsky flagged it twenty years ago.

### What is fertiliser and why does it matter?

Modern farming depends on nitrogen-based fertilisers -- chemicals like urea, ammonia, and other nitrogen products that help crops grow. Without fertiliser, crop yields drop dramatically. Less food is produced. Food prices go up.

### Where does fertiliser come from?

A huge share of the world's fertiliser trade passes through the Strait of Hormuz. Estimates range from 35% to 45% of global urea supply. The Gulf countries (Saudi Arabia, Qatar, UAE, Bahrain, Oman) are major producers.

### What is happening now?

The Strait is closed. Fertiliser is not shipping.

| Region | What is happening |
|--------|-------------------|
| **United States** | The American Farm Bureau Federation wrote to the White House warning of food supply disruptions "not seen since 2022, when food price inflation reached 40-year highs" |
| **Australia** | 64% of urea imports come from the Gulf; prices surged from A$830/tonne to A$1,195/tonne in two weeks; suppliers telling farmers "we can't give you a price"; peak demand (April-June winter crop) is weeks away |
| **New Zealand** | NZ Treasury modelling: if conflict lasts 3+ months, inflation rises 0.5-1 percentage point and GDP falls 0.2-0.4; BNZ: "worst of all worlds for the average New Zealander" |
| **Global** | Aluminium at 4-year high (smelters in Qatar and Bahrain affected); helium supply at risk; urea prices in Middle East roughly double year-ago levels |

### Why the timing is devastating

The agricultural calendar does not wait for wars to end. In Australia, the peak fertiliser demand season is April-June (winter crop). In the Northern Hemisphere, spring planting is underway. If fertiliser does not arrive in the next few weeks, the 2026 growing season is damaged regardless of when the war ends.

This is the chain Chomsky described in 2006-2007: oil shock → fertiliser shortage → food inflation → political instability in countries far from the battlefield.

## Step 5: Food prices rise

When farming costs more (diesel for machinery and trucks, fertiliser for crops) and transport costs more (diesel for delivery), food prices go up.

This has not fully hit grocery shelves yet on Day 14, but the signals are clear:

- **Australian farmers** have turned off operations. Jake Turner (WA Wheatbelt): "We have turned a farming operation off from front to back." Tractors use 200 litres of diesel per hour and he has less than 500 left.
- **Australian fishing fleets** are grounded. Pavo Walker (Mooloolaba): "The three major fishing wharves have no fuel for us. This week's haul could be the last catch for the foreseeable future."
- **The American Farm Bureau** has warned the White House directly.
- **Australian rural depots** are running dry. Fuel sales spiked 238% in Adelaide Hills/Barossa.

Food prices typically lag oil prices by 4-8 weeks. The war started on 28 February. If the Strait stays closed through March, grocery prices in the US, Australia, New Zealand, Europe and Asia will start rising noticeably in April-May.

---

## Step 6: Interest rates, markets, and the economy

The economic damage is spreading beyond fuel and food:

- **Stock markets:** The Dow crashed 750 points on Day 14. The ASX (Australia) lost $111 billion in a single day. Asian markets tumbled (Seoul -8%, Tokyo -7%, Taipei -5%). Fertiliser stocks surged (CF Industries up 65% year-to-date -- the people who sell fertiliser profit when it becomes scarce).

- **Interest rates:** Central banks were expected to cut interest rates in 2026 to ease the cost of living. The war-driven inflation spike has reversed that. Australia's RBA is now expected to hike rates. This means mortgages and rents go up.

- **The war itself costs money.** The Pentagon spent $5.6 billion in munitions in the first two days alone. The first week cost $11.3 billion. The war is running at approximately $2 billion per day. A supplemental budget request is going to Congress. This money comes from taxpayers.

- **The Strategic Petroleum Reserve is being drained.** Trump released 172 million barrels. The reserve is now 59% full. This is the emergency fuel supply -- once it is gone, there is no backup.

## What happens next

### If the Strait reopens in the next two weeks

Oil settles at $85-95. Gas prices ease slowly. Fertiliser shipments resume but arrive late -- some crop damage is locked in. Food prices rise modestly (5-10%) for a few months, then stabilise. Markets recover. This is the best-case scenario, and it requires both sides to agree to stop fighting, which neither has shown willingness to do.

### If the Strait stays closed through March

Fertiliser for the 2026 Northern Hemisphere spring planting and Australian winter crop is permanently disrupted. Food prices rise 10-20% over the following months, lasting well into 2027 regardless of when the war ends. Oil stays above $100. Recession risk rises sharply in Europe, Japan, and trade-dependent economies. Australia faces formal fuel rationing.

### If the war lasts into the summer

Oxford Economics models show recessions in the Eurozone, UK, and Japan if oil stays at $140 for eight weeks. The US faces a "temporary standstill." Food inflation returns to 2022 levels (40-year highs). Central banks are forced to raise interest rates aggressively, hitting mortgage holders and renters simultaneously. The economic damage becomes structural -- not a spike but a new baseline.

## What you can do with this information

This guide is not advice. But understanding the chain of causation helps you make decisions:

- **The fuel price is not going back to pre-war levels soon.** Even if the war ends tomorrow, the Strait needs to be de-mined, shipping lanes need to be declared safe, and insurance companies need to agree to cover tankers again. That takes weeks to months.
- **Food prices will lag oil prices by 4-8 weeks.** The grocery price increases from the war have not fully arrived yet.
- **Interest rate cuts are off the table.** If you were counting on lower mortgage rates in 2026, the war has changed the calculus.
- **The people profiting from the war are not the people paying for it.** Oil companies, defence contractors, and fertiliser producers are posting record profits. Consumers are paying more for petrol, food, and housing. Ron Paul described this exact pattern in 2006: "The war will be paid for by hidden inflation that hits the poor and middle class hardest."

## The bottom line

House Speaker Mike Johnson called the gas price spike "temporary" and said the mission was "nearly completed." The Colbert show noted: before the war, gas was $2.98. On Day 14, it was $3.58. In California, it was over $5.

Trump's special envoy Steve Witkoff was asked on CNBC how the war ends. His answer: "I don't know."

The economic chain is simple: war closes the Strait → oil spikes → diesel and petrol spike → fertiliser stops shipping → farming gets more expensive → food prices rise → everything gets more expensive → your cost of living goes up.

Every link in this chain was predicted. Every link has now activated. The only question is how long the Strait stays closed, and nobody in the administration appears to have an answer.
