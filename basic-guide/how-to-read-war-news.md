# How to Read War News

A guide to critically reading wartime coverage, identifying propaganda, and understanding why the predictions in this archive keep coming true.

Last updated: 13 March 2026 (Day 14 of the war)

---

## Why this guide exists

This archive tracks predictions made by Ron Paul, Noam Chomsky, Dan Kovalik, Scott Ritter and Professor Jiang about the US-Israel war on Iran. An unusual number of those predictions have come true.

But one prediction stands apart from the rest, because it is not about missiles or oil prices. It is about *you* -- the person reading the news. Chomsky, Kovalik and Ritter all predicted that the war would be sold to the public using the same propaganda techniques that sold the Iraq war. They predicted that most people would not notice, because the techniques are designed to be invisible.

This guide makes them visible. It uses real examples from the first 14 days of this war to show how wartime propaganda works -- not in theory, but in practice, in the news you are reading right now.

---

## The five techniques

### 1. Say two things at once -- and let the audience pick the one they prefer

This is the most common technique and the easiest to miss. The government says contradictory things simultaneously. Each audience hears what it wants to hear. Nobody is technically "lying" because both statements were made.

**Example: Is this a regime-change war?**

| Date | Who | What they said |
|------|-----|----------------|
| Feb 28 | Trump | Urged Iranians to "seize control" of their destiny and overthrow clerical rule |
| Mar 2 | Hegseth | "This is not a so-called regime-change war... laser-focused" on missiles, navy, nuclear |
| Mar 5 | Trump | "I have to be involved in the appointment" of Iran's new leader |
| Day 14 | Senate (classified briefing) | DOD's war plan does not include regime change; "we are apparently very satisfied to leave in charge hardline leadership" |

Four statements, four different positions, all from the same administration in two weeks. If you support the war, you can cite Hegseth: "Not regime change, just protecting America." If you oppose it, you can cite Trump: "I have to be involved in the appointment." The administration has covered both bases.

**Example: Is the war ending?**

| Date | Who | What they said |
|------|-----|----------------|
| Mar 9 | Trump (CBS) | "I think the war is very complete" |
| Mar 9 | Hegseth | "This is only just the beginning" |
| Mar 12 | Trump (Axios) | War will end "soon" -- "practically nothing left to target" |
| Mar 12 | Trump (same day) | "We're not done... we're gonna go further" |

Michael Wolff, author of *Fire and Fury*: "Two completely different stories. One from the president, one from Hegseth." Trump told a Fox News reporter: "You said excursion and you said war -- which is it?" Trump replied: "It's both."

**How to spot it:** When you read a headline that says "Trump says war is nearly over," ask: did anyone in the administration say the opposite the same day? Almost always, the answer is yes.

### 2. Deny what is documented -- and wait for the news cycle to move on

When evidence of something damaging emerges, the technique is not to disprove it. It is to deny it, wait 48 hours, and let the next crisis push it out of the headlines.

**Example: The school strike**

On 28 February, a strike on a girls' school in Minab, southern Iran, killed 168 children and 14 teachers. The evidence that it was a US strike:

- Footage shows a Tomahawk cruise missile -- a weapon only the US possesses in this war (AP, CBS, BBC analysis)
- The Washington Post reported the school was on a US target list and may have been mistaken for a military site
- The New York Times analysed missile fragments at the site -- they resemble the markings of a US-made cruise missile
- The Pentagon's own preliminary assessment found the US was at fault

Trump's response evolved over two weeks:

| Date | What Trump said |
|------|----------------|
| Mar 1 | Blamed Iran's military for the bombing |
| Mar 7 | "In my opinion... that was done by Iran" |
| Mar 7 | When pressed on Tomahawk evidence: "Other countries might also have Tomahawks, including Iran" |
| Mar 9 | "Under investigation... willing to live with that report" |
| Day 14 | "I don't know about it" |

The Pentagon had told Congress there was no intelligence suggesting Iran was planning to attack first. CBS's Nancy Cordes asked press secretary Leavitt directly: "Is he making this up?" Leavitt's answer: "The president is not making anything up. This was a feeling the president had based on facts."

A *feeling* based on *facts* is not a fact. But by the time you have parsed the sentence, the press conference has moved on.

**How to spot it:** When a denial contradicts physical evidence (footage, fragments, Pentagon reports), the denial is not evidence. Track the original claim over days. Watch how it softens from "they did it" to "under investigation" to "I don't know about it."

### 3. Make the cost of the war invisible

Chomsky identified this in *Manufacturing Consent* (1988): the media reliably presents the victims of enemy states in vivid, humanising detail, while the victims of allied states are presented as statistics, abstractions, or not presented at all.

**Example: Casualty numbers**

The Pentagon says 7 US soldiers have been killed and 140 injured in 14 days. Iran says hundreds of US soldiers have been killed. The gap is enormous. Who is telling the truth?

The Daily Beast reported that Landstuhl Regional Medical Center in Germany has received over 30 service members with traumatic brain injuries, memory loss, shrapnel wounds, and burns from a single drone strike in Kuwait. Twenty arrived with injuries designated "urgent." Over 100 medical personnel were dispatched. The Pentagon had initially claimed only "5 seriously wounded, several minor shrapnel injuries."

Sgt. First Class Cory Hicks: "I remember turning my head to the left and I'd seen the nose of that drone pop through... everything was just smoke and fire and crazy and chaos."

Hegseth called the strike that killed six soldiers "a squirter."

A pro-Iran militia claims it shot down a KC-135 refueling aircraft in Iraq and killed all six crew. CENTCOM says the aircraft was "lost" but not due to hostile fire. The crew's status remains unknown.

None of these details leads the evening news. The Wii Sports war video -- showing real drone strike footage spliced with Nintendo bowling animations, with Iranian officials as bowling pins -- was posted the same day the Pentagon confirmed it killed 168 children in a school.

**How to spot it:** Ask what is NOT being shown. If a war video goes viral but the school strike is buried, the ratio tells you something. Count how many minutes the news spends on each topic. The allocation of attention *is* the editorial position.

### 4. Frame self-defence as aggression

Kovalik called this the "crime of self-defence." When the US attacks a country, that country's response is not described as self-defence. It is described as aggression, terrorism, or provocation -- as if the original attack never happened.

**Example: Iran's retaliatory strikes**

The US and Israel attacked Iran on 28 February. Iran responded by hitting military bases in countries hosting US forces. Here is how different actors described those responses:

| Who | How they described Iran's response |
|-----|------------------------------------|
| UAE government | "Brutal and unjustified Iranian aggression" |
| White House | Focused on Iranian retaliation; context of US first strike absent |
| Carolyn Leavitt | Listed Iran's destruction as a goal -- with no mention that the US struck first |
| Iran's FM Araghchi | "Operation Epic Mistake -- a misadventure engineered by Israel and paid for by ordinary Americans" |

Notice what is missing from the first three: any acknowledgment that the US started the war. The word "retaliation" implies a prior action. The word "aggression" implies Iran acted first. The framing erases causation.

Kovalik predicted this in 2018: "If Iranians or allies like the Houthis dare try to defend themselves from complete annihilation... this is cause to punish them even more."

**Example: The Houthis**

Yemen's Houthis have attacked US naval assets. In Western coverage, these are "Houthi attacks" -- acts of aggression by a terrorist group. The context -- that the US is bombing Iran, that Yemen has been devastated by a Saudi-led war supported by the US, that the Houthis view their actions as solidarity with a country under attack -- is either absent or buried in paragraph 15.

**How to spot it:** When you read about an "attack," ask: who attacked first? If the story begins with the retaliation and not the original strike, the framing has already done its work.

### 5. Create the illusion of inevitability

The most powerful propaganda technique is not lying. It is making the war seem like it had to happen -- that there was no alternative, no diplomatic option, no other path.

**Example: "Iran was about to attack"**

Trump claimed Iran was going to attack the US "within a week... 100 percent." Leavitt said this was "a feeling the president had based on facts." CBS asked whether any US or Israeli leader was making the same claim. Leavitt did not answer.

The Pentagon told Congress there was no intelligence suggesting Iran was planning to attack first.

Meanwhile, an Omani mediator said a peace deal was "within our reach" before the strikes began. Iran had agreed to never stockpile nuclear material for a bomb. The deal was rejected.

Iran's Foreign Minister Araghchi: the Trump administration's claim of a preemptive Iranian strike was "a sheer and utter lie... the sole purpose of that lie is to justify Operation Epic Mistake."

Chomsky described this pattern in 2007: the US offers impossible terms, the other side refuses, and the refusal is used as proof that war was the only option. Kovalik traced the same technique through the Brookings Institution's *Which Path to Persia?* paper, which described how to make a "superb offer" designed to be rejected, so that the US could claim "the Iranians brought it on themselves."

**How to spot it:** When someone says "we had no choice," ask: what was the offer? Who rejected it? Was there a peace deal on the table? Almost always, there was.

## The bigger patterns

### Humanitarian imperialism

Kovalik coined this term to describe how war is sold to liberals and progressives. The argument is not "we need their oil" or "we want regime change." The argument is "we need to save Iranian women from oppression" or "we need to bring democracy."

Kovalik's specific prediction from 2018: "Narratives like *Reading Lolita in Tehran* will convince liberals that bombing Iran is a feminist necessity to save women from 'endemic misogyny,' ignoring that US sanctions and bombs are what actually harm them."

On Day 14, the war has been framed with "freedom" and "security" language. Trump urged Iranian women to "seize control of their destiny." Netanyahu urged Iranians who protested their government to "rise up -- we are standing by your side." The same day, 168 children lay dead in a school the US bombed.

The test for humanitarian imperialism is simple: does the "humanitarian" concern exist only for countries the US wants to attack? Saudi Arabia -- a US ally -- has no elections, no free press, and severe restrictions on women's rights. It is not being bombed. Iran -- a US adversary -- has problems with human rights. It is being bombed. The concern tracks with strategic interest, not with actual concern for human beings.

Shirin Ebadi, the Iranian Nobel Peace Prize laureate, said it plainly: "Democracy cannot be brought to a people with cluster bombs."

### The media blackout

Kovalik predicted that the war would require "a total media blackout" of two facts:

1. **The IAEA and 16 US intelligence agencies concluded Iran halted its weapons program in 2003.** The 2003 and 2007 National Intelligence Estimates said so with "high confidence."

2. **Ayatollah Khamenei issued a fatwa forbidding the production, stockpiling, and use of nuclear weapons.**

On Day 14, search for either of these facts in mainstream war coverage. They are almost entirely absent. The war is being covered as if Iran's nuclear program is an established threat, when the US government's own intelligence community concluded otherwise -- twice. The fatwa is never mentioned. The NIE is never mentioned.

This is not a conspiracy. It is a structural outcome. Ritter explained the mechanism: "The IAEA facts are ignored when they are inconvenient." The facts exist in the public record. They are simply not selected for coverage, because they undermine the premise of the war. The coverage selects facts that support the premise and omits facts that contradict it. Over time, the audience concludes that the omitted facts do not exist.

### Censorship on both sides

Both sides censor. But the nature and purpose differ, and understanding the difference is part of reading critically.

**Israel's censorship apparatus:**

- Israel has a literal "censorship office" -- foreign journalists must submit reporting for review
- The IDF banned live broadcast of the Israeli skyline during air raids
- Filming of air defence interceptions (previously allowed) is now forbidden
- Ben-Gvir (far-right Minister of National Security) vowed "severity and zero tolerance" against international media; police dispatched; detentions and arrests made
- No foreign journalists have been allowed into Gaza for the entire war
- CNN journalist said live on air: "The Israeli government doesn't want us to show that"
- CNN runs a disclaimer: "CNN operates in Iran only with government permission" -- but the same restriction applies in Israel, and no equivalent disclaimer is shown

Israeli academic Ory Goldberg, writing from inside Israel: "The Israeli government heavily censors any news regarding casualties and deaths as well as actual missile hits. We also get regular lies about the number of missile launchers Iran and Hezbollah have and about Israeli success in taking such launchers out of action. Over the past few days, missiles and rockets have hit Israel with regularity that is being officially denied."

Diana Buttu, a Palestinian lawyer living in Haifa: "We're neither told what's happening in Iran, we're not told how long this is going to last, we're not told what the end goal is, and we're not told what's happening inside the country either."

**The US administration's information management:**

- Hegseth banned press photographers from Pentagon briefings, citing "unflattering" photos
- Energy Secretary Chris Wright posted that the US Navy had "successfully escorted an oil tanker through the Strait of Hormuz." The post was deleted within minutes when it emerged it never happened. Wall Street briefly rallied on the false claim, then reversed.
- Trump claimed Iran had Tomahawk cruise missiles. Iran does not.
- The White House posted a Wii Sports video splicing Nintendo bowling with real drone strike footage. The bowling pins were labelled "Iranian Regime Officials." A previous Call of Duty-styled video (since deleted) labelled real drone attack footage with "+100" XP point gains.

Cardinal Cupich of Chicago: "A real war with real death and real suffering being treated like it's a video game -- it's sickening."

**How to read through censorship:** Look for the gaps. When Israel says 80% of Iran's missile launchers are destroyed but 20 launches happen in a single day, the number and the reality contradict each other. When the US says a tanker was escorted through the Strait and deletes the post within minutes, the deletion is the story. When both sides censor, seek out sources that neither side controls -- independent journalists, leaked statements, satellite imagery, and the testimony of people on the ground.

### Suppressing dissent at home

Chomsky and Kovalik both predicted that foreign aggression would be accompanied by domestic repression. On Day 14, the evidence is visible:

- **Queensland, Australia:** Two protesters arrested on the first day of a law criminalising the phrase "From the river to the sea" -- one arrested moments after explaining the phrase to a journalist, another for wearing a shirt. Up to two years in prison. Israeli Foreign Minister Sa'ar praised the law and urged other Australian states to copy it.

- **Trump vs. Massie:** Trump travelled to the Kentucky district of Congressman Thomas Massie to campaign against him. Massie's offences: co-sponsoring the Epstein Files Transparency Act and voting for an Iran war powers resolution. The message: dissent from the war narrative is punished.

- **Hegseth vs. the press:** Photographers banned from Pentagon briefings. The Pentagon's own legal offices restructured mid-war.

- **ICE expansion:** $895 million spent on 10 "mass deportation warehouses" as part of a $38.3 billion plan. The Cato Institute's immigration director testified that DHS had "joked" about deporting 100 million people -- "I think that advocating 100 million deportations is ethnic cleansing."

The pattern: the same government that is bombing another country to "bring freedom" is simultaneously criminalising protest slogans, punishing anti-war legislators, banning press photographers, and building mass detention infrastructure at home.

## A practical checklist

When you read a war story, ask these questions:

**1. Who struck first?** Does the story begin with the retaliation or the original attack? If it starts with Iran's response and not the US/Israeli first strike, the framing has removed causation.

**2. What is missing?** Are the NIE and the Khamenei fatwa mentioned? Are civilian casualties on both sides given equal weight? Is the Omani peace deal mentioned? The absences are as important as the presences.

**3. Who is speaking?** Is the source a government spokesperson, an anonymous official, or an independent journalist? Government sources have institutional incentives to frame events in a particular way. That does not mean they are always wrong -- but it means you should look for corroboration.

**4. Does the headline match the body?** Headlines are written to be clicked. The body often contains qualifications, contradictions, or context that the headline omits. Read the whole article.

**5. Is the same claim made on both sides?** Israel claims 80% of launchers destroyed; Iran launches 20 missiles in a day. The US claims 16 minelayers destroyed; Iran retains 80% of minelaying capacity. When both sides make claims that contradict observable reality, both claims need scrutiny.

**6. What happened to last week's story?** The school strike killed 168 children. The Pentagon confirmed the US was responsible. Has the story been followed up, or has it been replaced by the next crisis? Stories that disappear are often the most important ones.

**7. Who profits?** Sen. Markwayne Mullin (Trump's pick for DHS head) made profitable purchases in oil and security company stocks days before the strikes. Trump's sons have investments in a drone company. When officials who start wars also profit from them, the conflict of interest is the story.

**8. Is the language precise or emotional?** "Strikes on military targets" is precise. "Brutal and unjustified aggression" is emotional. "Operation Epic Fury" is branding. The more emotional the language, the more likely it is designed to short-circuit your analysis.

## Why the predictions keep coming true

The predictions in this archive are not prophecy. They are pattern recognition. Chomsky reads declassified documents. Ritter reads weapons reports. Kovalik reads policy papers. Jiang reads game theory. Paul reads the public statements of the people who advocate for war and takes them at their word.

The reason their predictions keep coming true is that the techniques do not change. The Iraq war was sold with manufactured intelligence, humanitarian rhetoric, suppressed contrary evidence, and the framing of self-defence as aggression. The Iran war is being sold with the same techniques, by the same institutions, using the same words.

Kovalik wrote in 2018: "Regime change disguised as disarmament." The JCPOA was stripped. The NIE was suppressed. The fatwa was ignored. The war was launched to "prevent" something that was not happening. The playbook does not change because it does not need to. It works.

The only defence against a playbook that works is knowing what it looks like. That is what this guide is for.

## Chomsky's method -- in one sentence

"Compare what governments say with what they actually do, and read the documents they publish."

Everything in this guide follows from that sentence. The documents are public. The contradictions are on the record. The pattern is visible. The question is whether you look.
