# March 2026: A Layperson's Guide

What is happening, how we got here, and what is likely to happen next. Written in plain language for people who have not been following the war closely.

Last updated: 13 March 2026 (Day 14)

---

## What happened

On 28 February 2026, the United States and Israel attacked Iran. They called it Operation Epic Fury. The opening strike killed Iran's 86-year-old supreme leader, Ayatollah Khamenei. His son Mojtaba was named successor.

Iran hit back. It fired missiles and drones at every country in the region that hosts US military bases. It closed the Strait of Hormuz -- the narrow waterway that carries 20% of the world's oil. It laid thousands of mines in the water. It declared: "Not a liter of oil through the Strait."

Two weeks later, the war has spread to over a dozen countries. Oil prices have doubled. Grocery and fuel prices are rising on every continent. Nobody in the US administration can explain how the war ends.

---

## How we got here (the short version)

The full history goes back to 1953, when the CIA overthrew Iran's democracy and installed a dictator (see `HISTORY-101.md`). The short version:

- **2015:** Iran signed a deal (the JCPOA) limiting its nuclear program in exchange for sanctions relief. It worked. Iran complied.
- **2018:** Trump withdrew from the deal anyway.
- **2020:** The US assassinated Iran's top general, Qasem Soleimani, in Baghdad.
- **2024:** Trump won re-election. Professor Jiang, a high school teacher who had been predicting this war for two years, said: "If Trump wins, he will start a war with Iran."
- **February 2026:** An Omani mediator said a peace deal was "within reach." Iran had agreed to never build a bomb. The US attacked anyway.

There was no evidence Iran was about to attack the US. The Pentagon told Congress so. The CIA confirmed Iran had no nuclear weapon. The war was launched on a justification that the government's own intelligence agencies said was false.

## What is happening by region

### The Middle East

**Iran** absorbed the strike that killed its supreme leader without collapsing. The military had pre-planned for exactly this scenario. Iran's strategy is not to match American firepower. It is to make the war so expensive that America cannot sustain it. Cheap drones ($50,000 each) against expensive interceptors ($1-13 million each). Iran destroyed a $300 million US radar system. It claims it hit the USS Abraham Lincoln aircraft carrier. It is exporting more oil now than before the war -- almost all of it to China.

**Israel** co-launched the war and says it has killed top Iranian nuclear scientists. Netanyahu said Israel is "becoming stronger than ever." But Iranian missiles keep arriving -- 20 launches in one day despite Israel claiming it destroyed 80% of Iran's launchers. The cabinet has been forced into bunkers. Israel is spending 30% of its entire budget on defence. It has moved troops from Gaza to the Lebanon border.

**Lebanon** has been devastated. Over 630 killed (91+ children). 816,000+ displaced. Double-tap strikes on a beachfront tent camp at 3am. A Red Cross volunteer was killed while evacuating wounded. Lebanon's prime minister said the country has been "dragged into a war it never wanted."

**Iraq** has become a secondary battlefield. US bases -- including Victory Base near Baghdad airport -- are under drone attack "every single night." A US refuelling aircraft crashed. French soldiers were wounded and one was killed. Iran-aligned militias are explicitly targeting anyone associated with the war.

**The Gulf states** (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman) did not choose this war but are paying for it. All six have been hit by Iranian strikes. Bahrain has imported anti-riot troops from Jordan to suppress protests -- the first time since 2011. Citi and HSBC are pulling out. Dubai's airport was hit. Kuwait's airport was hit. Oman's oil terminals are shut. The rich are fleeing on private jets ($250,000 one-way). The workers who run these countries are trying to leave.

**Yemen's Houthis** are attacking US naval assets in the Red Sea, threatening the alternative oil route around the Strait of Hormuz.

### The United States

The war is costing $2 billion a day. Gas is at the highest price of either Trump term. The Dow crashed 750 points on Day 14. The Strategic Petroleum Reserve (America's emergency fuel supply) has been tapped -- it is now only 59% full.

Trump is saying contradictory things -- sometimes on the same day. "We won" and "we're not finished." "Excursion" and "war." He blamed his own advisers (Kushner, Miller, Hegseth, Rubio) for the decision to attack. His special envoy was asked on live TV how the war ends. His answer: "I don't know."

The Pentagon confirmed the US bombed a girls' school on the first day, killing 168 children and 14 teachers. Trump said: "I don't know about it." The White House posted a Wii Sports video splicing Nintendo bowling with real drone strike footage. Chicago's Cardinal Cupich called it "sickening."

A classified Senate briefing revealed that the Pentagon's own war plan does not include regime change, does not include destroying Iran's nuclear program, and has no plan to reopen the Strait of Hormuz. Every stated public goal of the war is contradicted by the military's own internal assessment.

The American Farm Bureau has warned the White House that fertiliser disruptions could cause food price spikes "not seen since 2022."

### Europe

**France** lost its first soldier on Day 14 -- killed by a drone in Iraq. Six others were wounded. President Macron said: "The war in Iran cannot justify such attacks." France is now a combatant whether it intended to be or not.

**Germany** is backing the war. Its foreign minister visited Israel and said "the greatest danger comes from the Iranian regime."

**Italy** broke ranks. Prime Minister Meloni publicly criticised the war as a "dangerous trend" -- the first major European ally to do so.

**Spain** removed its ambassador to Israel.

**The European Union** is releasing emergency oil reserves -- the largest such release in IEA history (400 million barrels). European economies face recession if oil stays above $140 for eight weeks.

**Russia** is the sole strategic winner. Higher oil prices mean more Russian revenue. Western attention has shifted from Ukraine. Western arms stockpiles are being depleted. Russia is selling oil to fill the gap left by the Gulf shutdown. A Russian tanker conducted a $29 million oil transfer in Omani waters under cover of the war's chaos.

### Asia-Pacific

**China** is buying 90% of Iran's oil -- shipped through the "closed" Strait of Hormuz while everyone else is blocked. It has halted fuel exports to the West. It is in talks with Iran for safe passage of Chinese vessels. A Trump-Xi summit is scheduled for late March in Beijing, but the war has weakened Trump's leverage.

**South Korea** has lost 48 THAAD missile interceptors, transferred to the Middle East. Its president had to publicly reassure citizens that defences against North Korea "remain sufficient." They are watching nervously.

**Japan** has released emergency oil reserves and imposed gasoline price caps -- measures not taken since major international crises.

**India** is buying Russian oil to replace Gulf supplies. Officials said India "doesn't need permission" for energy decisions. Only 40% of India's crude transits the Strait.

**New Zealand** faces petrol heading toward $3/litre. Fertiliser prices have roughly doubled. Air New Zealand shares dropped 8%. The Treasury says if the war lasts three months, inflation rises up to 1 percentage point and GDP falls. BNZ called it "the worst of all worlds for the average New Zealander."

### Australia

Australia was one of the first countries to endorse the strikes. It sent a Wedgetail surveillance aircraft and 85 military personnel to the UAE. It has shut three embassies (Abu Dhabi, Tel Aviv, Dubai consulate).

The fuel crisis is the worst in decades. Distributors report a 90% reduction in supply. Farmers are turning off machinery. Fishing wharves are empty. Towns are running out of diesel. The government lowered fuel quality standards and released 762 million litres from reserves.

Fertiliser (urea) has surged from A$830 to A$1,195 per tonne. Australia imports 64% of its urea from the Gulf. The peak demand season for winter crops is weeks away.

Queensland arrested two protesters on the first day of a new law criminalising the phrase "From the river to the sea." One was arrested moments after explaining the phrase to a journalist. Israel's foreign minister praised the law and urged other states to copy it.

### Canada

Prime Minister Carney said Canada will "never participate in any kind of offensive operation against Iran." But a Canadian military camp in Kuwait was damaged by an Iranian missile on March 1 -- and the government did not tell the public until the press found out. Canada is boosting Arctic defences, saying it "can no longer rely on others."

## What is likely to happen for the rest of March

### The most likely outcome: a messy, face-saving exit (50%)

Trump is already saying the war is "very complete" while simultaneously saying "we're not finished." This is the pattern -- declare victory, find a way out, and move on. The problem: Iran is not cooperating. The Strait is still closed. The mines are still in the water. Iran demands a "permanent end to war," not a temporary ceasefire.

The economic pressure will push toward an exit. The Dow crash, the Farm Bureau warning, gas prices at $5 in California, and the approaching supplemental budget vote in Congress all create urgency. But "declaring victory" while Iran fires 20 missiles a day at Israel is hard to sell.

### The second possibility: a negotiated deal through back channels (35%)

China is in talks with Iran about safe passage for Chinese vessels. Multiple back channels are active. The economic pain on both sides creates incentive. But the positions are far apart -- Trump wants "unconditional surrender," Iran wants a "permanent end to war." Someone has to blink.

### The danger: escalation (15%)

The administration is considering sending special forces into Iran to secure nuclear material. Senator Blumenthal said: "We seem to be on a path toward deploying American troops on the ground." If ground troops go in, Jiang's prediction activates: they will be trapped in a mountainous country of 90 million people. Iran has been preparing for exactly this scenario for 20 years.

Iran has also hinted at closing a second strait (Bab el-Mandeb, near Yemen). If both chokepoints close, there is no alternative route for Gulf oil. That would push oil toward $200 and trigger recessions across Europe and Asia.

### The things to watch

- **The Strait.** If it reopens, the worst is over. If it stays closed through March, the food and fuel crises become permanent regardless of when the war ends.
- **Ground troops.** If special forces deploy into Iran, the war transforms from an air campaign into something much worse.
- **The budget vote.** Congress has to vote on funding the war. This is the first real opportunity for democratic accountability.
- **Oil prices.** If Brent crude hits $140 and stays there for eight weeks, Oxford Economics models show recessions in Europe, Japan, and the UK.
- **France.** A French soldier was killed on Day 14. Macron must decide: go deeper or pull out.
- **South Korea.** The US took missile defence systems from Korea and sent them to the Gulf. If North Korea tests a missile during the gap, the crisis doubles.

## The bottom line

The war was launched to "prevent Iran from getting a nuclear weapon." Iran did not have a nuclear weapon. The CIA said so. The IAEA said so. Sixteen US intelligence agencies said so. The Pentagon's own briefings say destroying the nuclear program is "not a goal of the war plan."

The people who predicted this war -- a congressman, a professor, a weapons inspector, a lawyer, and a high school teacher -- were ignored. The people who launched it cannot explain how it ends.

Meanwhile, the cost falls on everyone else. Farmers running out of diesel. Families paying $5 for gas. Children killed in a school the Pentagon mistook for a military site. Fishermen whose wharves have no fuel. Workers who say: "We do not want to be a part of this war."

The predictions came true because the pattern has not changed. The same country that overthrew Iran's democracy in 1953, armed Saddam in the 1980s, invaded Iraq on false pretences in 2003, and withdrew from a working nuclear deal in 2018 has now launched a war on the same country, for the same stated reasons, using the same playbook. The results are what everyone said they would be.

The only question left is how it ends, and nobody in charge appears to have an answer.
