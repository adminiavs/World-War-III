# The Analysts: Who They Are and Why They Got It Right

This archive centres the predictions of five people: Ron Paul, Noam Chomsky, Scott Ritter, Dan Kovalik, and Professor Jiang. A reasonable question is: who are these people, and why should I take their predictions seriously?

This guide answers both questions.

Last updated: 13 March 2026 (Day 14 of the war)

---

## Ron Paul

### Who he is

Ron Paul (born 1935) is a retired American politician. He served as a Republican congressman from Texas for twelve terms across three periods (1976-1977, 1979-1985, 1997-2013). He is also an obstetrician by training -- he delivered more than 4,000 babies before entering politics.

Paul ran for president three times: as the Libertarian Party candidate in 1988, and as a Republican in 2008 and 2012. He never won, but he built a large grassroots following, particularly among young voters and people sceptical of US foreign policy.

### His political position

Paul is a libertarian -- he believes in minimal government, free markets, individual liberty, and a non-interventionist foreign policy. He opposes most government programs, including the Federal Reserve, the income tax, the war on drugs, and most US military operations abroad. He has been called "the intellectual godfather of the Tea Party" and later of parts of the populist right.

Importantly, Paul is not a progressive or a leftist. He is a conservative Republican who opposes war from the right -- from the principle that the Constitution does not authorise the federal government to wage wars of choice, and that empire abroad destroys liberty at home.

### Why he is in this archive

On 6 April 2006 -- twenty years before Operation Epic Fury -- Paul gave a speech to the US House of Representatives titled "Iran: The Next Neocon Target." It is one of the most prescient speeches in congressional history.

In 20 minutes, Paul predicted:

- The US would attack Iran using the same playbook as Iraq (WMD claims, regime change, pre-emptive war)
- Oil prices would spike to $120 per barrel or higher
- The Strait of Hormuz would be disrupted
- The war would be paid for by printing money, creating hidden inflation "that hits the poor and middle class hardest"
- Congress would fail to stop it, even though only Congress has the constitutional power to declare war
- The war would backfire in ways nobody expected, worse than Iraq

Every one of these predictions has come true. Oil hit $120 on Day 6. The Strait collapsed from 138 transits per day to roughly one. Gas went from $2.98 to $3.58 nationally and over $5 in California. The Senate War Powers vote failed. The war has spread to over a dozen countries.

### How he knew

Paul did not have secret intelligence. He read the public statements of the people who were advocating for war -- the neoconservative think tanks, the speeches of Bush administration officials, the congressional testimony -- and took them at their word. His method was simple: when powerful people tell you what they plan to do, believe them.

### Caveats

Paul's libertarian views on domestic policy (opposing the Civil Rights Act, Social Security, the EPA) are controversial and have nothing to do with his foreign policy predictions. His son, Senator Rand Paul, has taken some anti-war positions but has not been as consistent. Paul's strength in this archive is narrow and specific: he read the neoconservative playbook correctly and warned about it two decades early. That does not mean his views on other subjects are correct.

## Noam Chomsky

### Who he is

Noam Chomsky (born 1928) is an American linguist, philosopher, and political commentator. He is Institute Professor Emeritus at MIT, where he taught for over 50 years, and Laureate Professor at the University of Arizona.

In linguistics, Chomsky is one of the most important figures in the history of the field. He revolutionised the study of language with his theory of universal grammar -- the idea that the capacity for language is built into the human brain. He has been called "the father of modern linguistics." He is one of the most cited scholars in any field, alive or dead.

In politics, Chomsky has been the most prominent American critic of US foreign policy for over sixty years, starting with his opposition to the Vietnam War in the 1960s. He has written more than 100 books. His political writing is not opinion or ideology -- it is based on reading declassified documents, government reports, and the public record, and comparing what governments say with what they do.

### His political position

Chomsky describes himself as a libertarian socialist -- someone who believes in individual freedom, opposes concentrated power (both government and corporate), and supports democratic control of workplaces and institutions. He is sharply critical of both US foreign policy and of authoritarian regimes, including Iran's.

He is not "pro-Iran." He has repeatedly criticised Iran's government for its treatment of women, dissidents, and minorities. His point is different: that the US does not attack Iran because of human rights concerns (the US supports Saudi Arabia, which has a worse human rights record) but because Iran refuses to follow American orders.

### Why he is in this archive

Chomsky made his Iran predictions in two books published in 2006-2007: *What We Say Goes* (with David Barsamian, 2007) and *Targeting Iran* (with Barsamian, Ervand Abrahamian, and Nahid Mozaffari, 2007). His predictions include:

- The US and Iran were on a "collision course" following the exact Iraq pattern
- Attacking Iran would strengthen hardliners, not weaken them -- "when you bomb a country, the people rally around whoever is in charge"
- Iran would be pushed toward Russia and China, creating the kind of anti-American alliance US policy was supposedly trying to prevent
- The oil shock would cause a fertiliser shortage, which would cause a food crisis -- because fertiliser is made from oil and gas and much of it transits the Strait of Hormuz
- European allies would be dragged in despite opposing the war
- Propaganda and fear would override common sense, as they did with Iraq's fictional WMD

All of these have come true. Mojtaba Khamenei was chosen as successor, described by the Senate's own DOD briefings as "more radical, more anti-American than the one we killed." Russia is providing intelligence; China is buying 90% of Iranian oil. Fertiliser prices have surged to A$1,195/tonne in Australia; the American Farm Bureau has warned the White House of food supply disruptions. France has lost a soldier in Iraq. The nuclear justification for the war has been contradicted by the Pentagon's own briefings.

### His method

Chomsky's method is the backbone of this archive: "Compare what governments say with what they actually do, and read the documents they publish."

He does not predict the future through ideology. He reads the institutional incentives, the historical pattern, and the public record. When the US has done the same thing five times in a row (Guatemala 1954, Chile 1973, Iraq 2003, Libya 2011, Syria), predicting it will do it a sixth time is not prophecy. It is pattern recognition.

### Caveats

Chomsky is 97 years old and his recent public statements have been limited. His predictions in this archive are from 2006-2007 -- they are nearly twenty years old. Some critics argue his framework is too US-centric and does not adequately account for the agency of other states (Iran, Israel, Russia, China). Others argue he underestimates the role of ideology and religion in driving events. His strength is institutional analysis -- understanding how large systems (governments, militaries, media) behave based on their structural incentives. Where this archive uses Chomsky, it uses him for that specific strength.

## Scott Ritter

### Who he is

Scott Ritter (born 1961) is a former US Marine Corps intelligence officer and former United Nations weapons inspector. From 1991 to 1998, he served as a chief inspector for the UN Special Commission (UNSCOM) in Iraq, charged with verifying the dismantlement of Iraq's weapons of mass destruction.

Ritter resigned from UNSCOM in 1998, accusing the Clinton administration of undermining the inspections process by using it for espionage. He then became one of the loudest voices warning that Iraq did not have WMD -- a position that was vindicated after the 2003 invasion found nothing.

### His political position

Ritter does not fit neatly into left or right. He is a former Marine who opposes war from an intelligence and evidence standpoint. His argument is not ideological -- it is technical: the evidence does not support the claims being made, and he knows this because he was personally involved in the inspections process.

### Why he is in this archive

In 2006, Ritter published *Target Iran: The Truth About the White House's Plans for Regime Change*. The book argued:

- There was no evidence of an Iranian nuclear weapons program -- the IAEA found safeguard breaches but no weapons program; HEU/LEU traces were explained by contamination from imported equipment
- The "Iran crisis" was "made in Israel" -- manufactured to serve Israeli strategic interests using the same playbook as Iraq
- The path the US was on regarding Iran would "inevitably lead to war" and "make the historical mistake in Iraq pale by comparison"
- The NCRI (an MEK front) was being used to launder fabricated intelligence about Iran's nuclear program, just as Ahmed Chalabi's Iraqi National Congress had laundered false intelligence about Iraqi WMD
- A US attack on Iran could lead to oil at $150-200 per barrel

Ritter's technical credibility as a weapons inspector gives his nuclear analysis particular weight. When he says the IAEA found no weapons program, he is speaking from direct professional experience with the inspections process.

### Caveats

This must be stated clearly: Ritter was convicted of criminal conduct in 2011 (arising from a 2009 online sex sting operation) and served time in prison. He was released in 2014.

Since his release, Ritter has contributed to Russian state media outlets (RT, Sputnik). His commentary on the Russia-Ukraine war has been sharply criticised as pro-Russian, and he was arrested for travelling to Russia in violation of the terms of his release.

These facts are relevant to evaluating Ritter's credibility as a current commentator. They do not retroactively invalidate his 2006 book, which was written before these events, was treated as a serious source at the time of publication (e.g. Democracy Now! with Seymour Hersh), and made arguments about Iran's nuclear program that were later confirmed by the IAEA and the US National Intelligence Estimate.

This archive uses Ritter for his 2006 analysis -- his technical expertise on weapons inspections and his identification of the Iraq-to-Iran playbook. His later personal record and media affiliations are noted here so readers can make informed judgments.

## Dan Kovalik

### Who he is

Dan Kovalik is an American human rights lawyer and author. He teaches international human rights law at the University of Pittsburgh School of Law. He has served as a senior attorney for the United Steelworkers union. His legal work has focused on labour rights, US foreign policy in Latin America, and the human costs of US sanctions and military intervention.

Kovalik has represented victims of political violence in Colombia and has been involved in cases concerning US complicity in human rights abuses abroad. He has written several books on US foreign policy, including *The Plot to Scapegoat Russia* (2017) and *The Plot to Attack Iran* (2018).

### His political position

Kovalik is on the political left. He is critical of US imperialism, corporate power, and the military-industrial complex. He is sympathetic to countries that resist US dominance (Cuba, Venezuela, Iran), which some critics argue makes him insufficiently critical of those countries' own human rights abuses.

He would respond that his focus is on the country that is doing the bombing -- the US -- because as an American citizen and lawyer, that is where his responsibility lies. This is consistent with Chomsky's position: "I concentrate on the United States because that is where I live. I can potentially influence US policy. I cannot influence Iranian policy."

### Why he is in this archive

In 2018, Kovalik published *The Plot to Attack Iran: How the CIA and the Deep State Have Conspired to Vilify Iran*. The book made more than 30 specific predictions about how and why the US would attack Iran. By Day 14 of the war, 18 have been directly confirmed, another 10 are actively tracking, and none have been contradicted. It is the single most accurate predictive document in this archive.

Key predictions that came true:

- **The JCPOA was a "Trojan Horse":** The nuclear deal would strip Iran's deterrent, the US would withdraw, and then attack. This is exactly what happened.
- **Same playbook as Iraq:** Manufactured intelligence, suppressed contrary evidence (NIE, Khamenei fatwa), media narrative pushing false WMD claims.
- **Proxy-first strategy:** The US would dismantle Iran's allies (Hezbollah, Syria) through proxy wars before attacking Iran directly.
- **Bolton and Pompeo's appointments guaranteed war:** Their placement meant diplomacy was abandoned.
- **Humanitarian imperialism:** The war would be sold to liberals using "freedom" and "women's rights" rhetoric.
- **"Crime of self-defence":** Iran's retaliatory strikes would be framed as "unprovoked aggression."
- **No democracy would be installed:** The US would leave Iran in ruins (the Libya model) or install a dictator.
- **Strengthening hardliners:** US hostility would empower exactly the people it claimed to oppose.

### His method

Kovalik reads policy papers. The most important is the Brookings Institution's *Which Path to Persia?* (2009), which Kovalik describes as a literal blueprint for regime change. Brookings proposed offering Iran a "superb offer" designed to be rejected, so the US could claim "the Iranians brought it on themselves." Kovalik also traces the continuity of regime-change planning across administrations -- Bush, Obama, Trump -- arguing that the "plot" is institutional, not personal.

He also draws extensively on historical parallels: the 1953 coup, Chile 1973, Libya 2011. His argument is that the US follows the same steps every time, and recognising the pattern allows prediction.

### Caveats

Kovalik's writing can be conspiratorial in tone -- terms like "the Deep State" and "plot" may alienate readers who prefer more restrained language. His framework assumes a high degree of conscious coordination among US policymakers, which some analysts consider overstated (institutional inertia and path dependence may explain the same outcomes without requiring a "plot").

His sympathy for governments that resist US power (Russia, Cuba, Iran) means he sometimes understates internal problems in those countries. However, his specific predictions about the mechanics of the Iran war have been remarkably accurate, regardless of his broader political orientation.

## Professor Jiang (Jiang Xueqin)

### Who he is

Professor Jiang is a high school teacher who teaches history, game theory, and geopolitics. He has lived and worked in Canada and appears to have connections to both North American and East Asian academic environments. His lectures were recorded by students and uploaded to YouTube on channels called "Predictive History" and "Contrarian History Thesis."

He is not a former intelligence officer, a senator, a tenured professor at MIT, or a weapons inspector. He is a high school teacher who applied game theory to geopolitics and got the war right in extraordinary detail.

### His political position

Jiang is harder to categorise politically than the others. He is not ideological in the way Chomsky (left) or Paul (libertarian) are. His analysis is closer to what international relations scholars call "realism" -- he examines what states do based on their interests, capabilities, and constraints, without moral judgment about who is "right."

He is critical of the US war but frames it as a strategic blunder rather than a moral crime. He is interested in Iran's strategy without endorsing Iran's government. He teaches his students to think about geopolitics the way a chess player thinks about the board: every player is rational, every player has goals, and the outcome depends on how their moves interact.

### Why he is in this archive

Jiang began teaching about a potential Iran war roughly two years before it happened. His lectures, uploaded to YouTube starting in April 2024, contain predictions that are almost eerie in their accuracy:

**Two years before the war (April 2024):**
- If Trump wins the 2024 election, he will start a war with Iran
- Iran fights using asymmetric warfare: cheap drones against expensive interceptors. A $50,000 drone against a $1-13 million interceptor means Iran wins the economics of war
- The Gulf states look rich but are structurally fragile -- desalination plants, imported food, expatriate workforces
- Operation True Promise was designed to achieve four strategic goals simultaneously (unite population, build alliances, win global opinion, weaken the enemy)
- The 2002 Millennium Challenge proved Iran could defeat the US in a simulation; the US responded by banning asymmetric tactics and running it again

**One year before the war (March 2025):**
- The war will be given a patriotic name (he guessed "Operation Iranian Freedom")
- It will be justified with five specific arguments: nuclear threat, protecting allies, protecting shipping, fighting terrorism, bringing democracy
- The coalition will include the UK, Israel, Saudi Arabia, Australia, and the UAE
- Iran has distributed command across 31 centres so decapitation cannot stop the war
- When Khamenei dies, his son Mojtaba will be chosen as successor because the IRGC prefers him
- If the US sends ground troops, they will be trapped -- Iran is mountainous and 90 million strong
- The Iranian people will not welcome American soldiers

**During the war (March 2026):**
- Iran is waging war on the global economy by targeting GCC infrastructure and closing Hormuz
- US military is built for the Cold War, not 21st-century asymmetric conflict
- Million-dollar interceptors vs $50,000 drones is unsustainable
- GCC countries are "basically finished" as safe havens for investment and expatriates
- Regime change has never been achieved from the air alone; pressure will build for ground troops

### His method

Game theory. Jiang's approach is mathematical: identify each player's interests, map their available moves, calculate the likely outcomes. He does not ask "who is morally right?" He asks "what will each player do, given their incentives?" This is why his predictions are so specific -- he is not making moral arguments, he is solving strategy problems.

He also uses historical analogy: the 2002 Millennium Challenge (where Iran won a simulated war), Vietnam (where asymmetric warfare defeated a superior military), and the British Empire's overextension. He teaches his students that empires fall through three mechanisms: overextension, debt, and civil unrest -- and argues that the US is experiencing all three simultaneously.

### Caveats

Jiang is a high school teacher. He does not have the academic credentials of Chomsky, the legal credentials of Kovalik, or the intelligence community credentials of Ritter. Some of his lectures include speculative material (eschatology, secret societies, conspiracy theories) alongside rigorous game-theory analysis. Not everything he says is equally well-sourced.

His strength is the game-theory framework -- the structural analysis of why each player acts the way they do. When he sticks to this framework, his accuracy is remarkable. When he ventures into eschatological speculation or theories about secret societies, the quality of analysis drops. This archive primarily uses Jiang for his game-theory predictions and his analysis of asymmetric warfare, which have been confirmed by events.

He is also -- uniquely in this archive -- a teacher. His lectures are designed for students, not academics. They are accessible, concrete, and built around examples. This makes them unusually useful for newcomers trying to understand the war.

## Why these five -- and not others

A reasonable objection: why centre these five voices rather than, say, the CIA, the Pentagon, or mainstream foreign policy experts?

The answer is in the results.

| Source | What they predicted | What happened |
|--------|-------------------|---------------|
| CIA | Iranians would welcome American intervention | No uprising; regime consolidated |
| Pentagon planners | Quick, decisive campaign | Multi-front war still escalating at Day 14 |
| Administration | Killing the supreme leader would break the regime | Mojtaba chosen; hardliners strengthened |
| Paul (2006) | Oil to $120, Hormuz disrupted, Congress won't stop it | All confirmed |
| Chomsky (2007) | Collision course, hardliners strengthened, oil-fertiliser-food chain | All confirmed |
| Ritter (2006) | No nuclear weapons program, war inevitable, crisis "made in Israel" | All confirmed |
| Kovalik (2018) | JCPOA trap, same Iraq playbook, humanitarian imperialism, crime of self-defence | 18/30 confirmed, 10 tracking, 0 wrong |
| Jiang (2024) | Asymmetric warfare, GCC fragility, Mojtaba succession, interceptor economics | All confirmed |

The people who were paid to get it right -- intelligence agencies, Pentagon strategists, administration officials -- got it wrong. The people who were ignored, marginalised, or unknown -- a congressman, a linguist, a weapons inspector, a lawyer, and a high school teacher -- got it right.

This does not mean everything these five say is correct. It means their frameworks for understanding power -- institutional analysis (Chomsky), constitutional originalism (Paul), weapons expertise (Ritter), policy paper analysis (Kovalik), and game theory (Jiang) -- produced better predictions than the frameworks used by the people in charge.

The lesson is not "trust these five people on everything." The lesson is: when the official story does not match reality, look for the people who are reading the documents, tracing the patterns, and asking the questions that the people in power do not want asked.

## How to read them together

Each analyst brings a different lens:

| Analyst | Lens | Strongest on |
|---------|------|-------------|
| **Paul** | Constitutional / libertarian | Economic costs, congressional failure, inflation as hidden war tax |
| **Chomsky** | Institutional / structural | Why states behave the way they do, propaganda, historical patterns |
| **Ritter** | Technical / evidentiary | Nuclear claims, weapons inspections, intelligence fabrication |
| **Kovalik** | Legal / policy | The deliberate "plot" -- policy papers, appointments, the JCPOA trap |
| **Jiang** | Game theory / strategic | Asymmetric warfare, interceptor economics, GCC fragility, player incentives |

Read together, they cover the full picture: Paul tells you the economic cost, Chomsky tells you the institutional logic, Ritter tells you the nuclear evidence is fabricated, Kovalik tells you the policy papers that laid out the plan, and Jiang tells you how the military dynamics will play out.

No single analyst has the complete picture. Together, they predicted the war with a level of accuracy that should be deeply uncomfortable for anyone who trusted the official sources instead.
