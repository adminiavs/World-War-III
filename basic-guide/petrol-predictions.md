# Petrol Predictions: Fuel Prices by Region

A living tracker of what is happening with petrol, diesel, jet fuel, and LNG around the world — where things stand now, what is likely to happen in the next week, next month, next six months, and what a year-long war would mean.

**Last updated:** 16 March 2026 (Day 17 of the war)

---

## The core mechanism (read this first)

Before the war, the Strait of Hormuz carried approximately 20 million barrels of oil per day — roughly 20% of the world's supply and most of the world's LNG. On 28 February 2026, the US and Israel attacked Iran. Iran's IRGC responded by closing the Strait to non-Iranian vessels. An IRGC spokesman warned: "Not a liter of oil through the Hormuz Strait. Expect oil at $200 per barrel."

The Strait has been closed for 17 days. Everything on this page flows from that fact.

**Key numbers as of 16 March 2026:**

| Metric | Pre-war | Peak (Day 6) | Day 17 |
|--------|---------|--------------|--------|
| Brent crude (per barrel) | ~$68 | ~$120 | ~$90–100 (IEA reserves suppressing) |
| Daily tankers through Hormuz | ~138 | ~1 | ~1–3 (shadow fleet only) |
| IEA emergency reserves released | — | — | 400 million barrels (double the 2022 record) |
| US Strategic Petroleum Reserve | 100% | — | 59% full |
| Iran oil exports (shadow fleet) | 2.0M bbl/day | — | 2.1M bbl/day (to China) |
| Gulf oil production cut | — | — | Iraq -60%; Kuwait, UAE -20–30% |

**Two new threats as of Day 16 that are not yet priced in:**

1. **Houthis** have announced they will enter the war and close Bab-el-Mandeb (the other major oil chokepoint) when oil hits $120–200. If both Hormuz and Bab-el-Mandeb close simultaneously, approximately 30% of global oil supply is blocked.

2. **Iran's yuan-for-tankers offer**: Iran offered limited Hormuz passage but only for transactions denominated in Chinese yuan — a direct attack on the US petrodollar system. If accepted, this is the first wartime weaponization of currency against the US in a major conflict.

### 1. United States

#### What happened
- Pre-war gas: $2.98/gallon national average
- Day 14: $3.58/gallon national average; $5+ in California
- Trump released 172 million barrels from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR), which is now at 59% capacity
- Kevin Hassett (White House): US has spent $12 billion on the war so far; briefed 4–6 weeks from start
- Trump called rising oil prices "a very small price to pay" and a "good thing" because "the US is the world's largest oil producer"
- The American Farm Bureau Federation wrote to the White House warning of food supply disruptions "not seen since 2022, when food price inflation reached 40-year highs"

#### What is happening (Day 17)
- Gas prices stabilised temporarily by SPR release and IEA 400M barrel emergency release
- Energy Secretary Chris Wright (15 March): "Americans are feeling it right now. Americans will feel it for a few more weeks"
- Hassett confirmed 4–6 week timeline is still the briefing — but no strategy for how the war ends
- Trump's special envoy Witkoff asked how the war ends: "I don't know"

#### Next week
Gas prices likely hold at $3.50–$3.80 nationally as IEA reserves flow to market. California and other west-coast states already at $5+ and will not ease much. If Prince Sultan Air Base is re-attacked or Houthis enter the war, prices spike toward $4.50+ nationally within days.

#### Next month
If the Strait stays closed: prices drift to $4.00–$4.50 nationally, $5.50–$6+ in California. SPR is already 41% drawn down from peak; further releases become politically and physically harder. The war's $2B/day cost begins showing up in supplemental budget debates. Food inflation begins appearing on grocery shelves (4–8 week lag from oil spike). Interest rates: Federal Reserve under pressure; if inflation spikes, rate cuts are off the table for 2026.

#### Next 6 months
Without a Hormuz resolution: oil above $100 becomes the floor, not the ceiling. SPR further depleted. Gas at $4.50–$5.50 nationally, $6–$7 in California. Farm Bureau's fertiliser warning becomes grocery reality — food inflation 10–20% above pre-war baseline. The Oxford Economics model projects a US "temporary standstill" (near-recession) if oil holds at $140 for eight weeks; a six-month closure makes that threshold look conservative. War costs could reach $350 billion+.

#### If the war lasts a year or more
Ron Paul's 2006 prediction — "hidden inflation that hits the poor and middle class hardest" — becomes the defining domestic political story. Gas above $5 nationally. Food at 2022-level inflation (40-year highs). SPR at dangerously low levels. Congress unable to cut war funding (political trap) or sustain it (fiscal trap). Deutsche Bank analyst Jim Reid: "With each passing day it gets harder to argue that the disruption will only prove temporary." If the Houthis have also closed Bab-el-Mandeb, the combined disruption approaches the 1973 oil crisis — the last time the US faced structural, not temporary, supply loss.

### 2. Australia

Australia is the region in this archive with the most granular and alarming reporting. It is structurally the most exposed Western-aligned nation.

#### What happened
- Australia imports over 90% of its refined petroleum products
- Pre-war: ~A$1.70/litre diesel; ~A$1.90/litre petrol
- By Day 11 (11 March): diesel distributors describing shortages as "worst they have seen in their careers"
- Danny Kreutzer (Westlink Petroleum): 90% reduction in supplies; 37 of 38 trucks at standstill
- Australian farms turning off operations. Jake Turner (WA Wheatbelt): "We have turned a farming operation off from front to back."
- Australian fishing fleet grounded. Pavo Walker (Mooloolaba): "The three major fishing wharves have no fuel for us."
- Urea prices surged from A$830/tonne to A$1,195/tonne (+44%); peak fertiliser demand season (April–June) is weeks away
- China halted jet fuel and refined fuel exports to Australia (March 13)
- Mark Wales (ex-SAS): Australia has "30 something days" of fuel supply

#### What is happening (Day 17)
- **Emergency higher-sulphur fuel measure stalled**: The government approved allowing higher-sulphur fuel sales for 60 days as an emergency measure, but the paperwork has not been finalised — approval not expected until end of this week at the earliest. Regional communities could be waiting **weeks** for relief. (7 News, 16 March)
- Environment Minister Murray Watt (Sunrise, 16 March): "37 days reserve of petrol and 30 days reserve of diesel"; government releasing 762 million litres from emergency reserves. "Working round the clock."
- **Reality on the ground**: Service stations like Nathan's Happy Valley (northwest Victoria) have run out of fuel; owners limiting sales to $50 per car.
- **Australia now has only 2 oil refineries** — down from 6 under the previous government. Barnaby Joyce: "We are really hurting now... no plan."
- **Plastics warning**: Joyce flagged the plastics industry as the next crisis — polythene pipe and water tanks rely on oil derivatives. "This is going to smack us between the eyes."
- **Price gouging**: ACCC report showed retail petrol prices rising faster than wholesale; Treasurer Chalmers to legislate $100 million fines; ACCC-prompted meeting of suppliers, retailers and automotive groups on Tuesday.
- ABC News Australia (16 March): "Fuel rationing on the horizon in Australia if Iran war continues"
- Australia's Big Four banks (CBA, Westpac, NAB, ANZ) unanimously expect RBA rate hikes in March AND May, taking cash rate back to 4.35%; the war-driven inflation is the driving factor
- Economists warn petrol prices could rise 40 cents per litre in weeks

#### Next week
Petrol rises another 15–30 cents/litre in major cities; rural areas disproportionately affected. Government likely continues to publicly downplay while quietly managing. Fertiliser purchase window for winter crop (April–June) begins closing — orders not placed this week may mean no fertiliser this season.

#### Next month
- Fuel rationing protocols activated if Hormuz stays closed and China's fuel exports to Australia remain suspended
- Two RBA rate hikes (March and May) add $272/month to a typical $600,000 mortgage
- Easter food supplies under pressure (fishing fleet idled; farm operations paused)
- Petrol likely A$2.50–$2.80/litre in capital cities; A$3.00+ in regional areas
- Property market analysts forecasting price falls in Sydney and Melbourne as rate hikes bite simultaneously with cost of living pressure

#### Next 6 months
- Formal fuel rationing becomes likely if war extends; government has the legal powers
- Winter crop (barley, wheat, canola) permanently compromised if fertiliser doesn't arrive by May–June
- Food prices 15–25% above pre-war baseline; regional communities hardest hit
- AUKUS obligations pulling Australia deeper — Pine Gap is a target; "if Pacific war … fuel rationing … very different economy" (Mark Wales)
- Analysts forecasting Sydney and Melbourne property price falls under double shock of rate hikes + recession risk

#### If the war lasts a year or more
Australia has approximately 30 days of real fuel reserve (despite government claims of security). A sustained one-year war with Hormuz fully or partially closed would require either (a) permanent rationing, (b) emergency US supply via Alaska pipeline (US EPA has flagged this), or (c) a major pivot to domestic production, for which Australia has neither the refinery capacity nor the infrastructure. Mark Wales's 1941 comparison is the right frame: Australia in 1941 faced a Pacific war it was not prepared for; in 2026 it faces a Middle East war that severs its fuel supply chain. The economic consequences — recession, food inflation, property crash — become structural rather than cyclical.

### 3. New Zealand

#### What happened
- New Zealand petrol heading toward $3/litre
- Air New Zealand cancelled 1,000+ flights; shares down 8%; route viability at risk
- NZ Treasury modelling: if conflict lasts 3+ months, inflation rises 0.5–1 percentage point and GDP falls 0.2–0.4
- BNZ described the situation as "the worst of all worlds for the average New Zealander"
- 1 News (14 March): "Fuel crisis: potential restrictions as supply concern grows"
- RNZ: "no need to panic" but average petrol price has surged past $3/litre

#### What is happening (Day 17)
- Government messaging: no need to panic; supplies are secure
- Private forecasters more alarmed: petrol approaching $3.50/litre; diesel following

#### Next week
Petrol at $3.00–$3.20/litre. Aviation fuel crisis continues; more Air NZ route cancellations likely. NZ's small economy and heavy import dependence mean it tracks Australia's trajectory on a slight lag.

#### Next month
$3.50–$4.00/litre petrol if Hormuz stays closed. Tourism economy (air travel, car hire) severely disrupted. NZ's BNZ recession scenario moves from possible to probable. Interest rate hikes following Australia.

#### Next 6 months
Without Hormuz resolution: recession confirmed by Treasury modelling (GDP -0.2–0.4%); inflation 0.5–1% above target. Air New Zealand route network permanently restructured. Food prices up 10–15%.

#### If the war lasts a year or more
New Zealand's economy is too small and too dependent on imported energy to absorb a 12-month Hormuz closure without structural damage. Tourism collapse, food inflation, and a housing market under rate pressure combine for a recession of the sort not seen since the 2008 global financial crisis.

### 4. United Kingdom and Europe

#### What happened
- UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves announced she would explore help for households with heating oil bills (BBC, 9 March)
- Northern Ireland: heating oil bills up 81% — the most heating-oil-dependent part of the UK
- Rural England similarly exposed to heating oil price spikes
- CMA (Competition and Markets Authority) warned on fuel pricing and profiteering
- European natural gas prices up ~50% since the war began (Qatar forced to throttle LNG production)
- Oxford Economics modelling: if oil holds at $140 for eight weeks, Eurozone and UK face recession

#### What is happening (Day 17)
- UK pump prices rising sharply; rural heating oil causing acute political pressure
- France deploying carrier group in Eastern Mediterranean (defensive posture; explicitly refused Trump's request to send ships to Hormuz)
- European governments negotiating directly with Iran for safe passage of their ships through the Strait — France and Italy confirmed in talks
- European gas prices remain elevated; LNG alternatives being sought; US EPA flagging LNG exports to Europe

#### Next week
UK pump prices rise another 8–12p/litre. Heating oil in Northern Ireland and rural England remains at elevated levels. France's "Non" to Trump on Hormuz warships holds — but creates diplomatic friction. Germany faces energy security review.

#### Next month
European recession risk materialises in economic indicators. Eurozone inflation likely rises 0.5–0.8% above ECB target; rate cut cycle put on hold or reversed. UK heating oil crisis worsens into April — colder months still ahead for Northern Hemisphere. LNG spot prices remain elevated as Qatar throttles production. European elections and domestic politics increasingly affected by energy costs.

#### Next 6 months
Oxford Economics' recession trigger (oil at $140 for 8 weeks) may be reached if Houthis close Bab-el-Mandeb. European energy security debate returns to centre stage — the continent is caught between its declared support for the US-led order and the reality that the war is making European energy more expensive and more precarious. If Russia continues to benefit from high oil prices and supply diversion, European voters will draw uncomfortable conclusions. German car industry (diesel-dependent, already under EV pressure) particularly exposed.

#### If the war lasts a year or more
Europe faces structural energy repricing similar to 2022 post-Ukraine. The 2022 shock was absorbed partly through LNG alternatives and partly through Russia-linked gas still flowing. A 2026–27 war removes another major supply source. European gas storage would face the 2022 problem again but without the same alternatives. Political instability in energy-import-dependent states (Hungary, Slovakia, Bulgaria) becomes acute. The Oxford Economics recession scenario becomes inevitable rather than probable.

### 5. Japan and South Korea

Japan and South Korea are the most energy-import-dependent major economies in the world. Both depend on the Middle East for approximately 85–90% of their oil.

#### What happened
- Japan PM warned: "8 months to run out" of oil without Hormuz
- Panic in Japanese cabinet from Day 1
- Yen fell to 160 to the dollar, partly driven by energy import costs
- Korea Stock Exchange triggered circuit breakers (KOSPI -8%); South Korea explicitly mulling an oil price cap
- Japan pledged a record 80 million barrels from strategic reserves (about 45 days of consumption)
- Japan signed $57B in deals with US companies including energy (Reuters)
- Japan's national reserves system (started in 1978 after Arab oil embargo): 254 days of consumption held in reserve; now releasing

#### What is happening (Day 17)
- Japan began releasing oil from stockpiles Monday 16 March
- Government asked refiners to use released crude to secure domestic supplies
- METI seeking alternatives from US, Central Asia, South America, and Gulf nations that can bypass Hormuz
- Currently Japan gets ~4% of oil from the US; that share will need to rise dramatically

#### Next week
Japan and South Korea gas/diesel prices continue rising. Utility bills climbing. Government rationing talk intensifying in South Korea. South Korean chipmakers and automakers (diesel-dependent logistics) under cost pressure.

#### Next month
Japan's 45-day reserve release buys time but does not solve the problem. If Hormuz stays closed, Japan faces an energy emergency by late April. The concept of an IEA-coordinated 400M barrel release being "only 4 days of global consumption" (analyst iaTs6LxZmlo) becomes Japan's lived reality. Seoul may implement formal fuel conservation orders. Japanese utility prices spike 20–30% from pre-war levels.

#### Next 6 months
Japan's 2026 economic growth target (revised down from 1.5% to near zero) abandoned. Manufacturing (Toyota, Sony, Panasonic) operating at reduced capacity due to energy costs. South Korea's chip industry — already under US-China trade pressure — faces energy-input cost crisis. Both countries' governments under severe domestic political pressure. The IEA warns Japan's oil stockpile drawdown at current rate exhausts reserves within 6 months even with the record release.

#### If the war lasts a year or more
Japan and South Korea face the most acute long-term scenario of any developed economies. Both would be forced into: (a) permanent rationing programs, (b) emergency US supply deals (Alaska LNG; pipeline routes bypassing Hormuz), or (c) normalisation deals with whatever government emerges in Iran. Professor Jiang noted that Japan's Hormuz dependency — 75% of Japan's oil transits the Strait — was a core reason he predicted the war would "draw Japan" into escalation. The Hormuz crisis is the most direct threat to Japan's post-war economic model since the 1973 oil shock that created the reserve system in the first place.

### 6. China

China's situation is paradoxical: it is both hurt by the war and benefiting from it.

#### What happened
- China halted refined fuel and jet fuel exports to Australia (March 13) — domestic priority
- China condemned US–Israeli strikes from Day 1
- Iran has been exporting 2.1 million barrels per day to China via shadow fleet — *higher* than pre-war levels
- China's petrochemical refinery (Zhejiang Petrochemical, 200k bpd) cut runs 20–30% due to feedstock disruption
- China holds 120 days of petroleum reserves (Economist, Oct 2025)
- China donated $250,000 to families of school bombing victims — symbolic but significant

#### What is happening (Day 17)
- Iran offered Hormuz passage conditional on Chinese yuan payment — direct invitation for China to break petrodollar
- Bessent (US Treasury)–He Lifeng (China) meeting in Paris (15–16 March) — Xi–Trump summit pre-meeting; war and trade negotiations now merged
- China buying Iranian oil at 2.1M bbl/day; most transactions already denominated outside dollar system

#### Next week
China's domestic fuel prices managed by state control; consumers see modest increases but nothing like the West. The geopolitical story is the yuan-for-tankers offer. If any non-Chinese state accepts it, the petrodollar breach is systemic. China's refinery cuts will ease slightly if Iranian shadow fleet deliveries hold.

#### Next month
China's 120-day reserve provides significant buffer; domestic price pressure manageable. But Chinese exporters (manufactured goods) face global demand destruction as Western economies slow. The Bessent–He Lifeng meeting outcome will determine whether the US–China trade war escalates simultaneously with the military conflict — the worst case for global supply chains.

#### Next 6 months
China emerges from a 6-month war materially stronger in relative terms: its oil supply (Iranian shadow fleet, own reserves) is more stable than competitors; it has not depleted military assets; it has avoided direct military involvement while gaining leverage. The yuan-for-tankers framework, if established, begins restructuring how Middle Eastern oil is priced. China's position in the Xi–Trump summit (late March) shifts from defensive (trade war) to offensive (Hormuz leverage, yuan, Iranian oil lifeline).

#### If the war lasts a year or more
China's 120-day reserves run low if shadow fleet is disrupted. But China is the only major power with both the financial leverage (yuan, Iranian oil deals) and the geographic positioning (no Hormuz dependency for existing Iran pipeline routes) to weather a year-long war better than any other major consumer. The long-term prize is the post-war reconstruction of Iran and the reconfiguration of Middle East energy flows away from the dollar system. Jiang's prediction — that if Iran collapses, the US keeps petrodollar and global financial dominance — means China has a structural interest in Iran's survival, even if it stays officially neutral.

### 7. India

#### What happened
- India negotiating directly with Iran for safe passage through the Strait
- India approved a 30-million barrel Russian oil purchase with US waiver
- India is the world's third-largest oil consumer; heavily dependent on Gulf imports
- Indian foreign minister hailed talks with Iran to open the Strait of Hormuz (Reuters, 15 March)

#### What is happening (Day 17)
- India is pursuing a distinctive position: not condemning the US, not supporting Iran, actively seeking bilateral passage deals
- Diesel prices rising; subsidised fuel prices managed by government, but at fiscal cost

#### Next week
If India's Hormuz talks with Iran succeed, Indian-flagged tankers may pass through at yuan-linked or rupee-linked rates. This would be a significant breakthrough — and further evidence that the dollar-based oil trading system is fracturing.

#### Next month
India faces the same structural problem as Japan: heavy Gulf dependency, limited substitutes. But India's relationship with both Russia and Iran gives it more diplomatic options. Domestic fuel subsidies will come under fiscal pressure.

#### Next 6 months
If India secures bilateral passage, it could emerge as a key intermediary — buying Gulf oil at discounted prices and potentially re-exporting refined products. If passage deals fail, India faces a serious supply crunch and inflation surge that threatens the government's political position.

#### If the war lasts a year or more
India's dual-track approach (buying Russian oil, talking to Iran, not alienating the US) becomes harder to maintain as the war polarises the international system. India's strategic autonomy is its greatest asset but also its greatest vulnerability — it could be pressured from multiple directions simultaneously.

Pakistan is the most immediately exposed large economy. #### What happened
- **Thailand**: Half of Thailand's fishing fleet may remain in port due to diesel shortage; rising fuel prices threatening protein supply for 70 million people
- **Vietnam**: Vietnam implementing fuel tariff adjustments in response to the Iran war (Reuters, 9 March)
- **Indonesia**: Major fuel subsidies strain under oil spike; government reviewing prices

#### What is happening (Day 17)
- Southeast Asian fishing industries — heavily diesel-dependent — are being idled
- Food supply chains disrupted at the primary production level
- Thailand's diesel crisis is the leading edge of a global food supply cascade beginning in the developing world

#### Next week to next month
Diesel prices across Southeast Asia rise 20–40% from pre-war levels. Fishing fleets partially idled; food prices begin rising for fish, seafood, and transport-dependent staples. Governments face difficult choices between maintaining fuel subsidies (fiscal cost) and removing them (social cost).

#### Next 6 months / one year
Southeast Asia is highly vulnerable to an extended Hormuz closure because it has neither strategic reserves nor alternative supply chains. A year-long war would devastate regional fishing industries, push food inflation to crisis levels, and destabilise governments that depend on cheap food to maintain social peace. Vietnam in particular — dependent on Gulf oil for manufacturing — faces an industrial output crisis.

### 10. Middle East Gulf States (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar)

The Gulf states are simultaneously oil exporters who benefit from high prices and oil infrastructure owners whose assets are being targeted.

#### What happened
- Iraq cut oil output by 60% (to 1.7–1.8M bbl/day from 4.3M) because storage has filled up — can't export
- Kuwait, UAE reduced production 20–30% for the same reason
- Qatar throttled LNG production; Asian spot LNG almost doubled, European gas up ~50%
- Saudi Arabia: major oil storage outside the Middle East pre-positioned before the war (US Energy Secretary Wright confirmed)
- Prince Sultan Air Base (Saudi Arabia): five USAF refueling planes destroyed by Iranian ballistic missiles (Day 15/16) — Saudi Arabia now exposed as active war participant
- Bahrain, Kuwait, UAE: infrastructure strikes ongoing
- Iran's shadow fleet passing through Hormuz; Arab Gulf states' vessels cannot

#### What is happening (Day 17)
- Saudi Arabia can no longer maintain fiction of neutrality after Prince Sultan strike
- Iran targeting desalination plants (Gulf states' primary water supply); limited damage so far but threat is existential
- Gulf AI ambitions (data centres, AWS, Google, Palantir, Nvidia) at risk: AWS hit; Iran warned all tech infrastructure is a target

#### Predictions (one week to one year)
The Gulf states face the most acute scenario in this document. Unlike other regions, they are not just fuel consumers — they are fuel producers who cannot export. The storage-full problem means even if production was safe, it cannot reach markets. Iran's selective Hormuz closure (open for Iran and China, closed for everyone else) is specifically designed to bankrupt the Gulf states while keeping Iran's revenue flowing.

Over one year: if the Gulf oil infrastructure remains contested and desalination plants are seriously damaged, the Gulf states face not just economic but civilizational risk. They import 89% of their food and rely on desalination for 60%+ of their water. Jiang's "giant mirage" prediction — that the Gulf states are not viable without the US security umbrella — is being tested in real time.

## Jet fuel (a special case)

Jet fuel is the most immediately dislocated product in the war. Per the Fabian Vera analysis (March 6):

- Asian jet fuel crack spreads doubled; European prices at 28-month highs
- ~18–20% of global jet fuel exports transit Hormuz
- Europe sources 25–50% of jet fuel from Gulf (Kuwait is the top exporter)
- Jet A-1 cannot be substituted with diesel (different freeze point, -47°C)
- Inventories are structurally brittle with no quick alternatives

**Airline consequences (Day 17):**
- Air New Zealand cancelled 1,000+ flights; shares down 8%
- Flights cancelled across the Gulf, Middle East, and Southeast Asia
- Aviation fuel futures pricing in sustained disruption through at least mid-2026
- A 90-day disruption scenario (Fabian Vera): combined global stakes $500 billion to $1 trillion (aviation, infrastructure, inflation)

**The reinforcing loop:** Hormuz closed → jet fuel supply disrupted → inventories fall → prices rise → hoarding → further price rise → airlines cut routes → economic activity further contracts. This loop takes 4–8 weeks to begin unwinding even if Hormuz fully reopens tomorrow.

## Fertiliser (the slow-motion catastrophe)

The fuel story most people are missing. As Chomsky predicted in 2006–07, the oil shock cascades to fertiliser, then to food.

| Region | What is happening |
|--------|-------------------|
| Australia | Urea A$830 → A$1,195/t (+44%); suppliers can't quote prices; winter crop (April–June) fertiliser window closing now |
| United States | Farm Bureau letter to White House; food supply disruption "not seen since 2022" |
| Global | ~35–45% of global urea supply transits Hormuz; supply blocked for 17 days |
| New Zealand | BNZ: "worst of all worlds"; Treasury modelling recession if conflict >3 months |

**The agricultural calendar does not wait.** If fertiliser does not reach farms in the next 2–4 weeks, the 2026 Northern Hemisphere spring planting and Southern Hemisphere winter crop are damaged **regardless of when the war ends**. This is Chomsky's cascade — the food price inflation from this war will last years, not months.

No reserve release can offset that. The $200/barrel IRGC warning would no longer be a threat — it would be an underestimate.

## What the analysts say

**Jiang** (law of asymmetry): "Iran doesn't need to win militarily. It needs to outlast. Every day the Strait is closed, the economics force American withdrawal." Iran keeping its own oil exports flowing (2.1M bbl/day to China) while blocking everyone else's is the strategic embodiment of that logic.

**Ritter** (2006): "Oil will go to $150–200." The IEA reserves bought time. But as analyst iaTs6LxZmlo noted: 400 million barrels = only 4 days of global consumption. "The reserves can stabilise supply in the short term but they mainly buy time." (Japan's Yuriy Humber, 15 March 2026)

**Chomsky** (2006–07): The cascade from oil to fertiliser to food to political instability in countries far from the battlefield. Pakistan schools closed, Thailand's fishing fleet idled, Australian farms turning off operations — all on schedule, all exactly as described.

**Yergin** (8 March 2026): "The key question for global energy markets now is the duration of this explosive war." That question has no answer. Trump's special envoy says "I don't know" how it ends.
