# Who's Who: Key Actors and Factions

A guide to the states, factions, and figures you need to know to follow the US-Israel-Iran war. If geopolitics feels like an alphabet soup of acronyms, start here.

Last updated: 13 March 2026 (Day 14 of the war)

---

## Part 1: The States

### Iran (Islamic Republic of Iran)

- **Population:** ~88 million
- **Government:** Theocratic republic. A supreme leader holds ultimate authority over the military, judiciary, and foreign policy. A president handles day-to-day governance. Both are elected or appointed through a system controlled by religious clerics.
- **Why the US is hostile:** Iran has been independent of American control since the 1979 revolution. It sits on the world's fourth-largest oil reserves and second-largest natural gas reserves. Its geographic position controls the Strait of Hormuz -- the chokepoint for 20% of global oil. Chomsky's argument: the hostility is not about nuclear weapons or terrorism. It is about obedience. Iran disobeyed.
- **Military:** Iran's conventional forces are weaker than the US and Israel. Its strength is asymmetric warfare -- drones, missiles, mines, proxy networks, and the ability to make war expensive for stronger opponents. Professor Jiang's 2024 lectures centred on this: a $50,000 drone can threaten a $1 billion aircraft carrier.
- **In the current war:** Iran absorbed the US-Israeli decapitation strike (which killed Supreme Leader Khamenei) without collapsing. It closed the Strait of Hormuz, laid thousands of mines, and launched retaliatory strikes across the region. Mojtaba Khamenei was named successor.

### Israel

- **Population:** ~9.8 million
- **Government:** Parliamentary democracy. Currently led by Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu and a coalition that includes far-right ministers.
- **Role in this war:** Co-launched Operation Epic Fury with the US. Israeli strikes targeted Iranian nuclear scientists, IRGC facilities, and military infrastructure. Netanyahu has described the war as making Israel "stronger than ever."
- **Nuclear weapons:** Israel has approximately 90 nuclear warheads (estimated). It has never signed the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty, never submitted to international inspections, and has never officially acknowledged its arsenal. See `the-nuclear-question-for-beginners.md` for the full picture.
- **Why it matters:** Israel is the only nuclear-armed state in the Middle East. Its alliance with the US is the backbone of the war. Ritter (2006): "The Iran crisis is made in Israel." Chomsky noted that when US and Israeli interests conflict (e.g., Israeli arms sales to China in 2005), the US wins and the Israel lobby goes silent -- but on Iran, their interests align.

### The United States

- **Role in this war:** Launched Operation Epic Fury on 28 February 2026, alongside Israel. The stated objectives: destroy Iran's missiles and ability to make them, destroy its navy, permanently deny nuclear weapons, and weaken Iranian proxies.
- **Military presence in the region:** The US has military bases across the Middle East -- in Kuwait (Ali al-Salem, Camp Arifjan), Qatar (Al Udeid -- the largest US base in the region), Bahrain (NSA Bahrain, home of the Fifth Fleet), the UAE (Al Dhafra), Iraq (multiple), and naval assets in the Persian Gulf, Arabian Sea, and Red Sea.
- **The contradiction:** The US is simultaneously the world's largest oil producer and the country most disrupted by the Strait of Hormuz closure, because global oil markets are interconnected. Trump called rising oil prices "good" because "we make a lot of money" -- while American consumers face $5/gallon gas.

## Part 2: The Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC)

The GCC is an alliance of six Arab monarchies on the Persian Gulf's western shore. They did not choose this war, but they host US military bases, and Iran views those bases as legitimate targets. All six have been struck. For the full picture, see `the-iran-war-and-the-gulf.md`.

### Saudi Arabia (Kingdom of Saudi Arabia)

- **Population:** ~36 million
- **Leader:** Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman (MBS) -- the de facto ruler. His father, King Salman, is elderly and largely ceremonial.
- **Role:** Saudi Arabia is Iran's primary regional rival. The kingdom sees itself as the leader of the Sunni Muslim world; Iran leads the Shia world. This is sometimes called the "Sunni-Shia divide," though the rivalry is more about power than theology.
- **In the current war:** Eastern oil fields targeted repeatedly by Iran. Prince Sultan air base targeted with ballistic missiles. Saudi Arabia is ramping up crude through its east-to-west pipeline to bypass the Strait, but experts say it cannot replace Hormuz volume.
- **MBS warned in 2018:** If the Iran nuclear deal was scrapped, Saudi Arabia would develop its own nuclear weapons. The deal was scrapped. The nuclear proliferation risk Kovalik described is now live.

### United Arab Emirates (UAE) -- including Dubai

- **Population:** ~10 million (roughly 88% expatriates)
- **Leaders:** President Sheikh Mohammed bin Zayed (MBZ); Dubai is ruled by Sheikh Mohammed bin Rashid
- **Role:** The UAE has positioned itself as a global business and tourism hub. Dubai in particular depends on being perceived as safe, modern, and neutral.
- **In the current war:** Iranian drones struck a residential building in Dubai. Dubai International Airport was hit. Citi and HSBC are suspending operations. Standard Chartered is evacuating. The UAE called the strikes "brutal and unjustified Iranian aggression" -- but the frustration is directed at both sides.
- **Vulnerability:** Jiang's central argument about the Gulf: these countries "look rich and powerful but are actually extremely fragile." The UAE imports ~90% of its food and gets ~42% of its drinking water from desalination plants -- large, fragile, coastal facilities that a $50,000 drone can destroy.

### Qatar

- **Population:** ~2.9 million (roughly 88% expatriates)
- **Leader:** Emir Sheikh Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani
- **Role:** Qatar hosts Al Udeid, the largest US military base in the Middle East. It also maintains diplomatic ties with Iran and has historically served as a mediator. This balancing act has been destroyed by the war.
- **In the current war:** Explosions in Doha; airport shut; missiles intercepted. Qatar PM condemned Iranian strikes as "unacceptable under any pretext" while calling for negotiations. Previously described the situation as a "betrayal" -- Qatar had tried to mediate and was attacked anyway.
- **Vulnerability:** Qatar gets ~99% of its drinking water from desalination. It imports almost all of its food.

### Bahrain

- **Population:** ~1.5 million
- **Leader:** King Hamad bin Isa Al Khalifa (Sunni monarchy ruling a Shia-majority population)
- **Role:** Bahrain hosts NSA Bahrain and the US Fifth Fleet. It is the most politically fragile GCC state.
- **In the current war:** Iranian drones struck fuel storage tanks at Bahrain International Airport. Mass protests have erupted against the ruling class's role as a launchpad for the US war. Bahrain has imported anti-riot troops from Jordan -- the first time since the 2011 Arab Spring that it has called in foreign forces to suppress domestic unrest.

### Kuwait

- **Population:** ~4.3 million
- **Role:** Hosts Ali al-Salem Air Base and Camp Arifjan (US military). Kuwait was the staging ground for the 2003 Iraq invasion.
- **In the current war:** Kuwait International Airport hit by several drones. Oil production cut. A Canadian military camp at Ali al-Salem appeared damaged by Iranian missile on March 1 -- not disclosed to the Canadian public.

### Oman

- **Population:** ~5.1 million
- **Role:** Oman has traditionally played a neutral mediating role. An Omani mediator said a peace deal was "within reach" before the war began.
- **In the current war:** Oil terminals shut after attacks on tankers. Drones hit fuel tanks at Salalah port. Strikes on oil storage facilities.

## Part 3: The Factions

### The IRGC (Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps)

**What it is:** Iran's most powerful military force. The IRGC is separate from the regular Iranian army (Artesh). It was created after the 1979 revolution to protect the Islamic Republic from both external enemies and internal coups.

**What it does:**
- Controls Iran's ballistic missile program and drone arsenal
- Runs the Quds Force (external operations -- supporting allies and proxies abroad)
- Has major economic interests (construction, oil, telecommunications)
- Commands the Basij (a paramilitary volunteer force of millions)

**Why it matters:** The IRGC is the institution that makes Iran's asymmetric warfare possible. It built the "axis of resistance," controls the mines in the Strait of Hormuz, and commands the retaliatory strikes. Netanyahu described Mojtaba Khamenei as a "puppet of the Revolutionary Guards" -- whether or not that is true, it reflects the IRGC's central role.

**In the current war:** The IRGC declared: "Not a liter of oil through the Strait of Hormuz." An IRGC spokesman warned: "Expect oil at $200 per barrel." The IRGC ordered US troops in the region to leave "if they don't want to die and be buried under the rubble." The IRGC's Quds Force coordinates with allied factions across Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, and Yemen.

### Hezbollah (Party of God)

**What it is:** A Lebanese Shia political party and paramilitary organisation. Founded in 1982 during Israel's invasion of Lebanon, with Iranian support.

**What it does:**
- Operates as both a political party (members of the Lebanese parliament) and a military force
- Provides social services (healthcare, education) in southern Lebanon -- this is part of its popular support base
- Maintains a large arsenal of rockets and missiles pointed at Israel
- Acts as Iran's most capable non-state ally

**Relationship with Iran:** Iran helped create Hezbollah and provides funding, weapons, and training. But -- as Jiang emphasised -- Hezbollah is an ally, not a puppet. Iran supports it but "cannot completely control their behaviour."

**In the current war:** Lebanon has been hit from day one. 630+ Lebanese civilians killed by Day 14. Hezbollah is part of Iran's retaliatory network. Netanyahu said he wouldn't take "life insurance" on Hezbollah leader Naim Qassem.

### The Houthis (Ansar Allah)

**What they are:** A Zaydi Shia rebel movement in Yemen that controls much of the country's north and west, including the capital Sanaa.

**Background:** Yemen has been devastated by a Saudi-led war (supported by the US and UK) since 2015. The Houthis have fought against this coalition for nearly a decade. Kovalik devoted an entire chapter to the Yemen war, describing it as a US-Saudi campaign that created one of the world's worst humanitarian crises.

**Why they matter now:** The Houthis control the approaches to the Red Sea. They have demonstrated the ability to attack commercial shipping and US naval assets. In Western media, their actions are framed as "Houthi attacks" -- acts of aggression by a terrorist group. The context -- that Yemen was being bombed by a US-backed coalition -- is usually absent or buried deep in the article.

**In the current war:** Houthis have claimed attacks on US naval vessels. Their position near the Bab el-Mandeb strait means that even if the Strait of Hormuz were cleared, alternative oil routes through the Red Sea are also threatened.

### The PMU / PMF (Popular Mobilization Forces -- al-Hashd al-Shaabi)

**What they are:** An umbrella organisation of predominantly Shia militias in Iraq. The PMU was formally established in 2014 to fight ISIS after the Iraqi army collapsed.

**Why they matter:** Many PMU factions have close ties to Iran and the IRGC's Quds Force. Some were trained by Qasem Soleimani personally. Iraq's position -- hosting both US military bases and Iran-aligned militias -- makes it a secondary battlefield.

**In the current war:** Iraq has been described as a "secondary battlefield." Iran-aligned groups are targeting US bases, the consulate, and Victory Base (Baghdad airport) with drones "every single night." A pro-Iran militia claimed it shot down a US KC-135 refuelling aircraft in Iraq. PMU headquarters in Salahadin and positions at Al-Qaim (Iraq-Syria border) have been hit by US/Israeli strikes, with dozens killed or wounded.

### The "Axis of Resistance"

**What it is:** An informal alliance of Iran and its non-state allies: Hezbollah, the Houthis, Iraqi Shia militias (PMU factions), and (previously) Hamas. The term is used by the alliance itself. Western media often calls them "Iran-backed groups" or "Iranian proxies."

**Jiang's key point:** This alliance shares common interests -- resisting American influence and Israeli expansion -- but Iran does not fully control its members. "Hamas launched October 7. Iran says we were not involved. Israel says that can't be true because you control Hamas." The reality is somewhere in between: Iran supports, funds, and arms these groups, but they have their own leaderships, agendas, and decision-making processes.

**Kovalik's point:** The US spent years dismantling this network before attacking Iran directly -- weakening Syria through civil war, degrading Hezbollah through years of conflict, isolating Iran through sanctions. The 2026 war came only after the allies were weakened. This was the sequence Kovalik predicted: "US policy aimed to dismantle Iran's allies in Syria and Lebanon first, through proxy wars, before moving directly against Iran itself."

## Part 4: The Figures

### Iranian leadership

**Ayatollah Ali Khamenei (1939-2026)**
Supreme Leader of Iran from 1989 until his death in the opening strikes of Operation Epic Fury on 28 February 2026. Khamenei held ultimate authority over Iran's military, foreign policy, and judiciary for 36 years. He issued the fatwa banning nuclear weapons and privately opposed the JCPOA, believing the US was not a reliable partner. Kovalik: "In his substantial wisdom, he was correct in his misgivings."

**Mojtaba Khamenei**
Son of Ali Khamenei. Named as successor after his father's death. Reportedly injured in the February 28 strike -- legs, hand, and arm; six family members killed. Netanyahu has called him a "puppet of the Revolutionary Guards" who "cannot show his face in public." Mojtaba's statement that the Strait blockade "must certainly continue" drove oil prices higher. His selection as successor confirms Jiang and Chomsky's prediction: the strike strengthened hardliners rather than moderating the regime.

**Abbas Araghchi**
Iran's Foreign Minister. Has been the public voice of Iranian diplomacy during the war. Called Operation Epic Fury "Operation Epic Mistake -- a misadventure engineered by Israel and paid for by ordinary Americans." Called the preemptive-strike claim "a sheer and utter lie." Stated that only a "permanent end to war" -- not a temporary ceasefire -- would be acceptable, because prior ceasefires were "exploited."

**Qasem Soleimani (1957-2020)**
Commander of the IRGC's Quds Force until his assassination by US drone strike in Baghdad in January 2020. Soleimani built Iran's regional alliance network. His killing was the most provocative US act against Iran since 1953 and is widely seen as a turning point that made war inevitable. Jiang taught the assassination as the moment Iran concluded that war was a question of "when," not "if."

### US administration

**Donald Trump**
President of the United States. Launched Operation Epic Fury. Withdrew from the JCPOA in 2018. Ordered the Soleimani assassination in 2020. During the current war, has simultaneously said the war is "very complete" and "we're not finished." Blamed his own advisers (Kushner, Miller, Hegseth, Rubio) for the decision to attack. Journalist Mehdi Hasan: "Trump, the man-child, was manipulated into this war by people around him with their own political, financial, religious agendas."

**Pete Hegseth**
Secretary of Defense. Former Fox News host with no prior military command or government experience. Has said "This is only just the beginning" on the same day Trump said the war was "very complete." Banned press photographers from Pentagon briefings. Called the drone strike that killed six soldiers "a squirter." Restructured Pentagon legal offices mid-war. Described Tuesday of Week 2 as "the most intense day" of US strikes.

**Jared Kushner**
Trump's son-in-law and senior adviser. Named by Trump as one of the advisers who urged the attack. Kushner brokered the Abraham Accords (normalisation deals between Israel and Gulf states). Hasan described Kushner's agenda as "financial" -- Kushner has significant business interests in the Middle East, including a $2 billion investment from Saudi Arabia's sovereign wealth fund.

**Marco Rubio**
Secretary of State. Named by Trump as one of the advisers who urged the attack. Hasan described Rubio's agenda as "political."

**Steve Witkoff**
Trump's special envoy. When asked on CNBC how the war ends, his answer: "I don't know."

**Carolyn Leavitt**
White House Press Secretary. Listed Iran's destruction as a goal with no mention that the US struck first. When CBS's Nancy Cordes asked if Trump was "making this up" regarding the school strike, Leavitt's answer: "The president is not making anything up. This was a feeling the president had based on facts."

### Israeli leadership

**Benjamin Netanyahu**
Prime Minister of Israel. Has been in power (with interruptions) since 1996. Co-launched the war with the US. In his first major speech since the war began, said Israel was "becoming stronger than ever" and boasted about killing Iranian nuclear scientists. Has a pattern of framing Iranian threats in existential terms; once called Iran "Germany" and himself "1938."

**Itamar Ben-Gvir**
Minister of National Security. Far-right. Vowed "severity and zero tolerance" against international media covering the war. Dispatched police; journalists detained and arrested.

**Gideon Sa'ar**
Foreign Minister. Praised Australia for criminalising protest slogans ("From the river to the sea") and urged other Australian states to copy the legislation.

### Other key figures

**Mohammed bin Salman (MBS)**
Crown Prince of Saudi Arabia and de facto ruler. Saudi Arabia's most powerful figure. Has positioned himself as a moderniser while conducting a devastating war in Yemen and consolidating absolute power (including the detention of hundreds of Saudi royals and business leaders at the Ritz-Carlton in 2017). Warned in 2018 that if the Iran deal were scrapped, Saudi Arabia would develop nuclear weapons.

**Sheikh Mohammed bin Abdulrahman Al Thani**
Prime Minister of Qatar. Has tried to maintain Qatar's role as mediator while condemning Iranian strikes. Called the attacks "unacceptable under any pretext" and expressed "pride as a Qatari" in national resilience. Has received calls from Canadian and Spanish foreign ministers urging de-escalation.

**Naim Qassem**
Current leader of Hezbollah. Netanyahu said he would not take "life insurance" on him -- an implied threat of assassination.

## Part 5: The acronym decoder

| Acronym | Full name | What it is |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| IRGC | Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps | Iran's elite military force; controls missiles, drones, Quds Force |
| GCC | Gulf Cooperation Council | Alliance of six Gulf monarchies: Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman |
| PMU / PMF | Popular Mobilization Forces | Iraqi Shia militia umbrella; many factions allied with Iran |
| JCPOA | Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action | The 2015 Iran nuclear deal; Trump withdrew in 2018 |
| NPT | Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty | International treaty limiting nuclear weapons; Iran signed, Israel did not |
| IAEA | International Atomic Energy Agency | UN body that inspects nuclear programs; confirmed Iran had no bomb |
| NIE | National Intelligence Estimate | Assessment by all 16 US intelligence agencies; said Iran halted weapons program |
| CENTCOM | US Central Command | The US military command responsible for the Middle East region |
| THAAD | Terminal High Altitude Area Defense | US missile defence system; radar destroyed in current war |
| SAVAK | Sazman-e Ettela'at va Amniyat-e Keshvar | The Shah's secret police (1957-1979); created with CIA and Mossad help |
| MEK / MKO | Mojahedin-e Khalq | Iranian opposition group; once listed as terrorist org; de-listed 2012; Bolton ally |
