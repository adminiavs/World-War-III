## tl;dr

Open-source investigation by Nilo Tabrizy (Washington Post, New York Times) into the strike on Shajareh Tayyibeh elementary school in Minab, Hormozgan province, southern Iran. Video of the aftermath was verified as first posted Feb 28; location confirmed via Google Earth/OpenStreetMaps and georeferencing (Chris Osieck, Anno Nemo, author) on Resalat Boulevard next to an IRGC navy base. Narrative that the IRGC launched a failed rocket that hit the school was debunked: the image used was from Zanjan, ~800 miles away, and showed snowcapped mountains not present in Minab. A later claim that the IRGC had "confessed" to mistakenly bombing the school was debunked by Jasper Nathaniel; the screenshot came from a pro-monarchy Telegram channel, not official Iranian state channels. A source said the strike was perhaps an error by the US and Israel; the school's brightly coloured walls were visible on Google Earth for eight years and the school sits right next to the IRGC base. US military told media it was "looking into" reports; Red Crescent death toll rose from at least 108 on Saturday to 153 by Sunday. The strike raises operational questions about how targets were chosen and why civilian infrastructure such as this school was hit. ## Full story

Open-source investigation into the airstrike on the Shajareh Tayyibeh elementary school in Minab, Hormozgan province (inland from the Strait of Hormuz). The author learned within an hour of the US-Israeli operation that the school had been hit; post-strike video showed a crowd outside the building, smoke, partial collapse, and distress. Verification: screenshots reverse image searched to confirm the video was first posted on Feb 28. Location: a source said the school was right next to an IRGC navy base on Resalat Boulevard. Using Google Earth and OpenStreetMaps, and aided by georeferencing shared on Telegram, the author confirmed the exact location. Chris Osieck, Anno Nemo and the author independently located the school to the same coordinates.

Who carried out the strike? Information wars began quickly. Some accounts aligned with the camp supporting restoration of Iran's monarchy and Reza Pahlavi began asserting it was a failed rocket launch by the IRGC from the nearby base, alongside an image they claimed was the missile launch. This was quickly debunked by Ali Noorani on X: the image was from the town of Zanjan, roughly 800 miles from Minab, and showed snowcapped mountains not visible in southern Minab. On March 1, more pro-Pahlavi accounts claimed the IRGC had confessed to mistakenly bombing the school via a screenshot they said came from official Iranian state channels. Jasper Nathaniel debunked this: the assertion was posted on a Telegram channel supportive of the monarchy, not an official channel.

A source told the author that the tragic airstrike was perhaps an error on the part of the US and Israel. The school is right next to an IRGC navy base, but the brightly coloured walls of the school were visible on Google Earth as far back as eight years ago. The US military told media it was "looking into" reports of the airstrike and civilian casualties. Red Crescent spokesperson: death toll at least 108 on Saturday, rising to 153 by Sunday. The strike raises operational questions about how targets were chosen and why there have been attacks on civilian infrastructure such as this school. "Seeing images after the strike, such as a bloodstained backpack, a small hand in the rubble and more gruesome scenes that I have to compartmentalize, affirms that the most vulnerable are always casualties in war. On Saturday, the opening day of Operation Epic Fury, it was schoolchildren in Minab."
