## Full story

What is the White House's justification for authorising US military strikes on Iran? Right now, the answer changes every day.

Since Israel and the United States launched their attack on Saturday, we've been getting mixed messages.

US President Donald Trump has offered several different explanations for his decision. But his statements are being contradicted by senior officials in his own administration, and even Israel's Benjamin Netanyahu — his closest ally in the region.

The White House has named its current mission in Iran Operation Epic Fury. Depending on what happens next, it may be viewed by many as Operation Epic Failure.

Iran's supreme leader, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, was killed along with multiple other regime figures when his compound was bombed early in the campaign.

When Trump announced the attack on Saturday, he urged the Iranian people to "take over" their country's government.

"This will be probably your only chance for generations," he said.

But by Monday, the president's rhetoric had shifted. He did not mention regime change when he spoke about the mission. Instead, he highlighted the need to stop Iran developing a nuclear weapon and long-range ballistic missiles that were capable of striking US territory.

There's context here, of course. Trump is not like other world leaders. He often talks off the cuff, even about significant geopolitical and military issues. It's not uncommon for him to contradict himself, or for the threats he telegraphs to fail to materialise.

But some analysts argue this candidness the US president exudes — the type we see publicly, at least — is unhelpful when trying to explain significant decisions, like the one that was made to attack Iran and kill Khamenei.

It is not just Trump's words that have had some people scratching their heads. Netanyahu reiterated on Monday that while the mission was about increasing Israel's security, he also wanted the Islamic Republic to fall, or as he theatrically put it, "for the brave Iranian people to cast off the yoke of tyranny from upon themselves".

The Israeli prime minister's message was clear, but completely at odds with what US Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth declared a few hours later when he told journalists, "this is not a so-called regime change war".

Trump's Secretary of State Marco Rubio, another official to front the cameras on Monday, did not speak about the idea of a new government in Iran either. He did, however, bring even more justifications for the attack to the table.

"The United States is conducting an operation to eliminate the threat of Iran's short-range ballistic missiles, and the threat posed by their navy," he said.

Rubio also said: "I don't understand where the confusion is."

He may be in the minority there.

The Trump administration could frame all this as an issue with communication and the "fake news" media, rather than US military strategy. Multiple officials have said that everything is going to plan in Iran, even if they can't articulate what that plan is.

But outside the White House, concern is growing.

Jasmine El-Gamal, a former Middle East adviser to the Pentagon, argued Trump had no clear goal and was "retroactively trying to justify his decision to go to war".

"I think what we've seen … is the scene being set for uncontrolled escalation," she told British broadcaster Sky News this week.

"We are now getting to a point where Iran is desperately trying to maintain its survival as a regime, and you have a US president who is desperately trying to maintain his credibility ahead of midterm elections and trying to make sense of this decision that he made to go to war."

The death and destruction in the Middle East have overshadowed US politics, but many analysts argue November's midterms will be front of mind for Trump when it comes to what he wants out of Iran.

Polls are predicting his Republican Party will lose control of the lower house of Congress. His approval ratings are plummeting. Could a strong statement in Iran help the president win back votes at home?

Professor Robert Pape is a political scientist at the University of Chicago and an expert in global security affairs. He said the fact US and Israeli attacks had hit their targets on Saturday — the ayatollah and other regime figures — had "created the illusion of control".

"This was tactical success exactly as expected. But tactical success does not equal strategic control," he wrote on his SubStack, Escalation Trap, at the weekend.

For Israel, the objectives in Iran are simple: cause as much damage as possible to the regime and change the security reality in the region.

But the US is having more difficulty explaining what it is trying to achieve. Maybe it's a communication problem. But maybe it's an indication there is no definitive goal.

No goal means no end point. And if there's no end point, that's when Operation Epic Fury is in danger of becoming an epic failure.

*Riley Stuart in Jerusalem, ABC News. Tue 3 Mar 2026.*
