## Full story

On Tuesday, Feb. 24, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth met with Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei and threatened to invoke the Defense Production Act (DPA) if Anthropic doesn't agree to the Pentagon's terms by Friday. The DPA, Hegseth warned, would let the government compel Anthropic to provide its technology on the Pentagon's terms. Anthropic is resisting allowing its artificial intelligence (AI) to be used for autonomous weapons or mass surveillance.

The DPA is a Korean War-era statute that gives the president broad authority to direct private industry in the name of national defense. It has been extended many times, most recently through September 2026. The DPA already applies to AI; Biden's rescinded EO 14110 invoked the DPA (Title VII) for reporting requirements. Hegseth is likely threatening Title I, the statute's core compulsion power: priority for contracts, and "acceptance and performance" of contracts plus allocation of materials, services, and facilities.

The legal analysis depends on what the government demands. Two possibilities: (1) Claude without Anthropic's contractual usage-policy guardrails (same model, different terms); (2) forced retraining of Claude to strip safety restrictions from the model. For (1), the government could argue it's the same product on different conditions; Anthropic could argue guardrails are part of what Claude is. For (2), a retrained model looks more like a new product; the DPA's scope for compelling products a company doesn't ordinarily supply is ambiguous, and the major questions doctrine cuts against vast agency claims. Implementing regulation (15 C.F.R. § 700.13(c)) allows companies to reject rated orders for "an item not supplied or for a service not performed" by default.

If Anthropic resists a formal DPA order, the likely path is comply under protest (given criminal penalties for noncompliance) and seek a temporary restraining order. But Hegseth doesn't need to win in court; the threat may be credible enough to change behavior. The deeper problem is that Congress hasn't set substantive rules for military AI; the question of what values to embed in military AI is too important to be resolved by a Cold War-era production statute.

(Lawfare, 25 Feb 2026. Alan Z. Rozenshtein.)
