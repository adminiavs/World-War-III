## Full story

Iowa farmers are sounding the alarm on fertilizer and fuel prices amidst the war in Iran just weeks ahead of spring planting season.

Lance Lillibridge, a Vinton farmer and member of the Iowa Corn Growers Association says the timing couldn't be worse.

"We have to plant a crop. Just plain and simple. That's what has to happen," he told KCCI. "Is less fertilizer going to be used? Absolutely."

Typically, mid-March is when Iowa's farmers begin applying fertilizer to their fields. Spring planting happens in early to mid-April. Between the cost of fertilizer and fuel, the process isn't cheap.

But the problem now lies in getting that fuel and fertilizer to the U.S.

Currently, oil tankers and fertilizer transports aren't able to safely pass through the Strait of Hormuz, a narrow waterway near Iran which handles a significant portion of global energy and fertilizer supplies.

"A lot of places won't even price the fertilizer to us," Lillibridge said. "So, if we went in and wanted to prepay for some fertilizer for spring application, they won't take a check. They'll let us commit to a volume, a certain tonnage, but they won't give us a price on it."

Some farmers might end up forgoing their fertilizer on some of their land. While they may be able to skate by this year, Lillibridge says it could mean yields in future years are lower than they'd like.

But the biggest problem is this price hike is happening after years of already-high input costs and low commodity prices.

And for some farmers, it's a breaking point.

"A lot of guys have just said, I'm done. We're going to quit. They're hanging it up," Lillibridge said. "There are sales everywhere, lots and lots of farm sales. Financially, it's causing a lot of mental health problems as well."

While President Trump offered military support in transporting those ships through the Strait of Hormuz, there's still a lot of uncertainty. It's unclear if that protection and insurance coverage will apply to only American fleets or all ships making the passage.
