## Full story

Even as American and Iranian officials were participating in Omani-mediated talks aimed at preventing further escalation, the United States, alongside Israel, launched military strikes on Iran on Feb. 28. The mediation had raised cautious hopes. Instead, this use of force reflects a familiar post-1945 pattern of major powers acting unilaterally rather than through multilateral institutions like the UN. The author scrutinises Washington's five stated objectives against international law. First: "stand united in defense of our citizens, sovereignty and territory" – yet at the outset there was no verified report of Iran posing an imminent threat to US territory or allies; the objective closely aligns with Israel's priorities. Second: counter Iranian escalation – the joint statement describes Iranian missile and drone launches as "indiscriminate and reckless," but those strikes only came after US and Israeli strikes killed Iran's top leadership and caused enormous civilian casualties; Iran's regional strikes were responsive, not pre-emptive. Third: regional stability – the claim sits uneasily alongside widening instability, including friendly fire, cross-border missile exchanges and mounting casualties in Lebanon, Bahrain, Israel and the UAE. Fourth: uphold sovereignty – the statement accuses Iran of violating the sovereignty of regional states, yet prior to the joint offensive there was no evidence of such a breach; in contrast, US and Israeli airstrikes penetrated deep into Iranian territory, breaching Iran's sovereignty under Article 2(4) of the UN Charter. Fifth: collective self-defence – Article 51 permits self-defence only if an armed attack occurs; as reported, the initial attack was conducted by the US and Israel against Iran. This raises a deeper legitimacy question: are some states claiming a right to pre-emptive or preventive war under the guise of self-defence while denying that right to others? None of this denies Iran's record of supporting proxies (Hezbollah, Hamas, Houthis). Nevertheless the US has moved, de facto, into a war that looks like regime change – particularly given targeted strikes against senior Iranian leadership. The apparent calculation was that ordinary Iranians would quickly rise up, the IRGC would surrender and a US-friendly government would emerge. If history teaches anything: bombing can change a ruler, but not the lives of the ruled (Iraq, Afghanistan, Libya). The trend of unilateral interventions severely erodes the aspiration of collective security and sets a dangerous precedent. Economic consequences are great: oil and gas prices up; some countries rationing; hundreds of thousands of migrant workers stranded; displacement from Lebanon and Gaza. For the rule of law to prevail, states – especially powerful ones – must respect international norms consistently. Without that restraint, the international system risks descending into a jungle where only the strongest survive.
