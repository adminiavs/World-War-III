## Full story

Australian farmers have warned the Middle East conflict poses a greater threat to the nation's food security than disruptions to global oil supplies, as vital fertiliser shipments become caught in a major trade battle. The closure of trade-rich sea lanes through the Strait of Hormuz by Iran is causing alarm across the agricultural sector, with the blockage choking off almost one quarter of the globe's traded nitrogen fertiliser, urea. NSW farmer Jacob Wright said the situation is deeply concerning on multiple fronts: "To get the crop in the ground, the tractor's got to have diesel in it." But the fertiliser shortage presents an even more dire scenario. "We're worried. We're worried that prices could double or triple. We're worried that we might not even be able to get it," Wright said. Queensland farmer Brendan Taylor: "Expensive urea is bad. No urea is possibly catastrophic from a food security point of view." He fears already rising fertiliser prices will build into a cost shock most Australians are not expecting. "It would absolutely flow on into the price of bread, into the price of beef and meat, chicken," Taylor warned. The issue sparked heated debate in Parliament on Thursday. Energy Minister Chris Bowen said "It is a sad story that urea manufacturing closed while the Member for Hume was Minister for Energy." Nationals Senator Matt Canavan argued "the government should be stockpiling fertiliser while at least we don't make it ourselves." The minister responded that Australia holds five weeks of fertiliser stocks, with twelve more weeks privately held, and that the nation is well prepared with fuel supplies; critics dismissed this as "nonsense." Tony Seabrook, former president of the Pastoralists and Graziers Association of WA: "How can it possibly be that we've got two small refineries working, now producing about 10 per cent of what we need? What happened? What the hell happened?" The vulnerability of supply lines was further highlighted as Maersk, the world's second largest container shipping company, suspended most of its shipping to and from seven Middle Eastern countries as the conflict around Iran intensifies.
