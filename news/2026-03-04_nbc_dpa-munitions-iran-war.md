## Full story

Concerns over depleting munitions may lead the Trump administration to force defense companies to produce more weaponry quickly. In private briefings with Congress, officials have discussed invoking the Defense Production Act to accelerate munitions production for Operation Epic Fury.

Gen. Dan Caine, chairman of the Joint Chiefs, privately raised with Trump before the strikes that the U.S. stockpile of air defenses was lower after the 12-Day War in June and that a sustained defense against Iran now could put the U.S. at risk. Administration officials told Congress this week that more munitions are needed and that U.S. production has been slow.

Trump said "we have unlimited middle and upper ammunition"; a White House official later said "middle and upper" referred to the range of the munitions. A senior White House official said Trump has not personally discussed invoking the DPA but wants defense contractors to manufacture munitions as quickly as possible.

Kelly Grieco (Stimson Center) estimates the U.S. has deployed about half of its interceptor missiles to the Middle East and could run out in about a month if used at rates similar to the 12-Day War. Allies such as Saudi Arabia and the UAE use the same Patriot systems, creating a large replenishment backlog. Mark Montgomery (FDD): the DPA could give flexibility to draw down reserves if stockpiles can be rebuilt quickly; "That would solve next year's problem." The concern is largely about defensive missiles and interceptors.
