## tl;dr

Panel discussion on whether **control of Iran's oil and gas** is a meaningful part of US war aims, prompted by **Lindsey Graham's** public remark that post-regime-change control over Iranian and Venezuelan reserves would be profitable and strategically damaging to China. The report segment states that Iran is the **ninth largest oil producer**, holds roughly **209 billion barrels** of proven crude reserves (**~12% of global total**), and has the **world's second-largest natural-gas reserves** (**~17% of global total**), much of it centered on the giant **South Pars** field shared with Qatar. It notes that prior to the war Iran exported **1.5-2 million b/d**, over **90% to China**, accounting for roughly **13% of Chinese oil imports**. The panel then splits: Tehran-based Fouad Izadi argues oil and balkanization are central to US/Israeli aims; Paulo von Schirach in Washington argues Graham is not policy and that actually seizing and securing Iranian energy infrastructure would be politically and operationally extremely difficult; Mohammad Reza Farzanegan in Hamburg argues years of sanctions and now war have weakened the reformist/pro-diplomacy camp, making any pro-US regime outcome less likely, not more.

## Summary (key claims and themes)

- **Resource motive openly stated:** Graham's public comments are treated as unusually explicit evidence that oil and gas sit inside at least part of the US elite's strategic imagination.
- **China angle:** Iran's exports to China make Iranian energy not just a revenue source but a geopolitical lever in US-China competition.
- **Venezuela comparison:** Panel explicitly discusses whether Washington is imagining an Iran version of the Venezuela model, and why that analogy may be misleading.
- **Sanctions backfire:** Farzanegan argues sanctions and war have weakened reformist and middle-class constituencies, strengthening hardliners instead.
- **Operational problem:** Even if oil control is desired, physically seizing, securing, and commercializing Iranian energy assets under wartime conditions would require far more than airstrikes.
- **Hormuz and reconstruction:** Izadi argues Iran may try to force compensation or reconstruction mechanisms rather than allowing a return to "normal" low-cost Gulf shipping.

## Transcript / summary (user-provided)

Iran has significant oil and gas reserves and is a major supplier to China. A member of the US president's inner circle says control of them is a key American aim. So, how valuable are Iran's natural resources and could they be a factor in US thinking? This is Inside Story.

Hello and welcome to the program. I'm Imran Khan. If oil and gas are the measure of wealth, then Iran is one of the richest countries in the world. US President Donald Trump openly said he attacked Venezuela in January to capture its oil. He said less about Iran's far greater resources. But according to one of his inner circle, control of those is part of US strategy. Senator Lindsey Graham says it's not just their value, but the impact on China, too, which gets an eighth of its oil from the Islamic Republic. Iran says Graham's comments show Washington's intentions.

Control of oil and gas have caused foreign political interference in Iran before: a coup installing the shah before he was toppled in the 1979 revolution. So, what's at stake for Iran and for global energy supplies? Is seizure or control of Iranian oil and gas on Donald Trump's agenda?

The report segment says Iran is the ninth largest oil producer in the world, responsible for around 4.5% of global supplies. Until the joint US-Israeli attacks, Iran exported between 1.5 and 2 million barrels a day, with more than 90% going to China. The country has up to 209 billion barrels of proven crude reserves, representing about 12% of the global total. It also has the world's second largest known reserves of natural gas, roughly 17% of the global total as of early 2026. Most of it comes from the offshore South Pars gas field, the biggest in the world, which Iran explores jointly with Qatar.

Sanctions have limited Iran's markets, and China is by far its number one customer. But Iran is vital for China, too, accounting for around 13% of its oil imports. 90% of Iran's crude export is refined on Kharg Island and shipped through the Strait of Hormuz, a major maritime route for world energy supplies from the Gulf. With the conflict leading to the closure of this vital waterway, oil and gas prices have surged worldwide. Any prolonged shutdown of Iranian supplies will hit China hard, causing a wider impact given its importance in global trade.

Fouad Izadi in Tehran says Graham's comments are useful because they confirm what many Iranians already believed: that the aim is domination of resources, a return to a kind of 19th-century Persian Gulf order, leverage against China, and possibly even a balkanization of Iran focused on the oil-rich south. He argues the new people in charge in Iran will insist that every cent of damage inflicted on Iran must be repaid, whether through a reconstruction fund or some other mechanism, and that the era in which Iran absorbed costs while everyone else enjoyed low oil prices is over.

Mohammad Reza Farzanegan in Hamburg argues there may once have been factions within Iran more open to diplomacy with the West, but sanctions and now war have weakened those groups and strengthened hardliners. The continuation of war makes the scenario Trump is looking for — a pliable pro-Western arrangement in Tehran — less likely, not more. He says the same dynamic has weakened the middle class and reformist groups rather than producing political moderation.

Paulo von Schirach in Washington says Graham is not in the executive branch and his remarks should not automatically be read as official US policy. He argues that while America and Israel have dealt major military damage to Iran, actually seizing Iranian oil and gas or installing a cooperative regime that could safely host Western companies is a very long shot. Unlike Venezuela, Iran would require not just military victory but prolonged territorial control, security for workers and infrastructure, and a politically saleable level of casualties — all difficult under US domestic conditions, especially with elections approaching. He stresses that Venezuela and Iran are not analytically equivalent cases.

The panel closes on the point that the war is not just about military balances but about control, sanctions, China, regional hegemony, and who pays for destruction. Izadi argues Iran will no longer accept a system in which others attack it and then resume enjoying Gulf shipping and cheap energy as normal; Farzanegan emphasizes the war is strengthening hardliners and narrowing reformist options; and von Schirach emphasizes that even if oil control is desired, the means to achieve it are much less clear than hawks suggest.
