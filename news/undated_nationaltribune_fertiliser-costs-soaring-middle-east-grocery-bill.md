## Full story

Conflict between the United States, Israel and Iran has led to the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz, a key shipping route through which a fifth of the world's oil passes. The Middle East is also a key supplier of fertiliser, exporting some 45% of global supply. Since the conflict began, the price of urea, a key source of nitrogen used in agriculture, has surged by about 25%.

Australia is a large importer of urea. With effectively no domestic urea production, Australia is fully exposed to global supply shocks. After Incitec Pivot shut down its Gibson Island facility near Brisbane in 2022, the country was left with virtually no domestic production. Perdaman's Project Ceres in Western Australia is not expected online until 2027. More than half of Australia's urea imports come from UAE, Qatar and Saudi Arabia, all impacted by the conflict and shipping disruption. For farmers the crisis is not just affordability but potentially availability; winter crop planting is starting soon.

David Ubilava (Associate Professor of Economics, University of Sydney) notes that in this century urea prices have surged substantially twice before; in both instances, for wheat, the price spikes were followed by an increase in production. Urea and fertilisers are among many inputs; the key input in agricultural production is weather. A recent study found that in high-income countries such as Australia, commercial producers are able and willing to absorb increased input costs; fertiliser demand and farm profitability were not substantially affected by the 2021–2022 fertiliser price surge, partly because of high agricultural commodity prices. In Australia the price of food is largely determined by the cost of processing, packaging and marketing, not the prices paid to farmers. The Reserve Bank of Australia says it is too early to say what the conflict could mean for inflation. A surge in urea prices may not by itself drive food prices higher but will not help ease other inflationary pressures.
