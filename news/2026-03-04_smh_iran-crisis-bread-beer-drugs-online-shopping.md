## Full story

Australian farmers are bracing for a global fertiliser crisis that will hit the price of bread and beer, as soaring shipping costs threaten to drive up prices for pharmaceuticals and online shopping orders. US strikes on Iran have derailed fertiliser exports from the Middle East, where around 45 per cent of the world's fertiliser supply is produced. The timing could not be worse for local farmers, with price spikes biting just as rain kickstarts the winter cropping season.

Shoppers are likely to feel the pinch as the rising costs of growing wheat, barley and canola flow through to higher prices for bread and beer. Freight disruptions have sent the cost of transporting freight skyrocketing. Shipping companies have already introduced conflict and war risk surcharges, ranging from $US2000 on a standard 20-foot container up to $US4000 on refrigerated containers. Pharmaceuticals and online shopping orders are likely to be slow to arrive and more expensive. More than 150 vessels are anchored to avoid potential attack; three have been damaged by drone strikes.

The Middle East produces about half the world's urea and about one-fifth of the world's nitrogen. On Tuesday a senior Iranian official said the Strait of Hormuz was closed and Iran would fire on any ship trying to pass. Rabobank analyst Paul Joules: the Iran war could drive greater price spikes than when Russia invaded Ukraine; Russia only produces about 12 per cent of global urea compared to 45 per cent from the Middle East. Qatar has halted LNG exports; gas is crucial for fertiliser manufacture. Australia imports around 90 per cent of its urea. National Farmers Federation president Hamish McIntyre: highlights the importance of having more manufacturing and refining in Australia to ride out these highs and lows.

(SMH, 4 Mar 2026. Mike Foley, Elias Visontay.)
