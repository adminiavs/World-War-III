## Full story

Tehran is moving to restrict or effectively close the Strait of Hormuz to shipping as part of the latest escalation. Markets have reacted to the impact on oil and gas flows, crude prices and inflation. That concern is justified but captures only part of the story. A sustained disruption would not simply be an energy crisis; it would be a **fertiliser shock** (prices up, supply down) and a direct risk to global food security. Modern agriculture runs on natural gas. Haber and Bosch's nitrogen fixation launched a chemical revolution that remains a cornerstone of civilisation and agriculture: methane to ammonia to nitrogen fertilisers such as urea, which allow the yields on which the global population depends. Without it, harvests of wheat, maize and rice would fall dramatically. Around a third of globally traded urea passes through the Strait of Hormuz. The Persian Gulf is central for two reasons: access to some of the world's cheapest natural gas for ammonia production, and decades of capital investment in ammonia and urea capacity in Qatar, Saudi Arabia and the UAE for export. A significant share of globally traded nitrogen fertiliser and the LNG that powers fertiliser plants elsewhere must transit the strait. A closure would threaten not only oil and gas exports but the physical flow of nitrogen fertilisers and their inputs. Immediate effects: delays or stoppage of ammonia, urea and LNG shipments, or prohibitively high freight and insurance. The deeper impact would unfold over months on farms: in the northern hemisphere, fertiliser purchases accelerate before planting; a delay of weeks is disruptive, of months decisive. If shipments fail to arrive on time, farmers face sharply higher prices, reduced application rates or different crop mixes. Because of crop response, even modest reductions in nitrogen can produce disproportionately large yield declines, millions of tonnes of lost crops, and ripple effects through feed, livestock, biofuels and retail food prices. India relies heavily on Gulf LNG for domestic urea plants; Brazil depends substantially on imported nitrogen and phosphate for soy and maize; the US imports meaningful ammonia and urea; in sub-Saharan Africa, higher fertiliser prices would likely reduce already low use and increase food insecurity. The system's fragility extends beyond nitrogen: sulphur (essential for plant growth) is largely a byproduct of oil and gas processing; if energy shipments through Hormuz are disrupted, sulphur output falls. Synthetic nitrogen is tightly coupled to energy markets; disruption in gas or ammonia trade immediately constrains global nitrogen availability. Without synthetic nitrogen the world could feed only a fraction of its current population. The Strait of Hormuz sits at the intersection of energy and food security. Building new ammonia capacity takes years; a double-digit contraction in exports from a key region cannot be swiftly offset. In the interim, prices rise, trade re-routes and planting decisions are made under uncertainty; food price inflation, historically correlated with social unrest, could intensify. Central banks focused on fuel-driven inflation could underestimate fertiliser's contribution; fertiliser shocks do not register as fast as oil (crop yields reveal themselves months later) but may prove more destabilising. If the 20th century taught policymakers to fear oil embargoes, the 21st should teach them to fear a fertiliser shock; the global food system has far thinner buffers than energy. (The Conversation, 5 Mar 2026; Nima Shokri, Salome M. S. Shokri-Kuehni.)
