## Full story

The U.S. Navy is facing a high-stakes "firepower gap" as it approaches the mandatory retirement of the Ohio-class fleet. As of March 2026, the Navy's strategy to maintain undersea dominance relies on extending the lives of 40-year-old "boomers" to bridge the delay-plagued transition to the Columbia-class. With the concurrent retirement of Ticonderoga-class cruisers, the U.S. faces a net loss of over 2,000 VLS cells, a deficit that the Block V Virginia-class -- the second-largest submarine in U.S. history -- cannot immediately offset due to a 30% reduction in shipyard capacity since the Gulf War.

The Navy plans to retire all four Ohio-class SSGNs by 2027, creating a VLS (Vertical Launch System) shortage. With the additional retirement of the Ticonderoga-class cruisers by 2030, there will be a net loss of 2,080 VLS cells for Tomahawks and other missiles (616 from SSGNs, 1,464 from cruisers). The SSGNs each carry 154 Tomahawk cruise missiles; the cruisers carry 122 Tomahawks and can also launch Standard and Evolved Sea Sparrow surface-to-air missiles, ASROC, and Tomahawk land-attack cruise missiles. The Ohio-class SSGNs also support Special Operations Forces (e.g. lock-out chambers, Dry Deck Shelter, capacity for 66 SEALs or Green Berets).

The Columbia-class submarines are slated to replace the 40-year-old Ohio-class missile boats. Defense contractors continue to announce delays and cost overruns due to a shortage of qualified shipyard workers and supply chain disruptions. The total cost of the Columbia program is estimated at around $130 billion for 12 submarines. The first boat may be delivered over a year late and cost hundreds of millions more than planned. Building both Columbia-class and Virginia-class attack submarines concurrently presents industrial base challenges. The Ohio-class will begin retirement in 2027; the first Columbia SSBN must be added by 2030 to support the deterrent. The Ohio-class boats must be monitored for hull weakening and metal fatigue; reactor refuelling and refurbishment are costly.

The Block V Virginia-class adds an 83-foot Virginia Payload Module (VPM), increasing missile tubes from 12 to 40 per boat, but the timeline for a substantial number of Block V boats to be operational means there will likely be a several-year gap between the retirement of the last Ohio-class SSGN and full deployment of replacement capacity.

American shipyards have shrunk by 30 percent since the Gulf War. The author argues the government must invest in shipyards, strengthen the supply chain, and address the shortage of skilled workers; extending the Ohio-class until replacement capability is in place is a strategic necessity. "The loss of this many VLS cells would leave the Navy in too precarious a position."
