## Full story

Australians are being urged to think carefully about their travel plans for the next five months, especially trips to Europe, as the rapidly escalating conflict in the Middle East begins to spread.

Foreign Minister Penny Wong warned the situation was evolving far more quickly than many governments anticipated, with Iran now having launched attacks across 11 countries, including a NATO member. "Certainly the conflict is spreading, and that's of deep concern," Senator Wong told Sunrise on Thursday. "Iran attacking a NATO member, which now means it has sought to strike 11 countries, is deeply concerning."

The warning comes as the conflict continues to expand beyond the region, with the United States using a torpedo to sink an Iranian warship off the coast of Sri Lanka in the Indian Ocean on the same day NATO deployed its defences to intercept an Iranian ballistic missile headed for Turkey.

Senator Wong said the speed and the scale of the escalation had caught many by surprise. "The conflict has spread much more quickly and much more widely than we anticipated," she said. "No one would have anticipated that in the first 72 hours Iran would have struck nine countries." "No one would have anticipated that Iran would have responded in the way it has, which it has not done before, which is to attack travel hubs such as the United Arab Emirates," she said.

Wong said the government's priority remains the safety of Australians currently in the Middle East, with one evacuation flight returning home overnight and three more scheduled to depart from the United Arab Emirates over the coming days. She did warn that these remain subject to last-minute cancellation as the situation continues to evolve. "At the moment, the conflict is unpredictable," she said. "People do need to think very carefully about their travel plans and continue to look at the advice we provide."

Chairman of Avlaw Consulting and former head of Safety and Regulation at Qantas, Professor Ron Bartsch, warned Australia would feel the disruption more than most countries due to reliance on European stopovers, with airfares predicted to rise significantly. "What's going to happen now is that obviously Qantas and Virgin's sharing arrangements will try to deviate more towards other alternate routes through Bangkok, Singapore, Hong Kong and the Philippines in order to reach Europe," he told the Herald Sun. "The longer this military operation goes on means airfares will increase accordingly." Bartsch said the disruption to global aviation could last for at least the next four to five months.

Senator Wong said the escalating conflict was a reminder of why Australia continues to strengthen its defence capability, pointing to the AUKUS partnership with the United States and the United Kingdom. "We continue to invest in Australia's defence capability," she said. "This is a reminder of why the AUKUS partnership to enable Australia to replace its existing submarine capability with a new submarine capability matters, deterrence matters."

On Tuesday, Deputy Prime Minister and Defence Minister Richard Marles worked to reassure Australians that the conflict did not currently pose a direct threat to the country. He confirmed Australia had not been asked to provide any direct military assistance in the conflict, which is currently being undertaken by the United States and Israel. "We are taking this literally a day at a time... and keeping our minds open about what the possibilities are," he said. Reiterating previous statements from Prime Minister Anthony Albanese, Marles said Australia continues to support the United States' actions. "At its core, this is about preventing Iran from ever being able to acquire a deployable nuclear weapon," Marles said. "Were Iran ever to acquire that it would be an utter catastrophe for the world... So we are supporting the United States-led action in relation to this." Despite this support, the government has maintained that Australia will not deploy boots on the ground in the conflict at this time.
