## Full story

The U.S. and Israel have discussed sending special forces into Iran to secure its stockpile of highly enriched uranium at a later stage of the war, according to four sources with knowledge of the discussions. Preventing Iran from ever obtaining a nuclear weapon is one of President Trump stated war objectives. The regime 450 kilograms of 60%-enriched uranium, convertible to weapons grade within weeks, is one key to that goal.

Any operation to seize the material would likely require U.S. or Israeli troops on Iranian soil at heavily fortified underground facilities, and would only take place after both countries are confident Iran military can no longer mount a serious threat. At a congressional briefing Tuesday, Secretary of State Marco Rubio was asked whether Iran enriched uranium would be secured. People are going to have to go and get it, he said. The mission would likely involve special operators alongside scientists, possibly from the IAEA. A U.S. official said the administration has discussed two options: removing the material from Iran entirely, or bringing in nuclear experts to dilute it on-site. An Israeli defense official said Trump and his team are seriously considering sending special operations units into Iran for specific missions.

Two sources said such operations were part of a menu of options presented to Trump before the war. Semafor and NBC News had reported Trump interest in Special Operations raids on nuclear sites and a small contingent of U.S. troops for specific strategic purposes.

Trump told reporters aboard Air Force One on Saturday that ground troops were possible for a very good reason. Asked specifically whether troops might go in to secure nuclear material, Trump did not rule it out. At some point maybe we will. We would not do it now. Maybe we will do it later. If we ever did that, the Iranians would be so decimated that they would not be able to fight on the ground level.

Beyond the uranium, administration officials tell Axios there has also been discussion of seizing Kharg Island, responsible for roughly 90% of Iran crude oil exports.

The U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran nuclear facilities last June buried Iran uranium stockpile under rubble; Iranians have not been able to reach it since. In the opening days of the war, strikes on Natanz and Isfahan appeared aimed at sealing entrances. Most of the stockpile sits in underground tunnels at Isfahan, with the rest at Fordow and Natanz. Strikes destroyed nearly all centrifuges; no evidence enrichment has resumed. If the entire stockpile reached 90% purity, it would be enough for 11 nuclear bombs.

A senior U.S. official said: Boots on the ground for Trump is not the same as what it means for the media. What has been discussed has not been thought of in terms of boots on the ground. People think Fallujah. That is not what has been discussed. Small special ops raids, not a big force going in.
