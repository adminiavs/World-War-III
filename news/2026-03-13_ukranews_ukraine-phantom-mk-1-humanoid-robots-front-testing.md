## Full story

The American company **Foundation** has transferred two **Phantom MK-1 humanoid robots** to Ukraine to test their use in combat conditions. This was reported by **Time** (March 2026) with reference to company representatives.

The robots were transferred to Ukraine in **February 2026**. Foundation called Ukraine a **key platform for testing new military technologies** in real conditions of modern warfare.

**Planned use at the front:** Developers position humanoid platforms as a tool for tasks where conventional drones may be less effective. Possible scenarios: **delivery of equipment**, **reconnaissance in closed spaces** (shelters, bunkers), and **working with infrastructure built for humans**. The company also states a further development goal: in the future the robot should be able to **use any weapon used by a person**.

The technology is described as **early stage**: such machines remain expensive, have significant weight, depend on batteries, and can lose balance or suffer technical failures.

**Phantom MK-1 characteristics:**
- Speed: almost 4 mph (6.44 km/h)
- Payload: up to 44 lb (~20 kg)
- Weight: 175 lb (~79 kg)
- Height: 5 ft 9 in (~175 cm)

**Cybersecurity risks:** If an adversary gains access to software vulnerabilities or communication channels, the robotic system could theoretically be used against its operators.

**AI and use of force:** Experts quoted by Time note limitations of AI models — they can make mistakes, show bias, or operate beyond set limits in complex environments. In the US, the principle is that **the final decision on the use of force should be made by a person**.

**Phantom MK-2:** Foundation plans to introduce the next model in **April 2026** — Phantom MK-2, with enhanced electronics protection, water resistance, improved batteries, and capacity to carry up to **80 kg** of cargo.

The article also notes that OpenAI plans to change its agreement with the Pentagon to directly prohibit use of its AI systems for deliberate domestic surveillance of US citizens.
