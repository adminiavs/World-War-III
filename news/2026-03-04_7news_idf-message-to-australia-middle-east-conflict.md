## Full story

The operation against Iran will not stop until the mission is complete, the Israel Defence Force chief international spokesperson has told 7NEWS.

The Middle East conflict is continuing to escalate with America warning the hardest hits are yet to come as Iran threatens to blow up more oil tankers and close the crucial Strait of Hormuz.

But Israel says the fighting will not stop until the Iranian regime is felled.

"We're fighting for freedom, for stability, and it's an Israeli problem right now," Israel Defence Force chief international spokesperson Nadav Shoshani told 7NEWS.

"It could be an Australian problem tomorrow."

## Conflict expands to Lebanon

It comes as fighting continues on Tuesday afternoon local time with Israel launching "simultaneous" strikes on Tehran, Iran's capital, and Beirut in Lebanon.

The Israeli Air Force said the strikes were targeting "military objectives" of the Iranian regime and Iran-backed Hezbollah.

But a senior Hezbollah official says that after more than a year of abiding by the ceasefire, the latest strikes have seen the group's patience end, leaving it with no option "but to return to resistance" and fight an open war with Israel.

Mohamoud Komati said that Hezbollah exercised patience since a ceasefire ended the Israel-Hezbollah war in November 2024, hoping the government's diplomatic efforts would yield positive results in ending Israeli strikes.

In the comments released by Hezbollah's media office, Komati blasted the Lebanese government for calling Hezbollah's actions illegal and demanded it hand over its weapons, saying it did not act to stop Israel's airstrikes that continued on almost daily basis for nearly 15 months.

"The Zionist enemy wanted an open war, which it has not stopped since the ceasefire agreement," Komati said. "So let it be an open war."

Thousands of Syrians have crossed from Lebanon into Syria to flee Israeli strikes over the past two days as Israel and Hezbollah escalated their attacks against each other.

The UN refugee agency, UNHCR, said in a statement that around 3900 to 4400 people would typically cross from Lebanon into Syria during Ramadan.

But on Monday, after Hezbollah launched missiles toward Israel and Israel retaliated with bombarding Lebanon, a total of 10,629 people crossed, the vast majority of them Syrian.

Azzam Sweiri, a Syrian farm worker who had been working in southern Lebanon, crossed back into Syria Tuesday.

"The streets were packed with cars and people" as he fled, he said. "It took us 10 or 12 hours just to make it 30 or 40 kilometers."

After he left, he said, the house next to the one where he lived was hit by an Israeli airstrike.

Israeli troops crossed the border into Lebanon on Tuesday afternoon, though the IDF denies it's a ground invasion, describing it instead as creating a buffer zone.

## Strikes continue in retaliation from Iran

Meanwhile, sirens have sounded across Israel as Iran continues to launch missiles at the country on Tuesday afternoon local time.

Other countries in the Middle East have also continued to receive the barrage of airstrikes with a drone hitting Oman's largest port of Salalah.

The government media office also said two drones were shot down in the southwestern province of Dhofar.

The attacks left no casualties or damage in both Salalah and Dhofar, it said.

The United Arab Emirates said that it possesses all defence capabilities and ammunition stockpiles to protect itself "regardless of the time frame and the length of the escalation period in the region".

The country's defence ministry said in a briefing that it has so far repelled hundreds of ballistic missiles and drones fired into the country.

It said a total of 186 missiles and 812 drones were fired towards the country since the weekend.

Ministry spokesperson Abdel Nassir al-Hameedi said injuries that resulted from the Iranian attacks and what he called "minor damages" were the result of shrapnel from interception efforts, not a result of successful attacks against the country.
