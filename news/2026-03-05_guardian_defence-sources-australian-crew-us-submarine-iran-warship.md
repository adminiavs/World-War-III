## Full story

The Australian government has refused to disclose whether Australian sailors or officers were onboard the US attack submarine that torpedoed and sank an Iranian warship in the Indian Ocean, killing at least 87 people. More than 50 Australian sailors and officers are serving across the US attack submarine fleet, a training regimen that is part of preparations for Australia to command its own nuclear-powered submarines under the AUKUS deal. According to the Royal Australian Navy, one in 10 crew members onboard US Navy attack submarines is Australian. Defence sources have told the Guardian they believe two Australians were on board the submarine. If Australians were onboard, it would be the first direct involvement of Australian defence personnel in the current conflict. The foreign affairs minister, Penny Wong, told the Senate that US submarine actions were a matter for the US Navy. "For operational and security reasons, we don't disclose specific information regarding Australian personnel." The US submarine strike on the IRIS Dena was the first time an enemy vessel has been sunk by an American torpedo since the second world war, US secretary of war Pete Hegseth told a Pentagon briefing. The Pentagon has not identified which submarine was involved; defence trade press reported the submarine was the USS Minnesota, a Virginia-class submarine that rotated through HMAS Stirling in Western Australia last year. Up to 180 personnel were believed on board the Iranian frigate, which was sailing in international waters returning from a naval exercise organised by India in the Bay of Bengal. Emma Shortis, director of international and security affairs at the Australia Institute, said she felt it was "highly likely" an Australian citizen was part of the crew "but the point is we don't know." "This whole deal, the whole AUKUS optimal pathway, is shrouded in secrecy. And I think it's not only the point that the Australian people don't know, I think it's possible the Australian government doesn't know: that they didn't have advance warning of this and they don't actually know, as we don't, what kind of submarine this was, and if it was a Virginia class, whether there are Australians on board." Shortis said the Trump administration had a history of not sharing information on military strikes with allies. "In a democracy, the Australian people have a right to know if Australia is being dragged into any war, let alone one that is so transparently illegal like this one." On Friday the minister for home affairs, Tony Burke, said that as a matter of policy the government would not confirm the location of Australian military personnel on any operation carried out by allies. "We're certainly not directing any involvement in this conflict." Resources minister Madeleine King said it "really isn't appropriate to go into those details" but confirmed Australians were "absolutely" training with the US Navy under AUKUS; for operational security the ADF does not and should not disclose specific details regarding third-country deployments. Two US P-8A Poseidon aircraft arrived at RAAF Base Pearce on Monday from Diego Garcia; neither Australian nor US militaries have disclosed the purpose of the stopover or their role in the conflict.
