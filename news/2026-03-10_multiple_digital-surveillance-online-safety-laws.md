# Digital Surveillance and Online Safety Laws: Wartime Context

**Date:** 10 March 2026 (background compilation)  
**Sources:** UK Government, Freedom House, Guardian, CPJ, Cato Institute, ITIF, multiple others  
**Type:** Background analysis on information control infrastructure

---

## Overview

This compilation documents global digital surveillance and online safety regulatory frameworks now available during wartime. Key finding: infrastructure built for "child safety" and "online harms" is available for wartime censorship.

---

## UK Online Safety Act 2023

**Source: UK Government Explainer**

### Key Provisions

- Imposes "duty of care" on social media companies and search services
- Requires platforms to remove illegal content and protect children from harmful content
- Implement age verification for adult content sites
- Penalties: Up to 18 million pounds or 10% of worldwide revenue
- Criminal action against non-compliant senior managers
- Ofcom can require payment providers/ISPs to stop working with non-compliant sites

### Encryption Concerns (CPJ)

- Empowers regulators to require "client-side scanning" of encrypted messages
- Journal of Cybersecurity: would enable "bulk surveillance of everyone's private data"
- WhatsApp and Signal threatened to withdraw from UK if forced to comply
- Home Office: "End-to-end encryption poses an unacceptable risk"

### Actual Consequences (Guardian, ITIF, SiliconAngle, TechRadar)

- VPN usage spiked: NordVPN 1,000% increase, ProtonVPN 1,800% increase
- Sharp increases in searches for fake IDs, dark web access
- Urban Dead (zombie game) shut down over compliance concerns
- Minors blocked from mental health and suicide prevention content
- TechRadar: Ofcom now "collecting information about VPN adoption" and may take "further action"

## Australia Online Safety Amendment (Social Media Minimum Age) Act 2024

**Source: Wikipedia, Cato Institute, Freedom House**

### Key Provisions

- Prohibits under-16s from social media accounts
- Penalties: Up to A$49.5 million for non-compliant companies
- No parental consent exceptions; no penalties for circumventing users
- Age verification required by December 2025

### Affected Platforms

Facebook, Instagram, Kick, Reddit, Snapchat, Threads, TikTok, Twitch, X, YouTube

### Legal Challenges

- Reddit and Digital Freedom Project filed High Court challenges
- Argument: violates implied freedom of political communication
- VPN circumvention widespread despite eSafety Commissioner claims

### Public Opinion

- 70% voters endorse ban but only 33% confident it will work
- Behind the News poll: 75% of teens don't plan to stop using social media

## Australia eSafety Commissioner Powers

**Sources: AIIA Analysis, AP News**

### X Corp Legal Battle

- eSafety Commissioner ordered X to remove violent Wakely attack video
- X geoblocked for Australians but did not globally remove
- December 2025 Appeals Court: Unanimously rejected X's appeal
- Grant: "obligations to comply with Australian regulations still apply"

### Enforcement

- X fined A$610,500 for child exploitation content failures
- Grant driving "world-first" social media ban legislation

## Surveillance Legislation Review (Australia)

**Sources: APH Bills Digest, Law Council**

### Telecommunications Amendment Bill 2025

- Transfers Communications Access Coordinator to Home Affairs
- Allows agencies to access stored communications for "development and testing"
- Expands when warrant information can be used in prosecutions

### SLAID Act Review

- INSLM reviewing network activity warrants, data disruption powers
- Law Council supports harmonised surveillance act

## US Parallel: Kids Online Safety Act (KOSA)

**Source: JD Supra**

- Mirrors UK/EU/Australian approaches
- Senate version imposes "duty of care" including preventing "compulsive usage"
- 40 state/territorial AGs urging passage (Feb 2026)
- Amnesty: "Banning TikTok does nothing to address endemic human rights risks"

## Relevance to Iran War Predictions

### Information Control Infrastructure

1. Age verification and "harmful content" rules create takedown mechanisms
2. UK OSA client-side scanning threatens journalist-source communications
3. Platform compliance under pressure - X Corp battles show governments can compel takedowns
4. VPN surge as resistance; Ofcom now monitoring VPN adoption

### Specific War Connections

- Iran's 1% internet connectivity mirrors worst-case enforcement scenario
- Australia's emergency visa-ban legislation parallels emergency powers expansion
- eSafety powers tested during wartime content moderation
- Salt Typhoon hacking compromised telecoms during war

### Predictions Reinforced

- Propaganda/information control infrastructure exists
- Domestic civil liberties erosion via online safety laws
- Digital surveillance intensification as defensive response
