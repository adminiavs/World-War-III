Do the math: at 3.1 miles per second, 990 miles takes 4 minutes and 20 seconds.

And there is no defensive system currently deployed in Israel or anywhere else that can reliably intercept a target moving at Mach 15 while maneuvering during its terminal approach. The Fata 2 is not Iran's first hypersonic missile. Iran unveiled the Fata 1 last year with Mach 13 capability and a 1,400 km range. The Fata 2 is the upgraded variant. Same range, higher speed, better maneuverability, and most critically, a maneuverable re-entry vehicle that changes its flight path multiple times during descent. That last feature is what makes interception mathematically impossible with current technology.

Here's how missile defense works when it works. A radar detects an incoming missile. The system calculates where that missile will be in 30 seconds based on its current trajectory and speed. An interceptor launches toward the predicted intercept point. The interceptor arrives at that point at the exact moment the incoming missile is supposed to be there. They collide. The threat is neutralized. This works when the incoming missile follows a predictable ballistic arc. It does not work when the incoming missile changes direction mid-flight.

The FAT 2 doesn't fly a ballistic arc. It glides. After the initial boost phase, the missile separates from its rocket booster and enters a glide phase where small control surfaces adjust its trajectory continuously. Not huge course corrections, small adjustments, 5 degrees left, 3 degrees right, 2 degrees down. Each adjustment is tiny, but each adjustment means the missile is no longer where the defensive system predicted it would be. And when you're trying to hit a target moving at Mach 15, being wrong by 3 degrees means missing by miles.

Israel's Arrow 3 interceptor system is designed to engage ballistic missiles at altitudes above 100 km during the midcourse phase of flight. The Fata 2 glides at altitudes below 50 km during most of its flight. Arrow 3 cannot engage it effectively in that flight regime. David's Sling is designed for medium-range threats at lower altitudes. But David's Sling interceptors are optimized for targets moving at Mach 5 to Mach 8. The FAT 2 is doing Mach 15. The closing speed is wrong. The intercept geometry doesn't work. Iron Dome is designed for short-range rockets and small drones. It's not even designed to attempt engagement against hypersonic missiles.

The result is a missile that no deployed Israeli defensive system can reliably stop. Not because Israeli technology is bad, because the threat moves faster and maneuvers more aggressively than the systems were designed to defeat.

Israel's Home Front Command operates one of the most sophisticated civilian alert systems in the world. The system is designed to detect incoming threats, calculate impact zones, and warn the affected population with enough time to reach shelter before impact. For rockets fired from Gaza, warning time is 15 to 90 seconds, depending on distance. For missiles fired from Lebanon, warning time is 1 to 5 minutes. For ballistic missiles fired from Iran using traditional trajectories, warning time was 10 to 15 minutes. The alert system was calibrated for those time frames. Sirens are positioned based on how far people can move in the available warning time. Shelter locations are designated based on distance from residential areas that can be covered in the warning window. Public messaging tells people they have time to reach shelter if they move immediately when the alert sounds. All of that assumes the warning arrives before the missile.

The Fattah 2 inverts that assumption. The missile arrives before the warning completes. Not because the detection is slow. Israel's radar network detects the launch within seconds. Not because the calculation is slow. Computers calculate trajectories instantly. The delay is in the human systems that happen after detection. Alerts have to be transmitted to cellular networks. Sirens have to be activated. People have to hear the alert, process what it means, and start moving toward shelter. That process takes time. And the Fata 2 doesn't give you that time. By the time the siren sounds, the missile has already hit. The alert becomes notification of an attack that already happened rather than warning of an attack that's coming.

The 4-minute flight time doesn't just break civilian defense. It breaks military response cycles. Israeli doctrine for responding to Iranian missile threats assumes that early warning provides time to launch retaliatory strikes before the Iranian missiles impact. Four-minute flight time eliminates that response cycle. By the time Israeli commanders receive confirmation of a launch, analyze the threat, authorize a retaliatory strike, and get aircraft airborne, the incoming missile has already impacted. The retaliation happens after the damage is done, not before.

That changes the deterrence calculation. Iran no longer has to worry that launching missiles triggers immediate retaliation that arrives before their attack is complete. They can launch, assess the damage, and then decide how to respond to Israeli retaliation. That's a much more favorable operational timeline for Iran than the one that existed when Israeli forces had 10 to 15 minutes of warning.

The 4-minute window also compresses decision-making in ways that increase the risk of catastrophic miscalculation. When Israeli radar detects a Mach 15 object launched from Iran and headed toward Israeli airspace, commanders have four minutes to decide if that object is a conventional warhead or a nuclear warhead. Four minutes to decide if this is a limited strike or the first wave of a total war scenario. Four minutes to decide if the appropriate response is a proportional conventional retaliation or an escalation to weapons of mass destruction.

Those are decisions that should never be made under four-minute time pressure. But the Fata 2 forces exactly that scenario.

The Fattah 2 doesn't just hit its target. It hits the entire framework of assumptions that made living under missile threats survivable.
