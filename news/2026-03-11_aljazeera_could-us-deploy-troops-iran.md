## Full article

Speculation has been mounting in the US about whether American soldiers will be deployed on the ground in Iran as the US-Israeli war entered its 12th day.

Democratic Senator Richard Blumenthal said he was the angriest he had been in his political career after attending a classified Iran war briefing for the Senate Armed Services Committee: "I emerge from this briefing as dissatisfied and angry, frankly, as I have from any past briefing in my 15 years. I am most concerned about the threat to American lives of potentially deploying our sons and daughters on the ground in Iraq. We seem to be on a path toward deploying American troops on the ground in Iran to accomplish any of the potential objectives here."

Senator Chris Murphy: officials claimed the goal was to destroy Iran's military assets but "could not detail any long-term plan."

**What are officials saying?**

Defense Secretary Hegseth (CBS): the US is "willing to go as far as we need to." Press Secretary Leavitt: ground operations are "not part of the plan right now" but Trump was keeping options open. Secretary of State Rubio told a congressional briefing the US needed to physically secure nuclear material in Iran: "People are going to have to go and get it."

Trump spoke to Iranian Kurdish rebel groups based in Iraq along its border with Iran (unclear what was discussed, but analysts said it could involve using Kurdish armed forces as a proxy on the ground).

**Public opinion:** 74% of Americans oppose deploying troops (Quinnipiac). 41% support the conflict overall -- "most unpopular foreign war in modern US history" (NYT poll). Only 25% approved of the war when it began (Reuters-Ipsos).

**How would a ground operation work?**

Iran is four times larger than Iraq and features difficult mountainous terrain. Unlike the Iraq invasion, a mission to physically recover nuclear material would likely be tightly defined, involving small specialised units (Navy SEALs, Army Special Forces). Rapid-deployment forces like the 82nd Airborne Division would secure entry points (airfields, staging areas). Target facilities: Natanz Nuclear Facility, Fordow Fuel Enrichment Plant, Isfahan Nuclear Technology Center, and potentially Kharg Island (handles 90% of Iran's oil exports).

Thomas Bonnie James (professor, AFG College, University of Aberdeen): mission would involve "penetrating hardened facilities, collecting intelligence, and locating or securing sensitive nuclear materials with the overall emphasis on speed, precision and limited exposure." Then rapid exit.

Neil Quilliam (Chatham House): a US ground mission would trigger "a severe response" from Tehran, including more Iranian missile strikes and attacks by proxy groups. "These would be high-risk, complex and lengthy operations taking place in very hostile environments and against facilities heavily protected by the country's security forces."

**Background -- June 2025 (Operation Midnight Hammer):**

Before the current war, the US had already attacked Iran's three biggest nuclear facilities (Fordow, Natanz, Isfahan) in a covert overnight mission. Trump claimed the sites were "obliterated." But IAEA director Rafael Grossi warned that Iran could resume uranium enrichment "within months" as some facilities were "still standing" and it wasn't clear if enriched uranium stockpiles had been moved. This is why physical seizure of nuclear material is being discussed -- air strikes alone cannot destroy the knowledge or the material.
