## Full story

Bahrain has said an Iranian drone attack caused material damage to a water desalination plant in the country, marking the first time a Gulf nation has reported targeting any such facility during the war between Iran and the US and Israel. The attack on Sunday came a day after Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said a freshwater desalination plant on Qeshm Island in southern Iran was attacked by the United States. Water supply in 30 villages has been impacted. Attacking Iran's infrastructure is a dangerous move with grave consequences. The US set this precedent, not Iran, he said on X on Saturday.

While Tehran has not yet commented on the Bahrain attack, it has raised questions about the vulnerability of the Gulf countries, which depend on desalination plants for the majority of their water supply.

Desalination plants convert seawater into water suitable for drinking, irrigation and industrial use via thermal or membrane-based (e.g. reverse osmosis) technologies. GCC states use reverse osmosis as the dominant method. Groundwater and desalinated water account for about 90% of the region's main water resources (Gulf Research Center 2020). As groundwater has deteriorated, Gulf countries rely heavily on energy-intensive seawater desalination. More than 400 desalination plants are located on the Arabian Gulf shores from UAE to Kuwait. GCC member states account for about 60% of global water desalination capacity, producing almost 40% of total desalinated water worldwide (Arab Center Washington DC 2023). About 42% of the UAE's drinking water comes from desalination; Kuwait 90%, Oman 86%, Saudi Arabia 70%. Saudi Arabia produces more desalinated water than any other country. Desalination has enabled economic development and population growth; targeting or disrupting facilities places economic stability and daily life at significant risk.

During the 1990-1991 Gulf War, Iraqi forces intentionally destroyed most of Kuwait's desalination capacity and the damage to its water supply was severe. A 2010 CIA report warned that disruption of desalination facilities in most of the Arab Gulf countries could have more consequences than the loss of any industry or commodity. Raha Hakimdavar (Georgetown Qatar, Earth Commons) said long-term attacks can impact domestic food production and divert water from it; the region is highly food-import dependent and faces potential food security challenges due to Hormuz compromise. Naser Alsayed (environmental researcher) said effects depend on the local scenario: Saudi Arabia has Red Sea facilities and geographic space; UAE has 45 days of water storage per its 2036 strategy; Qatar, Bahrain and Kuwait have minimal strategic reservoirs and would feel impact most acutely. The most significant impact may be psychological; water is essential to life and perception of risk can cause fear and panic. GCC Unified Water Strategy 2035 called for national integrated energy and water plans by 2020 but this has not been fully achieved; experts recommend regional coordination, unified desalination grids, shared strategic reserves, and diversifying water resources. Iran also uses desalination in coastal areas such as Qeshm but has many rivers and dams and is not as heavily reliant as the GCC.
