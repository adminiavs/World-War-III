## Full story

For four days, Iranian missiles and drones have swept Gulf states' cities and infrastructure, sundering ties with Tehran and placing the neighbours on a potential course for greater military confrontation, analysts said.

After years of diplomatic rapprochement and weeks of arguing against war, Gulf countries including Saudi Arabia and the UAE face a dilemma: hit back and risk stiffer attacks, or maintain defence and hope to absorb the blows. "Arab Gulf states are on the frontlines of a war they sought to avert," wrote Middle East expert Hasan Alhasan for the IISS. "A debate is brewing in Gulf capitals about the merits of strategic patience and restraint."

Qatar downed two Iranian fighter jets, the first time a Gulf country has hit manned Iranian planes since the war broke out. Farea Muslimi (Chatham House) told AFP he would be "extremely shocked" if Saudi Arabia "did not respond militarily". Saudi military raised readiness; one source warned of possible military response if oil infrastructure were targeted. On Monday Saudi Arabia halted some operations at Ras Tanura refinery after falling debris from an intercepted attack sparked a fire.

Drone strikes hit the UAE's Fujairah oil refineries on Tuesday; debris sparked a fire in an oil field. The UAE has been targeted by more than 800 drones and 200 missiles since Saturday, bearing the brunt of Iran's campaign. Three dead in the Emirates (eight across the Gulf); fires at The Palm and Burj Al Arab; Abu Dhabi closed embassy in Tehran and recalled its ambassador. UAE political scientist Abdulkhaleq Abdulla: relationship at "the lowest of the low"; "We are definitely angry as hell." Despite being Iran's second-largest trade partner, 63% of projectiles aimed at the six Gulf states landed in the UAE. "There is an understanding now ... that Iran has become an enemy of a sort, has become an aggressor." Before Qatar downed two Su-24s, QatarEnergy had suspended production after drones hit two key facilities. Qatar foreign ministry spokesman Majed Ansari: all Qatar's "red lines have already been crossed"; "We reserve the right to retaliate"; leaders "considering right now all the options".
