## Full story

The girls' school in Iran where 165 people were killed by an apparent US-Israeli attack was hit with two strikes, with the second missile killing sheltering survivors, two first responders and the parent of a slain child have told Middle East Eye.

When the first bomb hit the school, one of the teachers and the principal moved a group of students to the prayer hall to protect them. The principal called the parents and told them to come and pick up their children. But the second bomb hit that area as well. Only a small number of those who had taken shelter survived.

Almost all the 165 people killed were girls aged between seven and 12. There were around 170 girls at the school in southern Iran's Minab at the time. Rohollah, the father of a girl killed in the second attack, told MEE he was contacted after the school was already attacked. His daughter survived the first strike and was moved to the prayer hall; the second strike hit before he could reach her. We could only identify her from her school bag, which she was still holding. She was completely burned. MEE is not disclosing identities for security reasons; all names given are false.

Two strikes on the same target with a pause so that medics and civilians arriving are killed in the follow-up are often called double-tap strikes, a tactic that is a war crime. It has been used by Israel in Gaza and the US elsewhere. The US and Israeli militaries have not replied to MEE. Marco Rubio said US forces would not deliberately target a school. Israel and the US said they are investigating. The school is Shajareh Tayyebeh (the good tree). Claims that the IRGC bombed the school have been debunked. Satellite imagery shows the school and at least two IRGC sites around 200 metres away; both were struck. Both medics said they are certain more girls were killed in the first strike on the school.

Sixty-nine schoolgirls are yet to be identified; remains are undergoing DNA testing. One medic described bodies without heads, hands, legs and dozens of severed limbs; some parents recognised their children only by gold bracelets. Human Rights Activists News Agency: at least 1,097 civilians killed across Iran since Saturday. A UN panel called the attack deeply disturbing; Iran's ambassador in Geneva called it unjustifiable and criminal.
