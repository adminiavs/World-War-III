## Full story

Speech to the House of Representatives, 6 April 2006, by Rep. Ron Paul (R-TX, U.S. Air Force veteran 1963-1968).

Three years after Iraq invasion, Paul said almost everybody knows there were no WMD and Saddam posed no threat. Yet plans were being laid for regime change in Iran using the same discredited arguments. He listed the pattern: propaganda about imaginary nuclear weapons, labeling opponents unpatriotic, congressional resolutions condemning Iran, demand for UN sanctions, and the assumption that all options remain on the table.

Historical context: In 1953 the CIA (with British help) overthrew democratically elected PM Mossadegh and installed the Shah, who ruled ruthlessly but protected oil interests; this led to the 1979 revolution and hostage crisis. In the 1980s the US supported Iraq's invasion of Iran, then turned on Saddam when he invaded Kuwait. Bush labeled Iran axis of evil. Each intervention produced blowback.

Paul noted removing Saddam and the Taliban inadvertently served Iranian interests (both were Iran's enemies; Iraqi Shia majority are Iran's allies). He said Iran has no nuclear weapon and the CIA confirmed they were nowhere close; IAEA Director El Baradei found no violations; Iran has an absolute right under the NPT to peaceful nuclear energy; demanding Iran prove a negative (no desire for weapons) is designed to fail. He cited Israeli historian Martin van Creveld: if Iran is not developing nuclear weapons, they are crazy, given they are surrounded by nuclear states.

Economic warnings: Iraq invasion cost $300B+, oil doubled to $60; confrontation with Iran could push oil over $120 and gas to $6. War financed by Federal Reserve money creation (inflation), not taxes; real costs hidden; inflation tax falls on poor and middle class while military-industrial complex profits. Saddam's demand for euros for oil believed to be an ulterior motive for Iraq invasion; Iran oil bourse seen as similar dollar threat. Peak oil concerns motivate control of Middle East.

Political warnings: Congress has abdicated war declaration power for 56 years; executive decides unilaterally. Patriotic fervor and fear override reason. Protectionism blamed on foreigners rather than monetary policy. Civil discord (like 1960s Vietnam-era unrest) will follow. Chinese and Japanese recycling of inflated dollars allows the US to avoid immediate pain but the day of reckoning will come.

Conclusion: Nonintervention is the moral, constitutional, and practical policy. Intervention does not work, backfires, and hurts Americans. Trade and diplomacy are superior. It is time for a change.

Ron Paul, House of Representatives, 6 April 2006.
