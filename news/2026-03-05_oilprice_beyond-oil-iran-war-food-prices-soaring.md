## Full story

In the wake of U.S. and Israeli strikes on Iranian military infrastructure, the financial press has focused on oil: tanker traffic, Brent crude, risk of triple-digit prices. But oil is not the only commodity posing serious long-term risk. Another deep vulnerability runs through natural gas and into **nitrogen fertiliser**. If commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz were significantly restricted, the impact would extend beyond fuel markets into **global food production**. The Gulf is not just a major energy exporter; it is one of the world's most important suppliers of nitrogen fertiliser, the foundation of modern agricultural yields. Nitrogen fertiliser begins with natural gas: Haber–Bosch turns methane into ammonia, then into urea and other nitrogen products. Roughly **half of global food production** depends on synthetic nitrogen; without it, crop yields would decline sharply. Globally, about 55–60 million metric tons of urea move through international seaborne trade annually; the Middle East accounts for approximately 40–50% of that volume, and nearly all of those exports must transit the Strait of Hormuz. In other words, **close to one-quarter of globally traded nitrogen fertiliser** moves through that single maritime chokepoint now threatened by war. Production behind Hormuz: Qatar (QAFCO) ~5.5–6m t urea and ammonia; Iran ~5m t urea (~10% of global trade); Saudi (SABIC) ~4–5m t; Oman and UAE several million combined; collectively more than 15m t annual export capacity inside the Gulf. Unlike oil, fertiliser markets **lack a meaningful strategic buffer**; there is no equivalent of the Strategic Petroleum Reserve. Fertiliser trade is largely just-in-time; seasonal demand aligns with planting cycles and inventories are not built for major geopolitical shocks. In the northern hemisphere, fertiliser procurement accelerates ahead of spring planting; if shipments are delayed in that window, farmers face higher costs, lower application rates or different crops. Lower nitrogen application generally means lower yields in corn, wheat and rice. The world saw a version of this in 2022 after Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Replacing 10–20m t of annual export capacity from the Gulf would not be straightforward; new ammonia plants require years to permit and build. India depends heavily on Gulf LNG for domestic urea; Brazil imports substantial Middle Eastern urea for soy and corn; the US is a major fertiliser producer but a significant portion of US urea imports transits Hormuz. Fertiliser disruptions operate on a slower timeline than oil: reduced nitrogen availability today can translate into lower crop yields months later, then tighter inventories and elevated food prices. Energy security and food security are intertwined; when a single chokepoint handles a large fraction of both oil and nitrogen fertiliser trade, the implications extend well beyond the fuel market. (OilPrice.com, 5 Mar 2026; Robert Rapier.)
