## Full story

Regional Australians facing critical fuel shortages could be waiting weeks for relief, as the government's decision to allow higher-sulphur fuel sales — an emergency measure permitting such sales for 60 days — remains unapproved with paperwork yet to be finalised. Final approval may not come until the end of this week.

Environment Minister Murray Watt appeared on Sunrise on Monday morning, defending the government's response. He stated his team is "working round the clock" to get supplies where needed, that Australia has stockpiled billions of litres of fuel lasting about five weeks if overseas supplies are cut off, and that the government is releasing up to 762 million litres from emergency reserves. "We now have 37 days reserve of petrol and 30 days reserve of diesel, which is a hell of a lot higher than it was when Barnaby was the deputy prime minister of this country," Watt said. The fuel shortage has been triggered by disruptions to supply through the Strait of Hormuz following the Iran war.

However, the reality on the ground differs. Service stations like Nathan's Happy Valley store in northwest Victoria have run out of fuel; the owner has been forced to limit sales to $50 per car.

One Nation MP Barnaby Joyce clashed with Watt, criticising government preparedness. He linked the crisis to climate policy decisions and the closure of oil refineries — Australia now has only two oil refineries compared to six when the previous government was in power. "We are really hurting now because of this." Joyce also warned the crisis could extend beyond fuel: the plastics industry faces severe shortages (polythene pipe, water tanks and other products critical to regional Australia). "This is going to smack us between the eyes. And there's been no plan."

Treasurer Jim Chalmers said the government will legislate proposed $100 million fines for price gouging by petrol retailers when parliament returns this month; ACCC has been given power to issue on-the-spot fines. An ACCC report released Friday showed evidence of retail petrol prices rising faster than wholesale prices. Petrol suppliers, retailers and automotive groups will meet on Tuesday — prompted by the ACCC — to formulate solutions to the crisis.
