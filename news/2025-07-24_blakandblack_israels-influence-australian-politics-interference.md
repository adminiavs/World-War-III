## Full story

**Abstract**

The relationship between Israel and Australia is underpinned by strong diplomatic ties, shared democratic values, and mutual strategic interests. However, the extent and nature of Israel's influence on Australian politics have been subjects of contentious debate. This paper critically examines allegations that Israel, primarily through its lobby groups and affiliated activists, has exerted an inappropriate level of influence on Australian political processes.

**Historical Context**

Australia was among the first nations to recognise Israel following its declaration of independence in 1948. The formalisation of pro-Israel lobbying in Australia can be traced back to the Zionist Federation of Australia (ZFA), founded in 1927, and the Executive Council of Australian Jewry (ECAJ), established in 1945. A significant evolution occurred with the creation of the Australia/Israel & Jewish Affairs Council (AIJAC) in 1997, modelled after AIPAC, adopting a professionalised approach to lobbying.

Historical evidence indicates that pro-Israel lobbying groups wielded considerable influence on Australian foreign policy during the 1970s and 1980s. A declassified 1974 cable from the Department of Foreign Affairs explicitly noted "considerable pressure" from Jewish community leaders on Middle East policy decisions.

From the 1980s onwards, one of the lobby's key strategies involved sponsoring politicians, journalists, and opinion leaders on all-expenses-paid trips to Israel. A 2010 Lowy Institute report estimated that over 200 federal politicians had visited Israel on these sponsored trips, far exceeding visits to other Middle Eastern countries. In 2019, controversy erupted at the University of Sydney when a pro-Israel donor threatened to withdraw funding over a lecture critical of Israel.

**Bob Carr's Revelations**

The most prominent allegations emerged through former Foreign Minister Bob Carr's memoir, Diary of a Foreign Minister (2014). Carr described the pro-Israel lobby as exerting an "extraordinary" and "unhealthy" influence on the Julia Gillard government. He recounted internal cabinet conflicts over Australia's 2012 UN vote on Palestinian observer status, where Gillard initially favoured abstaining in line with Israel's position, contrary to Carr's recommendation. Carr attributed Gillard's stance to intense pressure from Melbourne-based lobbyists, whom he characterised as "extreme right-wing."

**Financial Contributions**

The Australian Electoral Commission's 2015 analysis indicated that pro-Israel donors contributed over AUD 500,000 to major political parties between 2010 and 2014. Though modest compared to mining or union contributions, critics argue the qualitative impact is significant in shaping policy nuances.

**Media Influence**

A 2021 case study by the Centre for Advancing Journalism at the University of Melbourne described AIJAC as one of Australia's "best-funded foreign influence operations." In 2014, AIJAC successfully pressured the ABC to retract a report critical of Israeli settlements. Former ABC journalist Sophie McNeill publicly accused the lobby of employing "bullying" tactics. Since 2010, over 50 Australian media figures accepted sponsored trips to Israel.

**Post-October 2023 Developments**

Investigative reporting by The Guardian Australia in January 2025 revealed that activists associated with pro-Israel organisations such as the Australian Jewish Association (AJA) and Together with Israel had joined branches of the Australian Labor Party in late 2023. Figures like Ofir Birenbaum and Sophie Calland organised grassroots campaigns, including "Better Australia" and "Better Council Inc.," distributing materials urging voters to "put Greens last." Birenbaum was accused of covertly filming Greens candidates and posting inflammatory social media content comparing pro-Palestinian protests to Nazi rallies. These activities reportedly contributed to Green losses in Queensland council elections.

In March 2025, the Albanese government introduced stricter hate speech legislation, which critics allege was partly influenced by lobby pressure equating anti-Zionism with antisemitism. These laws have been used to arrest pro-Palestinian activists.

Israeli officials, including Ambassador Amir Maimon, publicly criticised Australia's handling of antisemitism in June 2025. Ahead of the 2025 federal election, reports emerged of pro-Israel donors funnelling funds into marginal seats. A July 2025 article in The Monthly argued that "Zionist influence" has created a political climate where it is "dangerous" for officials to condemn Israel's actions in Gaza.

**Counterarguments**

AIJAC chairman Mark Leibler dismissed Bob Carr's allegations as "a figment of his imagination," defending sponsored trips as educational opportunities. A 2022 ASPI report noted the lobby's power is moderated by bipartisan consensus and countervailing pressures from other communities. Labor MP Michael Danby described notions of lobby control as "laughable." Defenders emphasise lobbying is a democratic right exercised by many groups, and no credible evidence of illegal conduct (bribery, espionage) has surfaced.

**Analysis**

The pro-Israel lobby's effectiveness derives from its organisation, funding (estimated at AUD 10-15 million annually), and alignment with Australia's pro-Western orientation. An asymmetry exists, with pro-Israel groups enjoying relatively privileged access to political power compared to pro-Palestinian advocates. If lobbying activities deter balanced foreign policy debate or suppress criticism, they risk eroding Australia's independent foreign policy and alienating diverse communities.

**Conclusion**

The evidence indicates that Israel, primarily through its lobby organisations, exerts significant influence on Australian politics via lobbying, sponsored trips, media engagement, and grassroots campaigns. Historical episodes like the 2012 UN vote and recent reports of political party infiltration substantiate concerns. However, the absence of proof of illegal conduct and the existence of competing influences complicates simplistic conclusions. A nuanced understanding recognises the lobby's role as part of a democratic ecosystem while acknowledging the need for safeguards.
