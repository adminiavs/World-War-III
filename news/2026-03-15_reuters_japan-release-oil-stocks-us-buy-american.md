## Full story

Japan will start releasing oil from its stockpiles on Monday to cushion the impact of the U.S.–Israeli war on Iran, a reminder of the oil crisis that prompted Tokyo to create reserves half a century ago. Gasoline prices in Japan have risen as the war disrupts supplies from the Strait of Hormuz. Tokyo pledged a record 80 million barrels (about 45 days of supply for the resource-poor nation). The government has asked refiners to use the released crude; it is not clear how much will count toward the IEA's 400 million barrel coordinated release. Japan's reserve system began in 1978 after the Arab oil embargo; the G7 nation, reliant on the Middle East for around 90% of its oil, now stockpiles 254 days of consumption. It will release 15 days' worth from the private sector on Monday and a month's worth from state reserves from late March. METI Minister Ryosei Akazawa said they are also seeking supplies from the U.S., Central Asia, South America and Gulf nations that can bypass Hormuz. Japan gets around 4% of its oil from the U.S. after largely stopping Russian purchases following Moscow's 2022 invasion. U.S. EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin told Reuters the conflict is a reminder that nations along the Indo-Pacific can look to the United States for resources. Yuriy Humber (Yuri Group): reserves can stabilise supply and prices in the short term but mainly buy time and cannot fully offset a prolonged Hormuz disruption.
