## Full story

Segment opens as breaking news from the Middle East: Reza Pahlavi, crown prince of Iran, has made an announcement about leading Iran after the regime is gone. In a prepared statement he says the Iranian people have called on him to lead the transition and he has accepted; their mandate includes returning the nation and foreign relations to normality. He commits to an orderly transition, stabilizing the country, and letting Iranians determine their future through the ballot box; to not repeating past transition mistakes; to avoiding de-Baathification-style scenarios and keeping as many bureaucrats and public servants in place as possible. He asks friends in the Arab world to recognize and engage the transitional government. Iran will be rebuilt to serve the Iranian people, not for expansion; diplomacy will be based on mutual respect and shared interest, not export of ideology; Iran will reintegrate into the regional and global economy. Standing with the Iranian people is framed as a strategic investment in making the region stable, secure, and prosperous. Nicole John, a journalist in Jerusalem, is then interviewed. Asked how much longer the regime can keep fighting, she says that in the last couple of days the conversation has moved from regime capabilities to the results of the combined US-Israeli campaign, and that same day she was in a briefing with an expat Iranian working on advocacy and democracy whose sources report the coming collapse of the regime: defections, people leaving the IRGC, putting down weapons, throwing away phones so commanders assume they are dead, and disappearing to defect. That source called for the international community, including diplomats and embassy staff abroad, to encourage more defections so that a critical mass could change everything overnight. Asked who else is in the mix for a transition government (with the caveat that nothing is official and the regime is still in power), John says Reza Pahlavi appears to be the popular choice and everyone she has interviewed sees him as the natural choice. Other actors: the MEK (Mujahedin-e Khalq), described as an Islamic Marxist group that some call cult-like (e.g. requiring hijab); Maryam Rajavi leads it and had declared herself head of a transitional government a couple of days before Pahlavi. Iran is a large, diverse country (90-plus million, many minority groups); the Kurds are being discussed for a ground invasion or uprising, and it is widely said that air strikes alone cannot change the regime. She has asked what if the Kurds break away or become oppressive, and about the Azeris and other groups; many Iranians insist Iran is built on diversity, would stay unified, and that all want the same outcome, a democratic government. The host reflects that the possibility of the Islamic regime in Tehran collapsing and Iran becoming a source of peace and a Western ally would have been unthinkable not long ago. He then asks about the situation in Israel and Jerusalem: have things quieted or are people still going to bunkers? John says the day of the interview was the first in Jerusalem without a siren; there are still some missile launches from Iran but very few; Hezbollah entered two days into the war and is sending many drones into northern Israel (she wonders if they have few missiles left after the long conflict and after the fall of Assad in Syria cut the physical corridor for weapons to Hezbollah). For Israelis the focus is now more on Hezbollah because it is closer and the north is getting more sirens. The host wraps by noting it is Shabbat in Israel and signs off (Shabbat shalom; Stakes Rush or similar). YouTube 2026, video ID hPRqex321yo.
