## Full story

"Our objectives are clear," US President Donald Trump declared today, as he sought to quash Americans' understandable confusion over why, exactly, he had started a war with Iran.

He listed four goals: "destroying Iran's missile capabilities", "annihilating" its navy, ensuring the nation "can never obtain a nuclear weapon", and stopping it from "continuing to arm, fund and direct terrorist armies outside its borders".

A belated injection of clarity, there, after days of vacillation, bordering on incoherence, from Mr Trump and his administration.

Note what's conspicuously absent from his list: any mention of regime change. The same guy who not so many hours ago urged Iranians to "take over your government", saying their "freedom" was a priority and raising hopes that a democratic transformation was possible, is now suggesting he'd be happy with an echo of the situation in Venezuela, where dictator Nicolas Maduro has been replaced by his similarly corrupt deputy, Delcy Rodriguez, and the same oppressive regime remains in power.

As recently as yesterday, Mr Trump was telling Iranians the US would "be there to help" them "take back" their country. Meanwhile, he was telling American media he'd be open to negotiations with the current regime. That contradiction seems to indicate not so much deception as indecision.

### Days of confusion

As the war began, two quite basic questions plagued the Trump administration: what had prompted its decision to strike, and what was it hoping to achieve? Typically, when launching a major conflict, a president would provide clear answers to those questions immediately. He would address Americans from the Oval Office, explaining exactly why he'd decided to place their sons and daughters' lives in jeopardy. As the article notes, six American servicemembers have already been killed by Iranian strikes.

Mr Trump instead spent the weekend at his Mar-a-Lago resort and barely communicated with the public. He posted a couple of short prerecorded videos on social media and phoned some journalists, to whom he gave conflicting information. For example, Mr Trump told one reporter he had three "very good choices" lined up to replace the now-dead Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. He told another those candidates had actually been killed in the initial US strikes. He only returned to the White House on Sunday night, US time, and spoke to his country properly for the first time on Monday.

Meanwhile, there was silence from most of the Trump administration's senior officials. We didn't hear from Secretary of War Pete Hegseth or Secretary of State Marco Rubio until today, and Vice President J.D. Vance continues to lie low. Perhaps he's conscious of how silly he now looks, having insisted to voters in 2024 that Mr Trump was the anti-war candidate, and only the Democrats would get the US mired in another Middle Eastern mess.

The absence of those senior voices left a void. Had the United States launched a pre-emptive strike? Had it been pressed to act by Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu? Was there intelligence suggesting the Iranian nuclear program was making progress again? Were the Americans worried about missiles capable of reaching Europe? Was Mr Trump merely fed up with Iran's disingenuous stance in negotiations? Was he finally delivering on his promise to help pro-democracy protesters? Had he seized a window to kill Khamenei? Or was the war prompted by Iran's record of sponsoring terrorism? All plausible reasons, all floated. And what was Mr Trump's endgame? Regime change from the air alone? Or, grimly, enjoying issuing orders and watching stuff explode?

The President's ambiguous motives were not made any clearer by his ever-changing estimates of the war's duration. On day one he said it could last a week or more. Then he told The Washington Post it could be wrapped up in "two or three days". Then The Daily Mail: "always been" planned to go for a month. Today: four to five weeks, with an admission it could last "far longer". Justin Logan (Cato Institute) told The Wall Street Journal the administration looks like it is "making it up as they go along". "The present policy looks more like incoherence."

### America's strange explanation of Iran's 'threat'

Mr Rubio said there "was an imminent threat" from Iran, even though administration officials had acknowledged, in meetings with congressional staff, that no intelligence existed to suggest Iran was planning to attack America first. His rationale: the US knew Israel was planning to strike Iran, and assumed the regime would respond by attacking American bases. So he framed US actions as defensive. "There was absolutely an imminent threat, and it was that we knew that if Iran was attacked, and we believed that they would be attacked, that they would immediately come after us. And we were not going to sit there and absorb a blow." "We knew that there was going to be an Israeli action, we knew that would precipitate an attack against American forces. If we stood and waited for that attack to come first, before we hit them, we would have suffered much higher casualties." He said America's chief objectives were to "eliminate the threat of Iran's short-range ballistic missiles" and "the threat posed by their navy".

Right-wing podcaster Matt Walsh: "He's flat out telling us that we're in a war with Iran because Israel forced our hand. This is basically the worst possible thing he could have said." Michael McFaul, former US ambassador to Russia: "So Bibi gets a say as to whether the US goes to war, but the US Senate and the American people do not?" Piers Morgan: "What? So the 'pre-emptive threat' was based on prior knowledge that, presumably, Israel was going to attack Iran? This is nuts." Marjorie Taylor Greene (to Megyn Kelly): MAGA was "America First, not Israel First"; "America and Israel definitely started this war"; "What is happening to the man that I supported? The man that said, 'No more foreign wars, no more regime change'?" Senator Joaquin Castro (Democrat): "Secretary Rubio's comments indicate Israel put US forces in harm's way by insisting on attacking Iran. And the administration was complicit, joining their war instead of talking them down."

In his own remarks today, Mr Trump said the campaign was going well: Khamenei killed, huge damage to the Iranian navy.
