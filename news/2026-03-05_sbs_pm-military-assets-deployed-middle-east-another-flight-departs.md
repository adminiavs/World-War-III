## Full story

Prime Minister Anthony Albanese has confirmed "military assets" have been deployed to the Middle East as the second of four confirmed flights with stranded Australians onboard departed from Dubai. SBS News understands two military planes were deployed: a Royal Australian Air Force C-17A Globemaster heavy transport aircraft and KC-30A Multi-Role Tanker Transport, as part of the government's response to the outbreak of conflict. It follows New Zealand announcing it will send two defence force aircraft to repatriate citizens. The first commercial flight out of Dubai since war broke out between the US, Israel and Iran on Saturday touched down in Sydney late Wednesday night with more than 200 Australians on board. Albanese revealed a second flight (EK414) left Thursday morning with over 200 Australians, and two more would depart the UAE on Thursday; the situation was "dangerous and fast-moving." There are 115,000 Australians in the region, at least 24,000 in Dubai. Agencies and partners were "working around the clock"; six additional crisis centre teams deployed; "we've already deployed military assets as part of our contingency planning earlier this week." The government has not disclosed asset locations but SBS understands they have been sent near the region. The Globemaster can be configured for 134 passengers; the KC-30A up to 270 with range 14,800 km. Senator James Paterson called on the prime minister to urgently clarify the deployments: what assets, to which country and for what purpose, and when they would take Australians on board and bring them home. He referred to Afghanistan 2021 repatriation as precedent. Foreign Minister Penny Wong said on Wednesday Australia was not considering sending troops to assist in the conflict if requested by the US. On Thursday she refused to go into specifics of assets on the ground but said the government was "looking at all contingencies"; she was "deeply concerned," the conflict had spread "very rapidly" and was "much more intense." "I don't think we anticipated, or any country might have anticipated that Iran would respond in the way that it has." On whether Australian personnel were on board the US submarine that sank the Iranian warship (87 dead) in the Indian Ocean off Sri Lanka, Wong told the Senate that US submarine operations were a matter for the United States. "For operational and security reasons, we do not disclose specific information regarding Australian personnel." Greens senator David Shoebridge had asked Wong whether any Australians were on the submarine; Wong said she could not comment.
