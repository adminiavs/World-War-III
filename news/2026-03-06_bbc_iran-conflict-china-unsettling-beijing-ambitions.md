## Full story

China is not feeling the shock of war in the Middle East yet. But it is feeling the ripples. In the short-term it has enough oil supplies for several months, after which it could turn to neighbour Russia for help. But China will be calculating what this could mean long-term for its investments in the Middle East and for its ambitions.

This week thousands of Communist Party delegates are meeting in Beijing to discuss a roadmap for the world's second-largest economy as it battles low consumption, a prolonged property crisis and huge local debt. For the first time since 1991 the Chinese government has lowered its expectations of economic growth. China may have hoped to export its way out of economic trouble but has spent a year fighting a trade war with the United States and now faces upheaval in the Middle East, which supplies both its major shipping routes and much of its energy needs. The longer the war drags on the more it could hurt, especially if traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remains blocked.

Philip Shetler-Jones (Royal United Services Institute): A prolonged period of turmoil and insecurity in the Middle East will disrupt other regions of importance for China. African economies have been the beneficiary of substantial flows of Gulf capital; if the investment tide goes out that risks wider instability undermining the sustainability of China's broader and longer-term interests. Professor Kerry Brown (Kings College London): China is thinking the same as everyone else. What is the game plan? Surely the Americans didn't go into this with no game plan. Probably along with everyone else they would also be thinking they really have gone into this with no plan at all. We don't want to get dragged in but we also need to do something.

Iran was designated an ally of China by many in the West. Khamenei's last foreign trip was to Beijing in 1989. Xi visited Tehran in 2016; the two signed a 25-year strategic partnership in 2021. China promised to invest 400 billion dollars in Iran over 25 years in exchange for oil; analysts believe only a fraction has reached Iran. China imported 1.38 million barrels of crude per day from Iran in 2025 (around 12% of China's total crude imports); many barrels alleged relabelled as Malaysian. Columbia report: more than 46 million barrels of Iranian oil in floating storage in Asia and bonded storage in Chinese ports Dalian and Zhoushan. There have been allegations of arms sales and Chinese support for Iran's ballistic missile programme; rights groups alleged Chinese facial recognition and surveillance tech fuelled Iran's crackdowns. Prof Brown: There's no real ideological or cultural reason why China would get on with Iran. China's divide and rule was sometimes well-served by Iran being a constant irritant to the US. That's a really fragile basis for a relationship. China does not sign mutual defence treaties and will not come rushing to an ally's aid. Beijing issued a muted condemnation and called for a ceasefire. Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi: unacceptable for the US and Israel to launch attacks against Iran, still less to blatantly assassinate a leader of a sovereign country and instigate regime change. Washington's actions in Venezuela in January and now in Iran have highlighted the limits of those countries' partnerships with China; on both occasions Beijing has been left on the sidelines. Shetler-Jones: In terms of military balance the US is demonstrating what being a superpower really means; Beijing is not equipped to protect its friends against this kind of action even if it wanted to. Xi will continue positioning himself as a stable and predictable global leader in contrast to Donald Trump. Professor Steve Tsang (SOAS): China's argument will be that Trump has demonstrated western hypocrisy; disruptions to energy and air travel will have far greater ramifications economically in the Global South than in the West; some countries will have food shortage in a few months. Wang Yi has spoken to counterparts in Oman and France; China will send a special envoy to the Middle East. Trump is due to visit China later this month; China is treading carefully; officials from both sides to meet to discuss the trip. China may see the visit as a chance to look for cues on how Trump may respond to Taiwan. BBC, 6 Mar 2026. Laura Bicker.
