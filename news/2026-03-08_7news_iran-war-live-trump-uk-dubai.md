## Australian defence could offer defensive support, but rules out ground troops in Iran

The Australian government is reviewing requests for assistance from Gulf nations facing Iranian strikes, but will not commit troops to any ground operations in Iran, Foreign Minister Penny Wong confirmed.

Wong told ABC’s Insiders on Sunday that Australia had been asked for help following Iran’s missile and drone attacks on non-participating countries.

Penny Wong calls on Australians to register for DFAT updates as 115,000 caught in escalating Middle East crisis Credit: Sunrise

“You would anticipate, as a consequence, that we have been asked for assistance, and we will work through that carefully. And of course, we are,” she said.

She said any potential support would focus on defensive measures rather than offensive action.

“We are not participating in offensive action against Iran,” Wong said, adding that Australia will not deploy Defence Force personnel into the conflict.

“This is not Iraq, and we are not the Howard government; we are not asking Australians to accept Australian men and women being deployed into a ground war.”

The remarks mark a shift from last week, when the government maintained that while Australia supported US-led operations, it would not be participating directly.

## Trump refuses to rule out boots on the ground

President Donald Trump said US troops could “possibly” be sent on the ground in Iran, as the war continues, but there would have to be a “very good reason.”

“I don’t even want to talk about it now. I don’t think it’s an appropriate question; you know I’m not going to answer it. Could there be? Possibly, for very good reason, have to be very good reason,” he told reporters aboard Air Force One.

“I would say if we ever did that they would be so decimated that they wouldn’t be able to fight at the ground level”.

When pressed on whether he would consider using ground troops to secure enriched uranium at Iran’s nuclear sites, Trump also said it’s a possibility, but not right now.

“We’ll find out about that. We haven’t talked about it. It was a total obliteration. They haven’t been able to get to it. And at some point, maybe we will. It would be a great thing, but right now we’re just decimating them, but we haven’t gone after it,” Trump said.

“But something we could do later on. We wouldn’t do it now,” he added.

Part of the Trump administration’s justification for the war was to prevent Iran from obtaining a nuclear weapon.

## Trump blames Iran for school strike, despite preliminary evidence suggesting U.S. involvement

Trump has told reporters that the US is not responsible for the [bombing that destroyed an elementary school in southern Iran](), which left at least 168 dead.

The president’s comments contradict preliminary findings that a U.S. munition may have been involved in the strike, sources have told NBC News.

“Did the United States bomb a girl’s elementary school in southern Iran on the first day of the war and kill 175 people?” a reporter asked Trump during a press gaggle on board Air Force One.

“Based on what I’ve seen, that was done by Iran,” the US president responded.

Asked if that was true, Secretary of Defence Pete Hegseth told reporters that they are investigating, “but the only side that targets civilians is Iran.”

Airstrikes hit an [elementary school in the town of Minab]() on the first day that the U.S. and Israel began strikes in the country.

Dozens of children are believed to have been killed on an airstrike on a school in south Iran. Credit: X

Trump administration officials told members of Congress in a closed-door meeting that the U.S. was operating in the area where the school, Shajareh Tayyebeh, was struck, according to two U.S. officials. The administration officials also said that Israel was not responsible for the strike on the school.

An investigation into the bombing is ongoing, but preliminary findings from the Trump administration indicate it is increasingly likely a U.S. munition was responsible for the strike, a U.S. official and a person familiar with the investigation told [NBC News.]()

## Australian living in Dubai says life remains ‘normal’ despite Middle East tensions

An Australian woman living in Dubai says she still feels safe despite escalating tensions in the Middle East, describing daily life in the emirate as “somehow normal.”

### Australian expatriate Gael Balian provides a first-hand account of daily life in Dubai amid escalating Middle East tensions.

(Source: 7NEWS)

Australian expatriate Gael Balian provides a first-hand account of daily life in Dubai amid escalating Middle East tensions.

Gael Balian, who splits her time between Dubai, Lebanon, and Sydney, told Sunrise on Sunday that while there is some stress, the situation on the ground is very different from what’s portrayed on social media.

“Everything’s functioning normally. Yes, we do feel a bit of stress, but we’re very fortunate to feel the safety of this country,” Balian said.

The Australian, who has Lebanese heritage, said living with regional conflict has become part of life in the Middle East.

Supplies and services continue as usual, and she urged people not to believe everything they see online, saying she felt no immediate danger.

“As an Australian, I do have the chance to leave the country, go back to Australia, as it’s my second home as well. But personally, I do feel safe in Dubai. For me, there’s no need to get alarmed in that way,” she said.

Balian has chosen to remain in Dubai, continuing her daily routine and business operations despite the broader regional unrest.
